# Waltz for Debby on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Waltz for Debby* documents the Bill Evans Trio – Evans (piano), Scott LaFaro (bass), Paul Motian (drums) – live at the Village Vanguard in New York City on June 25, 1961, and was released February 22, 1962 as Riverside RLP 399 (mono) / RLP 9399 (stereo). Orrin Keepnews produced; Dave Jones engineered. This is the companion volume to *Sunday at the Village Vanguard*, drawn from the same day's five sets; ten days after the recording, LaFaro was killed in a car accident at age 25, making this the trio's final studio or live document together.

Riverside recorded to twin-track by 1961, so the stereo release is a genuine capture rather than a later derivative – collectors and mastering engineers alike treat both mono and stereo as legitimate, with stereo generally preferred for the way it separates LaFaro's bass and Motian's brushwork across the soundstage. The reissue history here is unusually well-documented and genuinely contested: three different mastering engineers across four decades have each drawn different things out of the same tapes, with no consensus on which is "best."

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Riverside stereo, RLP 9399](https://www.discogs.com/release/13975220-Bill-Evans-Trio-With-Scott-LaFaro-Paul-Motian-Waltz-For-Debby) – deep groove, "Bill Grauer Productions" label.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Analogue Productions UHQR (AUHQR 0018-45, 2024)](https://www.discogs.com/release/31288886-Bill-Evans-Trio-Waltz-For-Debby) – Kevin Gray, Clarity Vinyl, 45rpm; widely (though not universally) rated the finest version yet.

**Best-value modern:** [Craft Recordings Original Jazz Classics (CR00617, 2023)](https://www.discogs.com/release/27345327-Bill-Evans-Trio-Waltz-For-Debby) – Kevin Gray, RTI, single 180g LP.

## Recording history and tape provenance

By mid-1961, the Bill Evans Trio with LaFaro and Motian had developed a genuinely democratic interplay unusual for the era – rather than a piano lead backed by rhythm, all three musicians shaped the music with near-equal weight, LaFaro in particular pioneering a melodic, contrapuntal bass style that departed sharply from simple timekeeping. The trio played five sets at the Village Vanguard on June 25, 1961 – two matinee, three evening – recorded in full by engineer Dave Jones. Riverside had originally planned only one album from the date; the material was so strong that producer Orrin Keepnews and Evans recognized it could support two full releases.

That plan was upended ten days later when LaFaro died in a car accident in upstate New York. Keepnews and Evans split the recordings accordingly: *Sunday at the Village Vanguard*, rushed out first (by September 1961) and subtitled "Featuring Scott LaFaro," was built around LaFaro's finest solos, bookended by two of his own compositions. *Waltz for Debby*, arriving five months later, presents a more typical cross-section of the trio's working book – the title track (a solo-piano piece Evans had already recorded on his 1956 debut, here reworked for trio and named for his young niece), "My Foolish Heart," "Detour Ahead," "My Romance," "Some Other Time," and a searching reading of Miles Davis's "Milestones." The loss of LaFaro hit Evans hard enough that he didn't lead another trio recording for over a year, eventually returning with Motian and new bassist Chuck Israels.

Both *Sunday at the Village Vanguard* and *Waltz for Debby* have been awarded a crown in every edition of the Penguin Guide to Jazz since 1992, and both appear in *1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die*. The complete day's recordings – nearly everything captured across all five sets – were reissued in 2005 as the 3-CD box *The Complete Village Vanguard Recordings, 1961*. The master tapes remain with Concord (via the Fantasy/Riverside catalog administered through Craft Recordings), and are reported to be in good condition despite their age.

## Stereo note

Riverside was recording to genuine twin-track tape by 1961, and this session's stereo release (RLP 9399) is a real, native stereo capture – not a later rechanneled derivative. Some UK and European pressings from this era are believed to derive from a mono fold-down rather than the true stereo master, so buyers outside the US chasing an authentic stereo image should confirm the pressing's tape lineage where possible. Bill's piano sits in the right channel, with LaFaro's bass and Motian's drums in the left – a hard-panned arrangement typical of Riverside's early-1960s stereo mixes.

## Original Riverside pressings (1962)

**Mono: RLP 399** ([Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/3415732-Bill-Evans-Trio-With-Scott-LaFaro-Paul-Motian-Waltz-For-Debby)) / **Stereo: RLP 9399** ([Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/13975220-Bill-Evans-Trio-With-Scott-LaFaro-Paul-Motian-Waltz-For-Debby)), both on "Bill Grauer Productions" deep-groove labels. A narrower deep-groove/bluish-cover mono variant also circulates and is discussed by collectors as possibly an earlier or distinct pressing run, though its exact place in the timeline isn't firmly established. **NM originals: $150–$400 mono, $200–$500 stereo**, with exceptional promo copies (white-label mono) reaching considerably higher.

### Later Riverside/OJC pressings

Riverside continued pressing RLP 9399 through the mid-1960s, followed by the Fantasy-era Original Jazz Classics reissue (**OJC-210**, from 1985) using the same tapes. **$20–$50.**

## Japanese pressings

Riverside's Japanese licensees issued this title repeatedly: **SR-7015** (1962), **MW 2004** (1970, gatefold), and **SMJ-6118 / SRS-6060** (1975, Riverside/Milestone Japan) – the latter specifically praised by owners for "outstanding" pressing quality: "very quiet, highly detailed, and truly top-tier." [Discogs (SMJ-6118)](https://www.discogs.com/release/3866394-Bill-Evans-Trio-Waltz-For-Debby) **$40–$90 with obi.**

## The reissue hierarchy

This title has one of the most closely-debated reissue histories in the whole Riverside catalog – four different audiophile pressings across four decades, no consensus winner.

### Analogue Productions HQ-180 (1992) – Doug Sax's benchmark

**Format: 180g LP, stereo.** **Mastered by Doug Sax at The Mastering Lab, all-analog.** Long considered the standard-bearer specifically for the "thwack and sizzle" of LaFaro's bass and Motian's drums – multiple reviewers (including Tracking Angle's Michael Fremer, comparing all four pressings directly in 2023) still rate it at or near the top for sheer visceral excitement, even against much newer competition. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/2415967-Bill-Evans-Trio-With-Scott-LaFaro-Paul-Motian-Waltz-For-Debby) **$100–$270 secondary.**

### Analogue Productions 45rpm box (1997/2010) – Kevin Gray & Steve Hoffman

Part of Analogue Productions' 11-album, 22-LP *Bill Evans: The Complete Riverside Recordings* box set, mastered by Kevin Gray and Steve Hoffman. Per direct comparison, this pressing surpasses the Sax version for the piano's glow and overall ambience, but several listeners find it falls short of Sax's bass-and-drum excitement – a genuine, well-documented trade-off rather than a straightforward upgrade. Only available as part of the full box set; individual volumes are not sold separately. **Box set: $250–$500+ secondary when complete.**

### Craft Recordings Original Jazz Classics (2023) – the modern standard-bearer

**Cat. CR00617** | Format: 180g LP, stereo, tip-on jacket, part of the relaunched Original Jazz Classics series. **All-analog mastering from the original tapes by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio, pressed at RTI.** Tracking Angle's Fremer rated this "the best-sounding of all the pressings" on first release, praising warmer, clearer bass resonance than the Sax pressing, though noting the comparison is close enough that reasonable listeners disagree – some later shootout participants still preferred Sax's more forward excitement.

[One detailed direct A/B against the AP box set edition](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M6Jx2QOgHFg) found a genuine, specific character tradeoff: the Craft OJC is more tonally balanced overall – each instrument sits at a similar level, giving a cohesive, unified feel – but the AP box's bass has noticeably more sustain and finger detail, the piano is louder and more separated from the rest of the ensemble, and the recording feels fuller and more "in the room." The OJC's cymbals and brushwork, by contrast, run hotter and brighter, occasionally tipping toward tinny – a character some listeners attribute to Kevin Gray's mastering signature showing up similarly across other labels (Blue Note, Craft) he's worked on. The OJC is also cut at a noticeably lower level than the AP, requiring more amplifier gain to reach the same perceived loudness. One artifact worth knowing about in advance, so it isn't mistaken for a pressing defect: a brief, audible "blip" roughly ten seconds into "My Foolish Heart," where the recording briefly gets louder (as if a microphone was adjusted mid-take), is present on this pressing, the AP box, and the original mono alike – confirming it's baked into the 1961 tape itself, not specific to any single edition. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/27345327-Bill-Evans-Trio-Waltz-For-Debby) **$35–$45 new.**

### Analogue Productions UHQR (2024) – the current reference

**Cat. AUHQR 0018-45** | Format: 2×200g Clarity Vinyl, 45rpm, gatefold box set, limited to 5,000 copies. **Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio from the original tapes, pressed at QRP.** Includes an 8-page booklet with liner notes by Bob Blumenthal. Several owners who've directly compared this to the 2023 Craft OJC single LP (same mastering engineer, different cut and format) report the UHQR pulls ahead on air and dynamic range, though a few note the Clarity Vinyl can retain more surface noise than the OJC's standard black vinyl even after cleaning. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/31288886-Bill-Evans-Trio-Waltz-For-Debby) **$150–$300 new/secondary.**

## Pressings to avoid

Numerous colored-vinyl and budget European reissues circulate (yellow, purple, transparent pink pressings on small labels), generally sourced digitally rather than from analog tape. Reviews of these are mixed but generally more positive than the typical public-domain reissue horror story – several owners report perfectly listenable, quiet pressings at low prices. These are reasonable casual-listening options but shouldn't be mistaken for AAA analog pressings; check the specific label and source before paying a premium.
