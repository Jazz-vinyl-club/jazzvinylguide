# Birth of the Cool on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Birth of the Cool* compiles recordings made in three sessions: **January 21, 1949** (four tracks), **April 22, 1949** (four tracks), and **March 9, 1950** (four tracks, including one vocal by Kenny Hagood), all recorded in New York City. The assembled album was released in **February/March 1957** on Capitol Records as T-762 (mono). The nonet's personnel shifted across sessions; constant members were Miles Davis (trumpet), Lee Konitz (alto saxophone), Gerry Mulligan (baritone saxophone), and Bill Barber (tuba). Key participants included J.J. Johnson and Kai Winding (trombones), John Lewis and Al Haig (piano), Gunther Schuller and Junior Collins (French horn), Max Roach and Kenny Clarke (drums), and arrangers Gil Evans, Gerry Mulligan, John Lewis, and John Carisi. The recordings are foundational to cool jazz and introduced French horn and tuba to the standard jazz ensemble vocabulary.

The music was originally issued as individual 78rpm singles between 1949 and 1950; the 1957 Capitol LP compilation — not released under the title "Birth of the Cool" until Capitol applied it retrospectively — is the canonical presentation. The mono is the only format: these 1949–50 recordings predated stereo; any "stereo" copy is electronic rechanneling and should be avoided. Original 1957 Capitol turquoise-label pressings are the collector's target. The Blue Note Tone Poet reissue (2026) is the most significant modern pressing.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Capitol T-762 mono, turquoise label, "Long Playing" only (1st press)](https://www.discogs.com/release/5456857-Miles-Davis-Birth-Of-The-Cool) – Scranton pressing, stamped runouts.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Blue Note Tone Poet 180g mono (2026)](https://www.discogs.com/master/62308-Miles-Davis-Birth-Of-The-Cool) – Kevin Gray, AAA from original phono reel tapes, RTI, gatefold; Davis centennial edition.

**Best-value modern:** Also the Tone Poet — no competing budget audiophile option currently exists that sources from original analogue tapes.


## Recording history and tape provenance

The nonet grew out of informal musical discussions at arranger Gil Evans's 55th Street apartment in Manhattan, a gathering point for musicians interested in a lighter, more harmonically complex alternative to bebop. The group played only two public engagements — a residency at the Royal Roost in September 1948 and a Clique Club residency in 1949 — before disbanding. Capitol Records had Davis under contract for 78rpm sides and the three studio sessions were the result.

**The 1957 compilation:** Capitol released the 12" LP in 1957 as T-762, gathering eleven of the twelve recorded tracks (omitting "Darn That Dream," the vocal number, which appeared only on the 1972 complete edition as M-11026). The title "Birth of the Cool" was Capitol's own marketing framing applied at the 1957 release; the musicians did not use it. The 1972 "Complete Birth of the Cool" (M-11026) added "Darn That Dream" and has since become the standard template for subsequent releases.

**Recording technology:** The 1949–50 sessions predate modern studio practice. The recordings were made mono to acetate disc in standard 1940s-era New York studio conditions. The Capitol tape archive holds original three-track and mono tape transfers from these sessions; the condition of the original tapes has been described as remarkably good for recordings of this age.

**Label chronology:** The first pressing (T-762) uses Capitol's **turquoise label** with the Capitol dome logo, and the text **"Long Playing"** only at the base (later copies added "High Fidelity"). The rim text on first pressings reads "Manufactured By Capitol Records Inc • Hollywood, California • U.S.A." — a different rim text from a second turquoise pressing variant. Runouts are machine-stamped (not etched). The Scranton pressing plant is identified by the anvil symbol in the runout.

## Original Capitol pressings

**Catalogue: T-762** (mono, 12" LP, 1957)

### First pressing – turquoise label, "Long Playing" only

The earliest identified copies:
- **Turquoise label** with Capitol dome logo at top
- **"Long Playing"** text only at the bottom (second pressing adds "High Fidelity")
- Rim text: **"Manufactured By Capitol Records Inc • Hollywood, California • U.S.A."**
- Runouts: **machine-stamped** (no handwritten etchings); Scranton anvil symbol
- Matrix: **T1-762 D2#2** (Side A) / **T2-762 D1** (Side B)
- Laminated sleeve

**NM first-press originals: $200–$600. VG+: $80–$250.**

### Second pressing – "Long Playing High Fidelity" label

The second pressing (still turquoise, still 1957–58) adds "High Fidelity" to the base text. Sonically equivalent. More commonly found. **$60–$200 VG+.**

### Later Capitol pressings (orange "swirl" label, green label, etc.)

Capitol's label design changed multiple times through the 1960s and 1970s. Later pressings are identifiable by the distinctive orange-and-yellow "swirl" Capitol logo adopted in the mid-1960s. These are reissues from later metalwork and are not generally preferred over originals, though some vintage Capitol enthusiasts find early 1960s copies competitive. **$15–$60.**

## The reissue hierarchy

### Blue Note Tone Poet 180g mono (2026) – the definitive modern pressing

**Cat. B0046xxxx** (confirmed Tone Poet series, exact catalogue pending) | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓, mono, gatefold tip-on jacket with session photography by William "PoPsie" Randolph. **Produced by Joe Harley. Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio, AAA, from the original Capitol analogue phono reel master tapes. Pressed at RTI.** Released to coincide with Miles Davis's centennial year. Harley has described the original tapes as being in excellent condition and the mastering process as revelatory. Headphonesty (2026): "On this pressing, the nonet's space and tone should be preserved beautifully." This is the first truly audiophile-grade AAA reissue of this title from original sources. **$50–$70 new.**

### Various 1970s–2010s reissues – none recommended

The history of *Birth of the Cool* reissues is largely disappointing. A 2019 reissue was noted by collectors as substandard (Tracking Angle: "no advice on Birth Of The Cool; the 2019 reissue supposedly sucks"). Various Music On Vinyl, Simply Vinyl, and generic LP reissues exist but source from digital transfers or copy tapes. None are recommended when the Tone Poet is available.

## Pressings to avoid

**Any "stereo" version:** The 1949–50 recordings are inherently mono. Any stereo copy is electronically rechanneled and degrades the original recording. Avoid unconditionally.

**2019 Capitol/UMe reissue:** Widely considered below standard; sourced from a digital master. Not recommended.

**Generic European reissues (EMI, CBS, etc.):** Various European licensed pressings were produced from the 1960s onward from copy tapes. Not recommended over an original Capitol.

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $70:** The [**Blue Note Tone Poet 180g mono (2026)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/37415985) — the only currently recommended modern pressing, and for most listeners the best available version of this music on vinyl.

**$80–$250:** A clean [**second-press Capitol T-762**](https://www.discogs.com/release/4609609) ("Long Playing High Fidelity" label) in VG+ condition — the budget audiophile's original.

**$200–$600:** A [**first-press Capitol T-762**](https://www.discogs.com/release/5456857) (turquoise, "Long Playing" only) in honest VG+ or better — the collector's grail.
