# Time Out on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

*The Dave Brubeck Quartet — Columbia CL 1397 / CS 8192 (1959)*

## Summary

*Time Out* was recorded at Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York City across three sessions in June, July, and August 1959 — the same converted Presbyterian church, the same engineer (Fred Plaut), and overlapping with the sessions for *Kind of Blue* and *Mingus Ah Um*. It is the first jazz album to sell one million copies, certified double platinum by the RIAA, and the only jazz album to produce a mainstream pop hit (Paul Desmond's "Take Five" at #25 on the Billboard Hot 100). Columbia's label executives initially refused to release it; Brubeck convinced them by first recording the conventional *Gone with the Wind*.

The album's sonic character is defined by the 30th Street Studio's vast acoustic: Brubeck's piano has an earthy resonance, Desmond's alto is feathered but focused, Joe Morello's drums have ideal weight and shimmer, and Eugene Wright's bass – the most commonly criticised element of lesser pressings – is either present and grounded or frustratingly buried depending on the cut. The stereo image places Brubeck slightly left, Desmond slightly right, rhythm section centred – less artificially wide than some Columbia jazz of the period, but still with a notable left-right spread. The mono collapses this into a more unified presentation that many seasoned collectors prefer. *Time Out* is also one of the most bootlegged jazz titles in the EU budget market – DOL, Waxtime, Vinyl Passion and similar grey-market pressings should be avoided.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Six Eye stereo CS 8192](https://www.discogs.com/master/34081-The-Dave-Brubeck-Quartet-Time-Out) – 1A/1A stampers, Fred Plaut lacquer from 30th Street; rich midrange, natural air; $200–$500 NM.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Analogue Productions APJ 8192-45 45rpm 2LP](https://www.discogs.com/release/3791080-The-Dave-Brubeck-Quartet-Time-Out) – Bernie Grundman, AAA, 200g QRP; the current benchmark for piano and bass reproduction.

**Best-value modern:** [Analogue Productions AAPJ 8192 33rpm 180g](https://www.discogs.com/release/7632519-The-Dave-Brubeck-Quartet-Time-Out) – same Bernie Grundman AAA mastering, single LP, gatefold, QRP; widely available new.


## Recording history and tape provenance

*Time Out* was recorded at **Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York City** across three sessions: **June 25, 1959** (tracks B1–B3: "Three to Get Ready," "Kathy's Waltz," "Everybody's Jumpin'"); **July 1, 1959** (tracks A2–A3: "Strange Meadow Lark," "Take Five"); and **August 18, 1959** (tracks A1 and B4: "Blue Rondo à la Turk," "Pick Up Sticks"). Produced by **Teo Macero**, engineered by **Fred Plaut**. Personnel: Dave Brubeck (piano), Paul Desmond (alto saxophone), Eugene Wright (bass), Joe Morello (drums).

The recording took place in the same 100-foot ceiling converted church that simultaneously hosted sessions for *Kind of Blue* (April and August 1959) and *Mingus Ah Um* (May 1959). Fred Plaut's engineering approach for all three was consistent – wide-spaced microphone placement capturing the room's natural reverb, Ampex tape at 30 ips, Scully/Westrex cutting lathes. The sonic "family resemblance" between these three albums is not coincidental; it is the signature of a specific studio, engineer, and chain.

Columbia's label executives were deeply reluctant to release *Time Out*. In Brubeck's own account, label president Goddard Lieberson agreed to release the album only if Brubeck first recorded *Gone with the Wind*, a conventional album of American standards – an insurance policy against commercial failure. *Time Out* was completed by August 1959 but held back until **December 1959** for release. The delayed release gave the sessions for *Kind of Blue* (released August 1959) a head start in the market, though both became defining documents of the year.

Paul Desmond composed "Take Five" – the only track on the album not written by Brubeck – after Brubeck challenged him to write a melody over Joe Morello's 5/4 drum pattern. Brubeck contributed all other compositions, each exploring a different non-standard time signature: "Blue Rondo à la Turk" in 9/8 (inspired by a Turkish street musician Brubeck heard on a State Department tour), "Strange Meadow Lark" in 4/4 with asymmetric phrasing, "Three to Get Ready" alternating 3/4 and 4/4, "Kathy's Waltz" in 4/4 (the title is ironic), "Everybody's Jumpin'" in 6/4, "Pick Up Sticks" in 6/4.

**The cover art** is a Neil Fujita abstract painting – the same designer responsible for *Kind of Blue* and *Mingus Ah Um*. Columbia's sales executives were opposed to an abstract painting as cover art; Brubeck insisted and prevailed.

## The Columbia Six Eye label: identification

Like *Kind of Blue* and *Mingus Ah Um*, *Time Out* was issued on Columbia's Six Eye label – so named for the six circles arranged symmetrically around the spindle hole. The Six Eye ran from approximately 1955 to 1962, replaced by the Two Eye design when Columbia introduced the CBS branding.

**Original catalogue numbers:**
- **Mono: CL 1397** – matrix codes XLP 47912 (Side A) / XLP 47913 (Side B)
- **Stereo: CS 8192** – matrix codes XSM 47914 (Side A) / XSM 47915 (Side B)

Both mastered on Scully lathes with Westrex cutterheads by Fred Plaut.

**Six Eye label characteristics:** Black label with red lettering. Six eyes (small circles) arranged around the spindle hole. "COLUMBIA" in arc at top. "GUARANTEED HIGH FIDELITY" (mono) or "360 SOUND STEREO" (stereo) printed on the label. No barcode.

**First pressing markers:** Machine-stamped matrix numbers in the deadwax – the critical identifier. The earliest stereo copies carry **1A/1A stampers** (Side A: XSM 47914-1A, Side B: XSM 47915-1A). These are the most sought-after copies.

**The CBS overprint problem:** Around 1961, Columbia introduced the CBS corporate name, leading to Six Eye labels with "CBS" printed at the 12 o'clock position above the spindle hole. [LJC's Columbia label guide](https://londonjazzcollector.wordpress.com/record-labels-guide/columbia-records/columbia-us-labels/) is clear on this: "The CBS Six-Eyes are inferior sonically to the original editions, in the case of my copy of Brubeck's Time Out below, significantly so." A CBS-overprinted Six Eye may look like a first press but sounds noticeably worse – stodgy bass, blurred piano, cymbals diminished. The CBS overprint at 12 o'clock is the tell. **A genuine first-press Six Eye has no CBS at all.**

**The mono situation:** The mono CL 1397 is **not** a fold-down of the stereo master – it is a **separate mono mix** of the original three-track session, cut in mono from the source tapes. This is an important distinction from *Kind of Blue* and makes the mono a genuinely different listening experience rather than a collapsed stereo. The mono is sometimes preferred by collectors for its more coherent ensemble presentation – instruments less spread, more "in the room" together.

**Market values (NM condition):**
- NM stereo CS 8192 Six Eye, 1A/1A stampers, no CBS: **$200–$500**
- NM stereo CS 8192 Six Eye, later stampers, no CBS: **$100–$300**
- NM stereo CS 8192 CBS Six Eye: **$50–$120** (sonically inferior; discount accordingly)
- NM mono CL 1397 Six Eye, no CBS: **$150–$350**

**The Dutch Fontana pressing:** [LJC documented a head-to-head comparison](https://londonjazzcollector.wordpress.com/2013/03/15/dave-brubeck-time-out-1959-fontana/) between a Dutch Fontana 682 065 TL (Philips Holland pressing, 1959) and a non-CBS US Six Eye and concluded: "CBS 6-eye shrivels before the Fontana." The Dutch pressing, made at the Philips plant in the Netherlands, carries the same matrix code suffix "670" as other fine Dutch pressings of this era. This is an undervalued alternative to the US original.

## Mono vs. stereo

Unlike *Kind of Blue* or *A Love Supreme*, where the mono is a fold-down, the mono CL 1397 of *Time Out* is a discrete mono mix from the three-track session tapes. The sonic difference is genuine – the mono places the quartet more tightly together, with Brubeck's piano more centred and Desmond's alto less artificially spread. The stereo's left-right separation – piano left, Desmond right – is notably wide for a quartet record and can feel spatially artificial on headphones or very wide stereo setups.

[Vinylreviews.com on the original stereo](https://vinylreviews.com/album/time-out/): "Like my original white-label stereo pressing, the left-right stereo separation feels a bit artificial." The same reviewer finds this less pronounced on the AP 45rpm, suggesting the wider groove cut at 45rpm resolves more of the inter-channel information.

The community is divided. The stereo has been the default for all major reissues; the mono is collected primarily on original pressings. No modern audiophile reissue has issued the mono specifically.

## The reissue hierarchy

### Classic Records 45rpm 4LP (1995 / 2000 / 2008) — the first great reissues

Classic Records issued *Time Out* in three distinct 45rpm editions, all on 4×single-sided LPs (one track per side at 45rpm):

**1995 edition (180g):** Bernie Grundman mastering from the original tapes. Pressed by Classic Records. The pioneering audiophile reissue — proof that the recording had more to offer than any standard LP had delivered. Market: **$100–$250** for a clean 4LP set.

**2000 edition (200g Quiex SV-P):** Same Grundman mastering, repressed on Classic Records' proprietary 200g Quiex SV-P vinyl compound. Wider groove, quieter surface than 180g. Generally considered the best of the three Classic editions. Market: **$150–$300**.

**2008 edition (Clarity SV-P II clear vinyl):** Same mastering again, on Classic Records' final vinyl compound iteration. Some collectors find the clarity vinyl more analytically neutral; others prefer the Quiex warmth. Market: **$150–$350** depending on condition.

All three Classic editions use single-sided pressings with the track listing across four discs. The packaging is spartan by modern standards — the original Classic was issued without a gatefold, leading some to describe it as "four records in white sleeves."

### Analogue Productions APJ 8192-45 45rpm 2LP (2012) — the current audiophile benchmark

**Cat. APJ 8192-45** | UPC **753088819278** | Format: 2×200g 45rpm, Stoughton tip-on gatefold with session photos, QRP pressing. **Mastered by Bernie Grundman, AAA, from the original analog master tapes.** The first time *Time Out* was issued in a gatefold jacket.

Matrix etching: "CS 8192-A1-45 APJ-8192-45 A Bernie Grundman" on all four sides.

[Wayne Garcia (*The Absolute Sound*, January 2013)](https://www.theabsolutesound.com): "The results speak for themselves: the platters from QRP are in my experience the most consistently flat and quiet being pressed today... at least for the foreseeable here and now, Analogue Productions' newly mastered 45 RPM (Brubeck) is the edition to own. You'll hear it right from the familiar piano intro to 'Blue Rondo à la Turk,' where Brubeck's playing seems richer, more lyrical, more rhythmically alive."

[Vinylreviews.com](https://vinylreviews.com/album/time-out/) on direct comparison with the original Six Eye: "Of all the choices, this reissue most authoritatively nails the piano and bass sound. At times with an original, you have to remind yourself the bassist is there. The piano's left-hand part is also much better fleshed out on the AP reissue than elsewhere."

Multiple Discogs owners confirm the wide dynamic range and detailed percussion: "The recording of the drum set is amazing – captures all the grungy sounds you get if you were next to a real set – reverb on toms, vibration of snare wires, overtones of cymbals." One notable criticism: some owners report piano distortion on "Kathy's Waltz" specifically, though this appears to be a cartridge/alignment issue on high-output passages rather than a universal mastering artefact.

Note on vinyl weight: while originally issued on 200g, pressings since approximately 2021–2022 have been on 180g. Sonically equivalent per community reports. **Still in print via Acoustic Sounds; ~$80–$100 new.**

### Analogue Productions AAPJ 8192 33rpm 180g (2015) — the single-disc option

**Cat. AAPJ 8192** | UPC **753088819216** | Format: single 180g LP at 33⅓, Stoughton tip-on gatefold with same session photos as 45rpm edition. **Mastered by Bernie Grundman, AAA**, from the same original tapes. Same QRP pressing.

Note: the cover of this edition erroneously states "RECORDING IS DESIGNED FOR USE ON 45 RPM STEREOPHONIC REPRODUCERS" — a copy-paste error from the 45rpm cover art; the vinyl is indeed 33⅓. This causes frequent Discogs confusion; check the label, not the cover.

The 33rpm Grundman mastering is essentially the same mastering decision applied to a single-disc format. The 45rpm advantage in groove width translates to marginally better transient detail and bass definition on the 45; the 33rpm is more convenient and considerably cheaper. [Waxhaw Wax](https://waxhawwax.com/dave-brubeck-quartet-time-out/): "Analogue Productions 45 RPM (2012): Pressed at QRP, quieter vinyl and rounder tone — many prefer it for balance." The same verdict applies to the 33rpm at slightly reduced resolution. **Still in print; ~$35–$45 new.**

## The cover art story: Neil Fujita and the reluctant label

The abstract painting on *Time Out*'s cover is by **S. Neil Fujita** — the same designer responsible for the covers of *Kind of Blue* and *Mingus Ah Um*, all released in 1959. The geometric abstraction for *Time Out* uses overlapping shapes in red, blue, green, and yellow against a cream ground. Columbia's sales executives initially refused this cover, insisting jazz albums needed a photograph of the artist. Brubeck held firm. The cover became one of the most recognised in jazz history, and Fujita's three Columbia covers of 1959 represent a peak moment in jazz graphic design.

Later pressings of *Time Out* added text to the cover that does not appear on first-year copies: **"Featuring Take Five, Blue Rondo à la Turk"** and an oval circle around the catalogue number. The absence of this text, plus the presence of the original clean cover with no superimposed typography, is a first-year pressing marker for the stereo CS 8192.

## What makes this recording particularly demanding on pressings

**Eugene Wright's bass** is the defining challenge. The 30th Street Studio's acoustic space gave Wright's bass a large natural resonance – it can sound grounded and physical on the best pressings, or thin and inaudible on lesser ones. Vinylreviews.com: "At times with an original, you have to remind yourself the bassist is there." The AP 45rpm specifically addresses this with Grundman's mastering. First-press Six Eye collectors similarly note that the bass is dramatically better-defined on a clean 1A/1A stamper than on later pressings.

**Joe Morello's drums** are a showpiece of the recording and a reference track for audiophile systems across multiple generations. The ring of the ride cymbal on "Take Five," the crack of the snare on "Blue Rondo," the tom resonance during Morello's extended feature – all require wide dynamic range and excellent high-frequency resolution to reproduce correctly. On lesser pressings these smear into undifferentiated hash; on the best they have spatial presence and individual texture.

**Brubeck's piano** occupies a distinctive register – not the close-miked intimacy of a Van Gelder piano, but the slightly warmer, more resonant character of the 30th Street church acoustic. Brubeck's left-hand voicings in particular can sound muddy and indistinct on compressed or poor pressings; on the best they have definition and harmonic complexity.

## Pressings to avoid

**CBS Six Eye copies:** These are the most common mistake purchase for collectors who don't know what to look for. The "CBS" overprint at the 12 o'clock position indicates a 1961+ pressing from later stampers. LJC's verdict after a head-to-head: "stodgy bass, blurred piano lacking transients, and Morello's ringing cymbals all but disappeared." Buy these only knowingly and at a significant discount from genuine first-press prices.

**EU grey-market editions (DOL, Waxtime, Vinyl Passion, etc.):** *Time Out* is among the most bootlegged jazz albums in the EU budget market, given its mass-market appeal and Columbia's long release history. These are digitally sourced from CD or streaming, pressed on substandard vinyl with no mastering credits and no licensed tape access. Avoid entirely.

**Japanese digitally remastered Columbia/CBS pressings (1980s–90s):** While some Japanese pressings of Columbia jazz titles are excellent (Toshiba plant, analog-sourced), the CBS/Sony digital remaster era produced a significant number of mediocre Japanese pressings of this title. Verify the mastering credit and matrix before purchasing.

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $45:** The **Analogue Productions AAPJ 8192 33rpm 180g** (~$35–40 new) is the answer – Bernie Grundman AAA mastering, QRP pressing, gatefold with session photos. It is simply the best accessible option in the catalogue and covers the essential listening experience completely.

**$80–$120:** The **Analogue Productions APJ 8192-45 45rpm 2LP** (~$80–100 new) gives you the full 45rpm treatment – wider grooves, marginally better bass and transient detail, plus the gatefold packaging with session photos. Wayne Garcia's "edition to own" verdict still holds.

**$100–$300:** A **clean NM US stereo Six Eye CS 8192, 1A/1A stampers, no CBS overprint** gives you Fred Plaut's original lacquer from the 30th Street Studio. The original has a specific "air" around the instruments – particularly around Morello's drums – that the AP reissues approximate but do not quite replicate. Buy only in genuine NM condition; VG+ copies of this title, with its dynamic peaks and exposed bass register, will disappoint.

**$150–$350:** The **Dutch Fontana 682 065 TL** or a **Classic Records 200g Quiex SV-P 4LP 45rpm** sit in this tier – both offer serious listening experiences, the Dutch pressing for its tube-era European character, the Classic for its resolution and quiet vinyl.

**The one-copy answer:** The **AP 33rpm AAPJ 8192** at ~$40 covers 90% of the sonic need. If 45rpm format matters to you and budget allows, the **AP 45rpm APJ 8192-45** is the complete answer without the search-and-condition anxiety of hunting originals.
