# Blue Train on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Blue Train* was recorded on **September 15, 1957**, at Van Gelder Studio in Hackensack, New Jersey – the last full year of Van Gelder's parents' living room studio before the 1959 move to Englewood Cliffs. Personnel: John Coltrane (tenor saxophone), Lee Morgan (trumpet), Curtis Fuller (trombone), Kenny Drew (piano, on loan from Riverside Records), Paul Chambers (bass), Philly Joe Jones (drums). Produced by Alfred Lion. Released November 1957 on Blue Note as BLP 1577 (mono); the stereo version (BST 1577) followed in 1960. This is Coltrane's only session as a leader for Blue Note – he was under contract to Prestige – and it stands as his finest recorded statement up to that point. All five tracks except "I'm Old Fashioned" are Coltrane originals. The title track is among the most immediately recognisable in jazz. Lee Morgan was 19 years old at the time of recording.

The Hackensack sessions have an acoustic character – the live room of a domestic space, close-miked – that Van Gelder never entirely replicated at Englewood Cliffs. The mono is widely considered the definitive format. The stereo (a separate native recording, not a fold-down – Van Gelder ran parallel mono and stereo chains at the 1957 sessions) opens the ensemble, particularly the horn arrangement, but places the horns hard-left, which is disorienting. The Tone Poet mono reissue (2022) is the most celebrated modern option.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Blue Note BLP 1577 mono, 47 West 63rd, deep groove both sides, no "Inc."](https://www.discogs.com/release/11786823-John-Coltrane-Blue-Train) – RVG stamped, Plastylite ear, NY23 label.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Blue Note Tone Poet 180g mono (2022)](https://www.discogs.com/release/23760082-John-Coltrane-Blue-Train) – Kevin Gray, AAA, RTI, gatefold.

**Best-value modern:** [Blue Note Classic Vinyl BLP 1577 180g (2020)](https://www.discogs.com/release/16219695-John-Coltrane-Blue-Train) – Kevin Gray, Optimal, 180g, ~$25.


## Recording history and tape provenance

Coltrane in September 1957 had been fired by Miles Davis for drug and alcohol use earlier in the year; his subsequent "spiritual awakening" and recovery had produced a startling creative urgency. The summer 1957 residency with Thelonious Monk at the Five Spot had sharpened his harmonic vocabulary further. By the time he entered Van Gelder's Hackensack studio, he was at the threshold of his mature style – the "sheets of sound" approach that would define *Giant Steps* was audible in embryo.

Alfred Lion gave Coltrane the full Blue Note session treatment: sufficient rehearsal time, a hand-picked rhythm section (the Davis band's Chambers and Jones), and the best supporting cast available. Lee Morgan at 19 was already among the most technically accomplished trumpeters in jazz – he had recorded his debut as a leader for Blue Note the previous year at 18. Curtis Fuller at 25 was in the early stages of establishing himself as one of the leading trombonists of the hard bop period. Kenny Drew appears "by courtesy of Riverside Records" – the same courtesy credit arrangement Blue Note used whenever a musician under contract elsewhere appeared on a session, as also occurred with Miles Davis on Cannonball Adderley's *Somethin' Else*.

The Five Spot residency with Monk had been crucial. Through six months playing Monk's angular, rhythmically displaced compositions, Coltrane had developed a fluency with unusual harmonic intervals and phrase lengths that Hackensack captured in full. The "sheets of sound" approach Paul Bley had named – dense, rushing cascades of notes covering chordal territory at speed – is audible in embryo on the title track and "Locomotion," both of which anticipate *Giant Steps*.

**Hackensack acoustic character:** September 1957 was Van Gelder's last full year at the Hackensack studio before the 1959 move to Englewood Cliffs. The close-miked domestic room gives *Blue Train* a particular intimacy – Coltrane's tenor is in the room with the listener in a way that no subsequent Blue Note session quite replicated. This character is most apparent in the mono.

**The mono question:** Van Gelder ran separate mono and stereo microphone chains and tape machines for the 1957 Hackensack sessions – the mono is not a fold-down of the stereo but a native recording. Most serious collectors strongly prefer the mono for *Blue Train* specifically: the tight mono soundfield concentrates Coltrane's tenor, the ensemble horn lines are compellingly dense, and the rhythm section anchor is more immediate. The stereo's hard-left horn arrangement – all three horns in the left channel, a 1957 RVG convention he later moved away from – is considered a weakness by many listeners.

**Label chronology:** The earliest mono pressings have **47 West 63rd, New York 23** (with "New York 23" suffix) on the label, no "Inc.," and no ® after "Note." This is the rarest and most sought-after state. Slightly later copies have 47 West 63rd without "New York 23." Later still: "47 West 63rd Inc." (1959) and then "43 West 61st" (1960 address change). All of these have deep grooves and RVG stamps; the address change is purely a dating tool, not a sonic indicator.

**Tape provenance:** The original Alfred Lion–era tapes are in the Blue Note/Universal archive. Tracking Angle noted the Scotch 111 original tape as being in excellent condition 65 years on. The Tone Poet team (Joe Harley, Kevin Gray) accessed the original mono master tape for the 2022 mono edition; the stereo 2-LP Complete Masters used the original stereo tape.

## Original US Blue Note pressings

**Catalogue: BLP 1577** (mono) / **BST 1577** (stereo, 1960)

### First mono pressing – 47 West 63rd, NY23, no "Inc."

The earliest copies of BLP 1577 are identifiable by the **"New York 23"** suffix on the label (addressing format used on a small number of Blue Note releases from early-to-mid 1957):

- **Blue and white label** with deep groove on both sides
- **47 West 63rd, New York 23** address (or "47 West 63rd, NYC" without the suffix on slightly later copies)
- No "Inc." after "Blue Note Records"
- No ® under "Note"
- **RVG stamped** in the deadwax both sides
- **Plastylite ear** etched in the deadwax both sides
- **9 M** in the deadwax (lacquer-cutting session code)
- Matrix: **RVG BN-LP-1577-A P 9 M** / **RVG BN-LP-1577-B P 9 M**
- Laminated sleeve (dull greenish-blue photo; later covers have a brighter blue)

The NY23 label copies are the rarest state. LJC has documented the address chronology in detail; the absence of "Inc." is the critical identifier for the first pressing.

**NM originals (NY23): $400–$1,200. VG+: $200–$600.**

### Later 47 West 63rd and 43 West 61st pressings

All still carry the RVG stamp and Plastylite ear if from original metalwork. The address changes are date indicators. The 47 West 63rd "Inc." pressings date from 1959; 43 West 61st from 1960. All are considered original-era and collectible. **$100–$400 depending on condition and address.**

### Music Matters 33rpm SRX pressing – the reference audiophile reissue (OOP)

**Cat. MM33 BLP-1577** | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓, mono, tip-on jacket. **Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio, AAA. Pressed at QRP.** The Music Matters *Blue Train* became a benchmark for the label's Blue Note 1500-series work. Now out of print and commanding significant secondary market prices. Tracking Angle: "try to score the out of print Music Matters SRX pressing (MM33 BLP-1577 A/B), but it will cost you." **$200–$400 secondary.**

## The reissue hierarchy

### Blue Note Tone Poet 180g mono (2022) – the current definitive reissue

**Cat. B0035204-01** | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓, mono, gatefold tip-on jacket. **Produced by Joe Harley. Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio, AAA, from the original mono master tape. Pressed at RTI.** The Tone Poet mono of *Blue Train* is the most acclaimed modern reissue of this title. Kevin Gray accessed the original tapes in excellent condition and the result is widely regarded as superior to all earlier reissues. Tracking Angle: "in every way the best sounding, most historically complete, visually stunning Blue Train ever." The gatefold includes Francis Wolff session photography. **$45–$65 new.**

A companion **Blue Train: The Complete Masters 2LP stereo** (with seven alternate and incomplete takes, four previously unreleased) is available separately – useful for the Coltrane scholar but not the listening collector.

### Blue Note Classic Vinyl 180g mono (2020) – the accessible entry point

**Cat. BLP 1577** | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓, mono. **Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio. Pressed at Optimal in Germany.** The budget-accessible Kevin Gray cut. Most listeners will not find a significant gap between this and the Tone Poet for normal listening conditions, though the Tone Poet is the superior pressing on resolving systems. **$20–$30 new.**

## Pressings to avoid

**Stereo for primary listening:** BST 1577 is not a fold-down – it is a native stereo recording – but the hard-left horn placement makes it an uncomfortable listening experience for most. Mono is the strongly recommended format.

**1970s–80s general reissues:** Various Blue Note/Liberty/United Artists reissues from this era are not recommended. The Classic Vinyl at the same price point is a better choice.

---

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $30:** The [**Blue Note Classic Vinyl BLP 1577 mono**](https://www.discogs.com/release/16219695) – Kevin Gray mastering, Optimal pressing. The starting point.

**$45–$65:** The [**Blue Note Tone Poet mono (2022)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/23760082) – the definitive modern version. This is the recommended purchase for any serious listener.

**$200–$400:** The [**Music Matters SRX MM33 BLP-1577**](https://www.discogs.com/release/5193282) if found at a fair secondary price – excellent, but the Tone Poet has largely supplanted it.

**$400+:** An original [**47 West 63rd BLP 1577 mono**](https://www.discogs.com/release/11786823) (ideally NY23 label, no "Inc.") in honest VG+ or better – the collector's grail for this title.
