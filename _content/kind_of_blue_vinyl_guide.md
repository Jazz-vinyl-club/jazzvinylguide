# Kind of Blue on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Kind of Blue* was recorded at Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York City across two sessions: **March 2, 1959** (Side 1: "So What," "Freddie Freeloader," "Blue in Green") and **April 22, 1959** (Side 2: "All Blues," "Flamenco Sketches"). Producer was **Irving Townsend**; recording engineer **Fred Plaut**. Personnel: Miles Davis (trumpet), John Coltrane (tenor saxophone), Julian "Cannonball" Adderley (alto saxophone, absent on "Blue in Green"), Bill Evans (piano on all tracks except "Freddie Freeloader"), Wynton Kelly (piano on "Freddie Freeloader"), Paul Chambers (bass), Jimmy Cobb (drums). Davis arrived both days with only modal sketches; the performances were largely first takes.

A tape machine error on March 2 ran the prime three-track deck roughly 1.5–2% slow, causing Side 1 to play sharp on every pressing made from that tape – both the original mono and stereo. The defect went undiscovered for 33 years until Mark Wilder found it in 1992; the original mono master was already lost, so all modern "mono" reissues are new fold-downs from the three-track safety reel. This single fact divides the entire pressing landscape into two lineages: the **Grundman/Classic/AP** chain (cut direct from the three-track session master in 1995, one generation closer to the source) and the **Wilder/Sony/MoFi** chain (from Wilder's 1997 two-track fold-down). Every serious pressing decision follows from understanding which lineage you are buying into.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Columbia CS 8163 six-eye stereo](https://www.discogs.com/release/1353040-Miles-Davis-Kind-Of-Blue) – black label with "Stereo Fidelity" banner; earliest matrices 1E/1K; uncorrected speed.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Analogue Productions UHQR 45rpm 2LP (UHQR 0004-45, 2022)](https://www.discogs.com/master/34670-Miles-Davis-Kind-Of-Blue) – Grundman metal parts, 200g Clarity Vinyl, QRP; consensus best.

**Best-value modern:** [Analogue Productions AAPJ 8163 2LP (2025)](https://www.discogs.com/master/34670-Miles-Davis-Kind-Of-Blue) – same Grundman lineage, speed-corrected, ~$50–$60 new.


## Recording history and tape provenance

Columbia ran four machines in parallel at both sessions: a prime full-track mono, a safety full-track mono, a prime half-inch three-track, and a safety half-inch three-track. On March 2 only, the prime three-track capstan ran slow. Every 1959 mix – mono and stereo – was made from the prime reel, so Side 1 plays sharp on all original pressings and any reissue derived from those mixes. Side 2 is unaffected; the machine had been corrected before April 22.

**Mark Wilder discovered the discrepancy in 1992** while pulling tapes for a reissue. He found five reels in the vault: original session masters, a deteriorating stereo mixdown master, and the two safety reels untouched since 1959. The original mono master was already lost. For his **1997 Sony SBM remaster**, Wilder created a new analog three-to-two mixdown using a tube Presto deck, GML line mixer, and Ampex ATR-102 at 15 ips with Dolby SR – speed-corrected from the safety reel. That 1997 two-track is the source for virtually every subsequent digital release and most vinyl reissues, including the Mobile Fidelity box.

**Bernie Grundman cut directly from the half-inch three-track session master in 1995**, folding the center channel to L/R and applying fades live during cutting. This is one generation closer to the source than anything Wilder worked from. Classic Records used these metal parts; Analogue Productions acquired them when Classic closed and has used them for all UHQR and 2025 editions.

Collectors identify speed-corrected pressings by running times: uncorrected "So What" runs ~9:07, corrected ~9:21. Anyone with relative pitch will hear the opening "So What" bass note centred closer to D♭ rather than D on original pressings.

The cover photograph is by **Jay Maisel** – a tight close-up of Davis's face lit dramatically against darkness, widely considered one of the great jazz album covers. Cover misspellings: "Adderly" for Adderley appears on all pressings from 1959 into the 1970s; the Side 2 track-listing error (All Blues listed before Flamenco Sketches) appears on earliest six-eye pressings and persists on the back cover into the late 1970s.

## Original US Columbia pressings (1959)

Released in both mono and stereo:

- **Mono: Columbia CL 1355**
- **Stereo: Columbia CS 8163**

### Six-eye label identification

The original pressings use Columbia's **six-eye label** – named for the six CBS "eye" logos arranged around the perimeter. Mono labels are red with "GUARANTEED HIGH FIDELITY" arc; stereo labels are black with "STEREO FIDELITY" banner at top.

**Mono (CL 1355):**
- Red six-eye label, "GUARANTEED HIGH FIDELITY"
- Deep groove pressing ring around the label
- Matrix format: **XLP 47324–1[alpha]** (Side 1) / **XLP 47325–1[alpha]** (Side 2)
- First pressing matrices: 1A, 1B, 1C, and some 1D copies, all accompanied by the **Side 2 label error** (All Blues listed first, Flamenco Sketches second – the reverse of the back cover order)
- White-label promos pressed from lacquers D, G, H (confirmed Terre Haute plant from "T/TA" stamps). LJC's survey of ~200 matrix pairs finds 1D the most common mono matrix

**Stereo (CS 8163):**
- Black six-eye label, "STEREO FIDELITY" at top
- Matrix format: **XSM 47326–1[alpha]** (Side 1) / **XSM 47327–1[alpha]** (Side 2)
- True single-alpha first matrices (1A, 1B, 1C) are extremely rare and disputed – LJC's comprehensive research found no verified 1A/1A or 1B/1B stereo copy in a sample of ~200. The earliest reliably documented stereo copies are 1E/1K and 1H; white-label stereo promos used lacquers E, K, AC, and AE
- Some early stereo copies also carry the Side 2 label error; the 1AA and 1AC matrices are documented with the error
- The stereo was pressed in significantly smaller quantities than mono; over two-thirds of six-eye copies at auction are mono, reflecting period purchasing habits

**The "Adderly" misprint** appears on the front cover of all pressings until well into the 1970s. It is not a dating indicator.

**The Side 2 track-listing error** on the *label* (not just the back cover) is the primary indicator of earliest pressings. The back cover error persisted independently to at least 1977 (confirmed on PC 8163 red label).

### Market values

NM **stereo CS 8163 six-eye**, early matrix: **$400–$700+**. White-label stereo promos: **$1,000–$2,000**. NM **mono CL 1355 six-eye** with Side 2 label error: **$250–$600**. White-label mono promos: **$800–$1,500**. Clean six-eye copies in honest VG+ are significantly more affordable; ultrasonic cleaning is standard practice for anything in this price range.

### The mono vs. stereo question

This is *Kind of Blue*'s most genuine collector controversy, unresolved in audiophile circles. The stereo mix places instruments across the field (Davis and Chambers near centre; Coltrane right; Adderley and Evans left depending on track) with the echo chamber send/return affecting only the centre channel. Mono has demonstrably stronger bass below 600 Hz – up to 5dB more per spectrum analysis – which drives much of the format preference. The stereo's three-dimensional depth and 30th Street Studio ambience cannot be replicated in mono.

The practical position taken by most experienced collectors: own both. The mono's intimacy ("a warm cozy fireside chat") and the stereo's epic roominess ("a Star Wars epic") are genuinely different listening experiences, not just different formats.

## Later US Columbia label variants

**Two-eye "360 Sound" stereo (1962–70):** Third-generation lacquers (1CA–1CL series). Many experienced collectors – including Tom Port of Better Records – consider clean two-eyes essentially equivalent to six-eyes sonically; price differences reflect collectibility more than sound. **Black-text two-eye NM: $100–$250. White-text two-eye NM: $50–$150.**

**KCS 8163 / PC 8163 red "Trident" label (1974 onward):** The 1970s red Columbia label pressing is a sleeper – occasionally praised by experienced collectors as a musical listen at very low cost. One Discogs reviewer with the full range of pressings calls the 70s red label "sweeter and more alive sounding than the Classic remix by BG." A minority view, but consistent enough to note. **$15–$40.**

## The two tape lineages and their reissues

### Lineage A – Grundman / Classic / Analogue Productions

Bernie Grundman's 1995 direct cut from the three-track session master is the foundation. Classic Records used these metal parts for everything they released; Analogue Productions acquired them when Classic closed and has used them for every subsequent UHQR and the 2025 2LP.

**Classic Records CS 8163-45 4×45rpm single-sided box (1999)**

Format: 4×LP, 45rpm, single-sided, Clarity Vinyl, stereo, uncorrected speed. The same Grundman 1995 cut as the UHQRs. Fred Kaplan (Stereophile) considered it "very close in quality to Columbia's Six-Eye first pressing." Out of print; **$500–$1,500** on the secondary market.

**Classic Records CS 8163 180g 33⅓rpm (1995–96)**

Format: double LP, 33⅓, 180g, Sides A/B at original (uncorrected) speed, Side C corrected – the first vinyl speed-corrected release of Side 1. Same Grundman cut. **$150–$300** used.

**Analogue Productions UHQR 33⅓rpm (UHQR 0004, 2021)**

Format: single 200g Clarity Vinyl, 33⅓, stereo, speed-corrected, numbered to 25,000, hand-pressed at QRP. Grundman metal parts. Michael Fremer reviewed it as "quite clearly and obviously" the best pressing he had compared across two early six-eyes, Classic's double 180g, Classic's 45rpm box, and the MoFi double 45. Some copies reported with Clarity Vinyl surface noise that responds to ultrasonic cleaning. **~$100 MSRP; $150–$250 secondary.**

**Analogue Productions UHQR 45rpm 2LP (UHQR 0004-45, 2022)**

Format: 2×200g Clarity Vinyl, 45rpm, stereo, speed-corrected, numbered to 25,000, hand-pressed at QRP. Grundman metal parts. The current consensus best pressing of *Kind of Blue* in any format.

Fred Kaplan (Stereophile): 11/11 music and 11/11 sonics – "the best-sounding Kind of Blue ever, superior in every way to all previous pressings, including the original… Cobb's drumkit is spooky real." Mike Hobson (Classic Records founder): "The Analogue Productions UHQR pressing from the same metal parts has taken it up a notch and gotten us even closer to the sound on those 1/2-inch analog session master tapes." New bass notes audible that weren't present on earlier pressings; extraordinary stereo depth and room ambience. **~$150 MSRP; $200–$350 secondary.**

**Analogue Productions AAPJ 8163 2LP (2025)**

Format: 2×180g, 33⅓, stereo, speed-corrected, gatefold tip-on by Stoughton, QRP. LP1 corrected speed; LP2 Side 4 at 45rpm with the alternate "Flamenco Sketches." Same Grundman/Classic metal parts. Mark Smotroff (AnalogPlanet): 11/11 music, 10/11 sound – the right go-to play copy for regular enjoyment relative to the more precious UHQR. Black vinyl standard edition (AAPJ 8163) and blue vinyl limited to 1,500 copies (AAPJ 8163BLU). **~$50–$60 new.**

### Lineage B – Wilder / Sony / Mobile Fidelity

Mark Wilder's 1997 two-track fold-down is the source for Sony/Legacy reissues, Music on Vinyl, and Mobile Fidelity's box. It is one generation further from the source than the Grundman cut but represents a thoughtful, well-regarded transfer in its own right.

**Sony/Legacy 180g mono (RSD Black Friday 2013, cat. 88883761031)**

Format: single 180g, 33⅓, mono, speed-corrected, RTI pressing. Mastered by Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound from a new mono fold-down of the safety three-track. Since the original mono master is lost, this is the definitive modern mono pressing. Fremer: 11/11 music, 11/11 sound – "a necessity not an add-on option." Also appears on the same day as Music on Vinyl MOVLP961. **$30–$80 used; periodically in print.**

**Mobile Fidelity MFSL 2-45011 45rpm 2LP (2015, repressed 2020)**

Format: 2×180g, 45rpm, stereo, speed-corrected, numbered box, RTI pressing. Mastered by Krieg Wunderlich on GAIN 2 Ultra Analog System. Source chain: Wilder's 1997 two-track → **DSD-64 digital intermediate** → lathe. The digital step was not disclosed until the 2022 "MoFiGate" revelations.

Pre-2022 it drew strong praise. Post-2022, comparisons against the UHQR 45rpm have placed it lower: one thorough YouTube shootout ranked it last of four – "this MoFi just doesn't hold up," described as "one-dimensional a little bit," with piano detail and bass register lacking relative to the UHQR. Fred Kaplan was already a pre-2022 dissenter: "the bass is too strong, the highs seem boosted." Still a musical pressing – better than most – but no longer the reference it once seemed. **$150–$300 used; not worth premium prices with the AP 2025 available new.**

**Music on Vinyl MOVLP019 2LP stereo (2010)**

Format: 2×180g, 33⅓, stereo, speed-corrected, gatefold, Optimal pressing. Includes "Flamenco Sketches (alt.)" and "On Green Dolphin Street." Source: 24/96 PCM from Wilder's master – digitally sourced. Fremer found it surprisingly good; others note the digital intermediate audibly. Dead-silent surfaces. **$30–$60.**

## Japanese pressings

**CBS/Sony 18AP 2056 (1981) – "Miles 25" series**

Released June 21, 1981; stereo, uncorrected speed (predates 1992 discovery); heavy dead-quiet Japanese vinyl, obi strip and insert. Consistently praised on Discogs for "beautifully pressed, super quiet vinyl, very clear and well-defined sound." **NM with obi: $50–$150.**

**CBS/Sony SOPL 155 SX68 Mark II (1972)**

Earlier Japanese audiophile pressing in the SX68 series; matrix SOPL-155A1. Heavy and quiet; generally well-regarded. **NM with obi: $40–$120.**

## Pressings to avoid

**EU public-domain pressings (DOL, Doxy, WaxTime, Not Now Music, Jazz Wax, Vinyl Passion, Jazz Images, Green Corner, 20th Century Masterworks):** Not licensed from Sony; sourced from CD or worse. "Very silent vinyl but sounds like the master was a not very high quality MP3" is a consistent description. The coloured 180g variants at $15–$20 are tempting; they sound like what they are.

**Sony/Legacy 50th Anniversary blue LP (2008, box only):** Lacquers by Greg Calbi and Ray Janos; pressing (Noiseland) widely criticised for noisy non-fill. Not sold separately.

**1980s Columbia Jazz Masterpieces series:** Early digital remix to low-resolution PCM tape.

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $60:** The **AP AAPJ 8163 2LP (2025)** at ~$50–$60 new is the obvious recommendation – Grundman lineage, speed-corrected, tip-on gatefold, QRP pressing. This is the entry point into the authoritative source chain at an accessible price. Add the **Sony 88883761031 mono** at $30–$80 for the other half of the picture.

**$150–$250:** The **AP UHQR 33rpm** for a step up in pressing luxury from the same source. Ulsonically clean before first play.

**$200–$350:** The **AP UHQR 45rpm** is the definitive modern pressing. Worth every penny if the music matters to you and you have the system to appreciate it.

**$400–$700:** A clean **Columbia CS 8163 six-eye stereo** original for the tube-cut, tape-fresh character no reissue fully recovers.

**$250–$600:** A clean **Columbia CL 1355 six-eye mono** – the intimate, bass-forward presentation that many experienced collectors consider the definitive listening copy regardless of pressing generation.

**The MoFi question:** Still a musical pressing worth having if acquired cheaply ($80–$120 used). Not worth premium prices now that the AP 2025 undercuts it on both price and provenance.
