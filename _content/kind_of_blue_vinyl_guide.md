# Kind of Blue on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Hierarchy and Buyer's Guide

---

## The slow Side 1 error, fully untangled

*Kind of Blue* was tracked at Columbia's 30th Street Studio on two dates: **March 2, 1959** (Side 1: "So What," "Freddie Freeloader," "Blue in Green") and **April 22, 1959** (Side 2: "All Blues," "Flamenco Sketches"). Columbia's standard practice was to run four decks in parallel — prime and safety full-track mono, plus prime and safety half-inch three-track. On March 2 only, the **prime three-track machine's capstan ran roughly 1.25% slow** (the figure Mark Wilder consistently cites; independent measurements range 1.5–2.16%, with the first bass note of "So What" measured at ~37 cents sharp). The safety three-track ran correctly. Because every 1959 mixdown — mono and stereo alike — was made from that fast prime tape, **both the original mono LP and the stereo LP play Side 1 sharp**. Side 2 is untouched because the prime deck had been recalibrated before April 22.

The defect went undetected for 33 years. Teo Macero later claimed he had intentionally wrapped tape around the capstan to "put it in the pocket," but most engineers dismiss this as posthumous embellishment — the safety reels are physical proof the slowdown was confined to the prime machine. **Mark Wilder discovered the discrepancy in 1992** while pulling tapes for a new release, finding five reels in the vault: the original session masters, the deteriorating stereo mixdown master, and the two safety reels untouched since 1959. The original mono master was already lost (never found), so every modern "mono" reissue is in fact a new mono fold-down from the three-track safety.

The first speed-corrected commercial release was the **1992 Sony MasterSound 24-karat Gold CD (CK 52861)**, followed by Sony/Legacy's 1997 SBM remaster (CK 64935), for which Wilder created a new analog 3-to-2 mixdown on a tube Presto deck through a GML line mixer to an Ampex ATR-102 at 15 ips with Dolby SR. **That 1997 two-track mix is the source of virtually every subsequent digital and most vinyl reissues**, except those using Bernie Grundman's 1995 metal parts (Classic Records, Analogue Productions UHQR, AP 2025), which were cut "live" directly from the three-track master — one tape generation closer to the source.

**Collectors identify slow vs. corrected pressings by running times**: uncorrected "So What" runs ~9:07, corrected ~9:21; "Freddie Freeloader" 9:25 vs 9:32; "Blue in Green" 5:21 vs 5:27. Anyone with relative pitch will hear "So What" centred closer to D♭ instead of D on the originals. Audibly, the corrected versions have a less brassy timbre; the originals retain a slight cymbal sparkle and forward energy that some collectors prefer as "the way we've always heard it." Classic Records was the only reissue label to commercially release both, their 1995 double-LP placing original-speed Sides A/B against corrected-speed Side C.

## The two diverging tape lineages

Every serious Kind of Blue pressing falls into one of two ancestral chains:

**Lineage A — Grundman/Classic/AP:** Bernie Grundman cut direct from the half-inch three-track session master in 1995, folding the center channel to L/R and applying fades manually during cutting. This is **one generation closer to the source** than anything Wilder worked from. Classic Records used these metal parts; Acoustic Sounds/Analogue Productions acquired them when Classic closed and has used them for every UHQR and the 2025 2LP.

**Lineage B — Wilder/Sony/MoFi:** Mark Wilder's 1997 two-track analog fold-down (1/4" / 15 ips / Dolby SR) is the source for Sony/Legacy reissues, Music on Vinyl, the 50th Anniversary LP, and — critically — Mobile Fidelity's 2015 box, which inserted an additional DSD-64 digital step before the lathe, revealed in 2022.

## The pressing hierarchy

### S-Tier — Reference level

**1. Analogue Productions UHQR 45rpm 2LP (2022)**
- Cat#: **UHQR 0004-45** / AUHQR 0004-45 | UPC: 753088045073
- Format: Stereo, speed-corrected, 2×200g Clarity Vinyl, 45rpm, numbered to 25,000
- Mastering: Grundman 1995 metal parts, cut live from three-track master; machine-pressed at QRP
- The current consensus pinnacle. Fred Kaplan (Stereophile): 11/11 music, 11/11 sonics — "the best-sounding Kind of Blue ever, superior in every way to all previous pressings, including the original… Cobb's drumkit is spooky real." Fremer: "head and shoulders above" all prior reissues. A YouTube collector who owns OG mono, Classic '95, and the MoFi confirms the ranking: "this is just head and shoulders above any other release… it doesn't feel close." Mike Hobson (Classic Records founder): "The Analogue Productions UHQR pressing from the same metal parts has taken it up a notch and gotten us even closer to the sound on those 1/2-inch analog session master tapes."
- New bass notes audible that weren't present on earlier pressings; extraordinary stereo depth and room ambience
- Market: ~$150 MSRP new; secondary $200–$350

**2. Columbia Six-Eye Stereo, original pressing (1959)**
- Cat#: **CS 8163** | Label: Black with red "Stereo Fidelity" banner
- Format: Stereo, uncorrected speed, 6-eye label
- Earliest matrix copies are 1E, 1H, 1AA, 1AC (no commercial 1A/1B/1C documented for stereo); Side 2 label error (Flamenco Sketches listed first) on earliest pressings; "Adderly" cover misspelling
- The tape-fresh "air" and tube-cut warmth that no reissue fully recovers. LondonJazzCollector: "It is sublime. It lives. It breathes… like being present during the performance in the presence of greatness." The Ingroove's Mike rates the Six-Eye stereo as his ultimate alongside the MoFi 2×45: "the ultimate is going to be stereo 6i and the MFSL double disc 45 RPM reissue"
- Significantly rarer than the mono; NM copies fetch $400–$600 routinely; Side 2 label-error copies $500–$1,200; stereo white-label promos (from lacquers E, K, AC, AE only) $1,000–$2,000
- Uncorrected speed is either a feature or a flaw depending on your ear

**3. Columbia Six-Eye Mono, original pressing (1959)**
- Cat#: **CL 1355** | Label: Red/maroon with six eyes, "GUARANTEED HIGH FIDELITY" arc
- Format: Mono, uncorrected speed, 6-eye label
- Matrix XLP 47324-1X / XLP 47325-1X; earliest commercial copies 1A–1D paired with the Side 2 label error; promos from lacquers A, D, G, H only
- More readily available than the stereo original — pressed in much larger quantities — yet still commands a premium in clean condition. The mono mix has noticeably stronger bass below 600 Hz (up to 5dB, per spectrum analysis) and a cohesive, intimate quality that the stereo's hard panning can't match. A Dutch collector who owns OG mono, MoFi, and UHQR calls it a "completely different album" — intimate and fireplace-close versus the epic room-filling stereo — and considers the shootout "a tie: gotta have both." Coltrane in particular sounds extraordinarily bright and dynamic on original mono copies
- White-label promos NM: $1,000–$1,500; standard early Six-Eye NM: $200–$400; Side 2 label-error copies: $400–$1,200

### A-Tier — Excellent, audiophile-grade

**4. Classic Records 4×45rpm Single-Sided Box (1999)**
- Cat#: **CS 8163-45** | Matrices: CS-8163-A1-45 through D2-45
- Format: Stereo, uncorrected speed, 4×LP 45rpm single-sided, Clarity Vinyl
- Grundman 1995 cut, same metal parts as the UHQRs. Considered by many (including Fred Kaplan) to be "very close in quality to Columbia's Six-Eye first pressing." Out of print; trades $500–$1,500
- The Ingroove notes Classic's 45rpm editions "would be right up there" but notes the Classic 33rpm as the more accessible recommendation

**5. Analogue Productions UHQR 33⅓rpm (2021)**
- Cat#: **UHQR 0004** / AUHQR 0004 | UPC: 753088400018
- Format: Stereo, speed-corrected, 200g Clarity Vinyl, 33rpm, numbered to 25,000, hand-pressed at QRP
- Same Grundman metal parts. Fremer: "Quite clearly and obviously this new UHQR Kind of Blue… sounds best" compared to two early Six-Eyes, Classic's double 180g, Classic's 45rpm box, and MoFi's double 45. Some buyers report Clarity Vinyl surface noise that responds to ultrasonic cleaning
- Market: ~$100 MSRP; secondary $150–$250

**6. Classic Records 200g Quiex SV-P (2002)**
- Cat#: **CS 8163** (200g Quiex SV-P pressing) | Barcode: 601704816319
- Released July 2002; a limited 100-copy blue vinyl variant also exists
- Same Grundman cut on flat-profile Quiex SV-P vinyl; prioritises dead-quiet surfaces, some feel it gives up a trace of "presence" vs. the earlier 180g variants. Still extremely good

**7. Sony/Legacy 180g Mono (RSD Black Friday 2013)**
- Cat#: **88883761031** | Barcode: 888837610315
- Format: Mono, speed-corrected (from three-track safety), 180g, numbered/unnumbered retail versions; RTI pressing
- Mastered by Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound from Wilder's new mono fold-down. Since the original mono master is lost, this is the definitive modern mono reissue. Fremer: 11/11 music, 11/11 sound — "a necessity not an add-on option." Also appears as Music on Vinyl **MOVLP961** (EAN 8718469534517) released simultaneously on RSD BF 2013
- Market: $30–$80 used; occasionally in print at retail

### B-Tier — Very good; worth owning

**8. Classic Records 180g 33⅓rpm (1995–96)**
- Cat#: **CS 8163** (180g) | Barcode: 601704816319
- Format: Stereo, Sides A/B at original (uncorrected) speed; Side C corrected; Side D the alternate "Flamenco Sketches" at 45rpm — the first vinyl corrected-speed release
- Same Grundman cut as everything above it. The YouTube collector who owns this, OG mono, MoFi, and UHQR ranks it second overall: "this is a solid release… better and more enjoyable than the mobile fidelity overall." His one complaint: "the stereo separation on this record is too separate — they just kept the two channels a little bit too contained." Cannonball sounds "really really good, maybe even better than on the UHQR." QC on new copies was inconsistent; The Ingroove recommends buying used to inspect condition first
- Market: $150–$300

**9. Analogue Productions AAPJ 8163 180g 2LP "Corrected Speed" (2025)**
- Cat#: **AAPJ 8163** (black) / **AAPJ 8163BLU** (blue, 1,500 copies) | Barcode: 196588867613 (black) / 198028376716 (blue)
- Format: Stereo, LP1 speed-corrected; LP2 Side 4 at 45rpm (alt. "Flamenco Sketches"); Stoughton tip-on gatefold; QRP pressing
- Same Grundman/Classic metal parts. Mark Smotroff (AnalogPlanet): 11/11 music, 10/11 sound — the right "go-to play copy for regular enjoyment" relative to the more precious UHQR. The accessible entry point into the Grundman lineage
- Market: ~$50–$60 new

**10. Mobile Fidelity MFSL 2-45011 45rpm 2LP (2015, repressed 2020)**
- Cat#: **MFSL 2-45011** | Barcode: 821797450119; Sony parallel: 88697943631
- Format: Stereo, speed-corrected, 2×180g 45rpm, numbered box; RTI pressing; mastered by Krieg Wunderlich on GAIN 2 Ultra Analog System
- Source chain: Wilder's 1997 two-track → **DSD-64 digital step** → lathe (the "MoFi-gate" revelation, 2022)
- The most contested pressing in the catalogue. Pre-2022, it had enthusiastic defenders: "my go-to stereo copy — great natural warm open sound" (The Ingroove's Mike, who still rates it alongside the Six-Eye stereo as the two he'd choose for listening). A Dutch YouTube collector fell in love with it before the UHQR arrived. Post-MoFi-gate, reassessed downward; the YouTube collector who did a four-way shootout ranked it last of four: "this mofi just doesn't hold up" compared to the UHQR — "one-dimensional a little bit," hard to hear bass in lower registers, piano detail lacking. Fred Kaplan was a pre-2022 dissenter: "the bass is too strong, the highs seem boosted… the horns are one-dimensional." The hidden DSD step is real and measurable
- Market: $150–$300; rare "smoky grey-blue SuperVinyl" variant higher

**11. Japanese CBS/Sony 18AP 2056 (1981)**
- Cat#: **18AP 2056** | CBS/Sony Japan "Miles 25" series, no. 6; released June 21, 1981
- Format: Stereo, uncorrected speed; heavy, dead-quiet Japanese vinyl; obi strip and insert
- Consistently praised on Discogs for "beautifully pressed, super quiet vinyl, very clear and well-defined sound." Uncorrected speed (predates 1992 discovery)
- Market: NM with obi $50–$150

### Avoid

**EU public-domain pirates** — DOL, Doxy, WaxTime, Jazz Wax, Not Now Music, Vinyl Passion, Jazz Images, Green Corner, 20th Century Masterworks. Not licensed from Sony; sourced from CDs or worse. "VERY silent vinyl but sounds like the master was a not very high quality MP3" (Pink Fish Media). The 180g coloured-vinyl variants at $15–$20 are tempting; they sound like what they are.

**1980s Columbia Jazz Masterpieces vinyl** — early digital remix to low-resolution PCM tape.

**Generic "Legacy Vinyl" Amazon-tier reissues** proliferating today.

**Note on Japanese red-label 1970s:** The Ingroove's Mike is unequivocal: "The 70s issue sounds horrible." This conflicts with the occasional collector praise in #16 above — caveat emptor.

## The mono vs. stereo debate

This is Kind of Blue's most genuine controversy and is **not resolved** in audiophile circles. The technical asymmetry is significant: the stereo mix is really the three-track folded to two, with instruments hard-panned (Davis and Chambers centre; Coltrane right; Adderley/Evans left depending on track), and only the centre channel had send/return to the studio echo chamber. Mono has noticeably stronger bass below 600 Hz (up to 5 dB more per spectrum analysis), which drives much of the format preference.

**The mono case:** Fremer, Steve Hoffman, and experienced collectors argue mono provides better overall instrumental balance — "the music experience is complete and organically whole." On headphones the stereo's hard panning becomes jarring; mono is the obvious choice. The Dutch collector who owns OG mono and the AP UHQR 33rpm says he cannot choose: "sometimes I just feel like putting on the mono version which is lovely and intimate in a romantic way, and sometimes you just want to put on this one which is a stellar sounding audiophile release that takes over the room." The original mono's Coltrane solo on "So What" sounds "much brighter, so dynamic and so clear, with so much energy" per one YouTube comparison. There is also a credible argument (per Ashley Kahn's research) that Davis actually approved the mono mix while stereo was an afterthought.

**The stereo case:** Every top-tier modern reissue — UHQR, Classic, MoFi — is stereo. The three-dimensional spaciousness and 30th Street Studio ambience genuinely cannot be replicated in mono. If you want the absolute best vinyl sound currently obtainable, you are listening to stereo.

**The pragmatic answer:** Own both. The Sony 88883761031 mono at $30–$80 and the AP UHQR 45rpm or Classic Records pressing for stereo covers the full picture.

## Phantom releases: what doesn't exist

Any seller advertising these should be treated with deep scepticism:

- **MFSL 1-200** — Mobile Fidelity's first vinyl edition was the 2015 box (MFSL 2-45011), not a 1-series LP
- **Mobile Fidelity One-Step Kind of Blue** — Sony reserved the title for Analogue Productions; no MoFi One-Step exists
- **Speakers Corner edition** — never released
- **Vinyl Me, Please edition** — never released
- **US CBS "MasterSound" half-speed LP** — the MasterSound branding applied only to the 1992 Gold CD (CK 52861), never to vinyl
- **Japanese 30AP Master Sound audiophile LP** — the 1982 Japanese Master Sound remastering was CD-only (35DP 62); no vinyl equivalent
- **Japanese 25AP-755 or 23AP-2556 "audiophile" variants** — these are standard CBS/Sony Japan pressings, not a special audiophile series

## What to actually buy, by budget

**No budget constraint:** AP UHQR 45rpm (UHQR 0004-45). It is the definitive modern reading of the stereo text. Add the Sony 88883761031 mono for the other half of the picture.

**Serious audiophile, more accessible:** AP UHQR 33rpm (UHQR 0004) or the new AP AAPJ 8163 2LP Corrected Speed (2025). Both use the same Grundman metal parts.

**Vintage original hunt:** Columbia CS 8163 Six-Eye stereo with Side 2 label error, or CL 1355 Six-Eye mono. Budget $200–$600 for a clean copy; be prepared to clean ultrasonically.

**Classic Records route:** CS 8163-45 single-sided 4×45rpm box if budget allows ($500–$1,500); Classic 200g Quiex SV-P for a more affordable entry into the Grundman lineage ($100–$200 used).

**Best value modern stereo:** AP AAPJ 8163 2025 at ~$50–$60 new. Same source as the UHQRs at a fraction of the price.

**Best value digital-sourced:** Music on Vinyl MOVLP019 at ~$30–$60. Acknowledge the 24/96 PCM intermediate; the pressing itself is excellent.

**MoFi question:** Still a good-sounding record despite MoFi-gate. Worth owning if acquired cheaply ($80–$120 used). Not worth paying premium prices any longer when the AP 2025 2LP undercuts it on both price and provenance.

## Key corrections noted from community sources

- The **Classic Records 1995 double-LP was not the first time corrected speed appeared commercially on vinyl** — it was the first *vinyl* corrected-speed release, but the 1992 Sony Gold CD preceded it
- The **stereo speed error affected Side 1 only** (March 2 recording). Side 2 (April 22) is correct on all pressings. Some online sources incorrectly claim the error affects both sides
- The **mono originals share the speed error** — both mono and stereo were mixed from the same fast prime three-track tape
- **88697638941** is not a standalone barcode; it is Sony's parallel catalogue reference co-printed on MOVLP019 copies, derived from EAN 886976389413
- The **Sony mono 88883761031 shipped November 29, 2013** (RSD Black Friday), not 2014 — though 2014-dated retail stock reflects the subsequent unnumbered general pressing
- The **Columbia 1970s reissue sequence runs KCS 8163 (1974) → PC 8163** (mid-1970s onward); there is no JCS 8163 for this title
