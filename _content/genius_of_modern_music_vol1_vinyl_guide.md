# Genius of Modern Music Volume 1 on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Genius of Modern Music, Volume One* compiles Thelonious Monk's first three sessions as a leader – October 15, October 24, and November 21, 1947, all at WOR Studios in New York City – first gathered onto a 10" LP in 1951 and expanded into the 12" format collectors chase today, Blue Note BLP 1510, in 1956. Personnel shifts by session: a sextet with Idrees Sulieman (trumpet), Danny Quebec West (alto sax), and Billy Smith (tenor sax) on October 15; a stripped-down trio of Monk, Gene Ramey (bass), and Art Blakey (drums) on October 24; and a quintet with George Taitt (trumpet), Sahib Shihab (alto sax), Bob Paige (bass), and Art Blakey (drums) on November 21. Alfred Lion produced; Doug Hawkins engineered.

This is the rare collector's guide where the tape itself, not the pressing, sets the ceiling on sound quality: these 1947 sessions were recorded on primitive equipment, and no reissue, however carefully mastered, can manufacture fidelity the original recording never captured. The collector question here isn't which pressing sounds most like the master tape; it's which pressing is the most honest, listenable transfer of inherently rough source material.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Blue Note BLP 1510, 767 Lexington labels](https://www.discogs.com/release/2587385-Thelonious-Monk-Genius-Of-Modern-Music-Volume-One) – flat edge, frame cover, RVG stamp, deep groove, Plastylite ear – the true 1956 first pressing.
**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Blue Note Classic Vinyl (2022)](https://www.discogs.com/release/25484383-Thelonious-Monk-Genius-Of-Modern-Music-Volume-One) – Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio, mono, 180g, pressed at Optimal.
**Best-value modern:** Same Classic Vinyl edition – it's also the most affordable and widely available way to hear this title well.

## Recording history and tape provenance

Thelonious Monk spent most of the 1940s as an underappreciated fixture of the Minton's Playhouse bebop scene – the pianist who younger, more famous players like Dizzy Gillespie and Charlie Parker would credit in interviews without translating into wider recognition for Monk himself. That began to change after journalist and photographer Bill Gottlieb, tipped off by pianist Mary Lou Williams, profiled Monk for a September 24, 1947 DownBeat cover story headlined "Thelonious Monk – Genius of Bop." Blue Note A&R man and recording artist Ike Quebec arranged for label co-founder Alfred Lion to visit Monk at his West 63rd Street apartment; Lion heard a solo performance including "'Round Midnight" and signed Monk within two weeks.

Monk led three sessions that autumn, all at WOR Studios with engineer Doug Hawkins. October 15 brought a sextet – Idrees Sulieman, Danny Quebec West, and Billy Smith joining Monk, Gene Ramey, and Art Blakey – for "Thelonious" and "Humph," among others. October 24 pared down to a trio of Monk, Ramey, and Blakey for "Well You Needn't," "Off Minor," "Ruby My Dear," and "Introspection." November 21 brought a different quintet – George Taitt and Sahib Shihab alongside Monk, bassist Bob Paige, and Blakey again – for "'Round Midnight," "In Walked Bud," "Monk's Mood," and "Who Knows?" A fourth session the following July, with vibraphonist Milt Jackson, rounded out the era's recordings but is generally associated with Volume 2 and the separate *Milt Jackson: Wizard of the Vibes* release rather than Volume 1.

These recordings were originally issued piecemeal as 78rpm singles (Blue Note 543, 547, 548, 549, and others) through the late 1940s. Blue Note first compiled the material onto a 10" LP, *Genius of Modern Music*, in 1951 – itself a scarce, largely overlooked format today given how quickly the 10" LP lost out to the 12" format industry-wide. In 1956, Blue Note recompiled and expanded the same core material as a 12" LP, *Genius of Modern Music, Volume One* (BLP 1510), with a Reid Miles cover – one of his earliest for the label, predating the graphic style he'd become famous for on later Blue Note sleeves. This 12" edition is what most collectors mean by "Genius of Modern Music Volume 1" today, and it's the version reissued in the modern era.

Critical and historical stature has only grown: these sessions contain the first recordings of compositions – "'Round Midnight," "Ruby My Dear," "Epistrophy," "In Walked Bud" – that would become jazz standards played by generations of musicians who never knew Monk personally. AllMusic's retrospective review calls the sessions foundational despite noting the band's evident unfamiliarity with Monk's material at the time – Blakey's drumming hadn't yet found its footing with Monk's compositional logic, and Shihab's alto tone reads as somewhat out of place to modern ears. None of that diminishes the historical weight: this is the first documented evidence of Monk as a bandleader and composer, not a sideman.

## Original Blue Note pressings

### 1956 first pressing – BLP 1510

Identifying features of the true first edition: "767 Lexington Ave" address on the labels, flat (not stepped) disc edge, framed front cover art, RVG etched in the deadwax, deep groove, and the Plastylite "P" ear. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/2587385-Thelonious-Monk-Genius-Of-Modern-Music-Volume-One) **$200–$500 NM**, reflecting genuine scarcity for a 1956 Blue Note original.

### The 1951 10" LP – a rarer historical curiosity

The original 10" compilation, also titled *Genius of Modern Music* (catalog BLP 5002), predates the 12" expansion by five years and draws from a slightly narrower selection of the same 1947 material. Given the 10" format's short commercial life, surviving copies are scarcer and less commonly traded than the 12" BLP 1510 – a genuine grail for Blue Note completists rather than a practical listening recommendation. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/21740968-Thelonious-Monk-Genius-Of-Modern-Music) **Rare; prices vary widely by condition and are not well-established in the used market.**

## Japanese pressings

Blue Note's catalog was licensed to King Records and later Toshiba EMI in Japan across several distinct series. This title appeared as **King GXF-3014** (1977, limited edition, part of King's higher-tier GXF series), **King GXK-8058** (1977–1980, part of the larger "Blue Note Masterpiece Selection 150" series), and later as part of **Toshiba EMI's "Blue Note BLP 1500 Series Part 1"** (c. 1983). As with other King/Toshiba Blue Note licenses, the earlier, more limited GXF series generally commands a premium over the larger-run GXK series among Japanese-pressing collectors. **$40–$100 with obi, depending on series.**

## Pressings to avoid

No specific bootleg or notably compromised pressing of this title is widely documented. The main thing to understand before buying any pressing, original or reissue, is that source-recording limitations (not pressing quality) are the ceiling on how good this can sound – don't expect Blue Note's later, tape-era hi-fi standards from material recorded in 1947.
