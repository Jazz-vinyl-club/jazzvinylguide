# Song for My Father on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Song for My Father* was recorded across three sessions, all at Van Gelder Studio, Englewood Cliffs, New Jersey. **October 31, 1963**: Blue Mitchell (trumpet), Junior Cook (tenor saxophone), Gene Taylor (bass), Roy Brooks (drums), and Silver (piano) — this lineup produced several tracks. **January 28, 1964**: A further session with the older quintet. **October 26, 1964**: Carmell Jones (trumpet), Joe Henderson (tenor saxophone), Teddy Smith (bass), Roger Humphries (drums), and Silver — this session produced the title track and the bulk of what became the album's new quintet material. Horace Silver (piano) leads all sessions. Produced by Alfred Lion; released December 1964 on Blue Note. The album's bossa nova influence — Silver had recently visited Brazil — gives it a warmth and melodic directness that made it one of Blue Note's most commercially successful releases of the 1960s. The title composition became a jazz standard; the main riff was famously sampled by Steely Dan for "Rikki Don't Lose That Number" (1974).

Both mono and stereo versions are worthwhile — the stereo is spacious and reveals the rhythm section detail particularly well. The best originals are the New York USA label pressings with the Van Gelder stamp. The Blue Note Classic Vinyl reissue is the accessible modern option; no dedicated Music Matters or Tone Poet edition had been released for this title as of mid-2025.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Blue Note BST 84185 stereo, New York USA label](https://www.discogs.com/release/2019742-Horace-Silver-Song-For-My-Father) – Van Gelder stamp, Plastylite ear.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Blue Note Classic Vinyl BST 84185 180g (2020)](https://www.discogs.com/release/16953990-Horace-Silver-Song-For-My-Father) – Kevin Gray, Optimal, 180g.

**Best-value modern:** Same Blue Note Classic Vinyl — it is both the audiophile option and the value option for this title.


## Recording history and tape provenance

Silver's trip to Brazil in the early 1960s shaped the harmonic and rhythmic sensibility of the title track — the gently swaying bass figure, the layered melody, and the overall warmth were departures from the more aggressive hard bop of his earlier Blue Note work. The two quintet sessions (one in late October, one slightly earlier) share musical territory but feature different personnel; the title track itself comes from the later October 26 session with Henderson and Jones.

**Joe Henderson's contribution:** The October 26 session is notable for Henderson's tenor work, which carries a slightly more open, searching quality than the Junior Cook lines on the October 31 date. Henderson was in his early Blue Note period and the contrast between the two quintets — one more blues-drenched (Mitchell/Cook), one more modal (Jones/Henderson) — gives the album an unusual internal range.

**Tape provenance:** The original Alfred Lion–era tapes are in the Blue Note/Universal archive. The Classic Vinyl series mastering by Kevin Gray sourced from the original analogue master tapes.

## Original US Blue Note pressings

**Catalogue: BLP 4185** (mono) / **BST 84185** (stereo)

### First pressing – New York USA label

Blue Note's New York USA label was in use 1964–67. Authentication points:

- **"Blue Note Records Inc. • New York USA"** on labels
- **Van Gelder stamp** in the deadwax
- **Plastylite ear** in the deadwax
- Deep groove labels on earliest copies
- Matrix: **BNST-84185-A VAN GELDER P** / **BNST-84185-B VAN GELDER P** (stereo)
- Gatefold sleeve with inner sleeve liner notes; some first copies have "25 Years Blue Note" inner sleeve

The mono (BLP 4185) carries the same authentication markers and commands similar prices. **NM originals: $150–$400. VG+: $75–$200.**

### Liberty-era pressings (1966–1971)

Labels read "A Division of Liberty Records Inc." Van Gelder stamps remain on many of these. Affordable and sonically adequate. **$30–$80 VG+.**

## The reissue hierarchy

### Blue Note Classic Vinyl 180g (2020) – the recommended modern pressing

**Cat. ST-84185** (or BST 84185) | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓. **Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio. Pressed at Optimal in Germany.** For *Song for My Father*, the Classic Vinyl is the only widely available modern audiophile reissue and it performs well. No Music Matters or Tone Poet edition exists for this title. **$20–$35 new.**

---

## Pressings to avoid

**Liberty/UA pressings without Van Gelder stamp:** Some later pressings in the Liberty era were cut from dubs. Inspect the deadwax before buying any non-original copy.

**Generic European reissues:** Various 1970s European reissues on Stateside, HMV, and related imprints vary considerably in quality and are not recommended over the Classic Vinyl.

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $35:** The [**Blue Note Classic Vinyl BST 84185**](https://www.discogs.com/release/16953990) — the only broadly recommended modern option for this title. Kevin Gray mastering, Optimal pressing.

**$150–$400:** An original [**New York USA label pressing**](https://www.discogs.com/release/3346498) in honest VG+ or better, with Van Gelder stamp and Plastylite ear confirmed.
