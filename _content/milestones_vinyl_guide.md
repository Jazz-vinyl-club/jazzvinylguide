# Milestones on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Milestones* was recorded across two sessions at Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York City – February 4, 1958 (four of the six tracks) and March 4, 1958 ("Dr. Jekyll" and "Sid's Ahead") – and released September 2, 1958 as Columbia CL 1193. Miles Davis (trumpet, and piano on "Sid's Ahead") leads a sextet of Julian "Cannonball" Adderley (alto sax), John Coltrane (tenor sax), Red Garland (piano), Paul Chambers (bass), and Philly Joe Jones (drums). George Avakian produced – his last Miles Davis session before handing the reins to Columbia's jazz A&R for *Kind of Blue* the following year – and Harold Chapman engineered.

*Milestones* was recorded and released in mono only, and – like several period Blue Note titles – later saw an "Electronically Rechanneled for Stereo" reissue that is honestly labeled as such but is not a real stereo mix. What makes this title's stereo story unusually interesting is that a *genuine* stereo mix does exist: in 2000, Sony's Mark Wilder built one from the original multi-track session tapes for a Miles Davis/John Coltrane box set, and that legitimate mix – not the 1967 fake – is what modern stereo reissues use.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Columbia six-eye mono, CL 1193](https://www.discogs.com/release/17262070-Miles-Davis-Milestones) – red six-eye labels, Columbia 10th-anniversary inner sleeve on true first pressings.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Sony Japan mono reissue (2021)](https://www.discogs.com/release/20649181-Miles-Davis-Milestones) – domestic cutting and pressing at Sony Music Solutions' Shizuoka plant, widely regarded as the best-pressed vinyl in the world today.

**Best-value modern:** [2013 Record Store Day mono reissue](https://www.discogs.com/release/4493829-Miles-Davis-Milestones) – Kevin Gray, RTI, six-eye replica labels, sourced from an unplayed 1958 safety tape.

## Recording history and tape provenance

By early 1958, Miles Davis's group was in transition. Cannonball Adderley had just joined, expanding the working quintet with Coltrane into a sextet, and Red Garland's tenure was visibly winding down – he's absent from "Sid's Ahead" entirely, with Davis himself filling in on piano. The sessions came almost exactly a year before *Kind of Blue*, and the title track is now recognized as an early, decisive step toward the modal approach that album would crystallize: built on two scales rather than a chord progression, "Milestones" pointed a different direction than the bebop changes Davis had spent the decade mastering.

The album gathered six tracks from two dates. February 4 yielded "Milestones" (mislabeled "Miles" on the original LP's back cover and labels, a printing error corrected only decades later), "Billy Boy" (a trio feature for Garland, Chambers, and Jones with no horns), "Straight, No Chaser," and "Two Bass Hit." March 4 added "Dr. Jekyll" – actually a mistitled version of Jackie McLean's composition "Dr. Jackle," a mix-up that persisted on labels and sleeves for decades – and "Sid's Ahead," essentially a group improvisation over "Walkin'" changes that Davis and the band worked out on the spot, with Davis on piano in Garland's absence.

George Avakian, who had signed Davis to Columbia and produced everything from *'Round About Midnight* onward, produced this as his final Miles Davis session; Teo Macero would take over the following year, though not for *Kind of Blue* itself (produced by Irving Townsend). The album was inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame on February 8, 2004.

## Stereo note

*Milestones* was recorded and issued in mono only in 1958 – Columbia's jazz output hadn't yet standardized simultaneous stereo releases. In 1967, Columbia issued a "stereo" pressing, CS 9428, explicitly labeled on the jacket as "Electronically Rechanneled for Stereo": a fold-out processing trick applied to the mono master, not a real stereo mix, and one collector describes the effect as adding "reverb and delay on the og mono track for added ambience." It's a period curiosity, not a sound-quality upgrade.

The more interesting twist: a genuine stereo mix of this material does exist. For the 2000 box set *Miles Davis & John Coltrane: The Complete Columbia Recordings 1955–1961*, Sony engineer Mark Wilder built a real stereo mix from the surviving multi-track session tapes rather than reprocessing the mono master. This legitimate mix – not the 1967 fake stereo – is the source for Mobile Fidelity's 2023 SuperVinyl stereo edition (MFSL1-528), making it a genuinely different listening experience from either the mono original or the old CS 9428.

## Original Columbia pressings (1958)

**Mono only: Columbia CL 1193**, red six-eye labels. True first pressings can be identified by label design details – artist name width close to the album title's width, centered (not left-justified) track listings, and credits placed below rather than beside each title – and were originally packaged with a Columbia inner sleeve commemorating the 10th anniversary of the LP format. Several six-eye label sub-variants exist across the pressing run (Hollywood and Bridgeport plant pressings both circulated), all sonically comparable since they share the original metalwork.

Because the album was pressed in substantial quantities and reissued steadily through the 1960s and 70s, clean six-eye originals remain more attainable than many contemporaneous jazz titles – typically $60–$200 depending on condition and label variant, with exceptional near-mint copies commanding more.

## The reissue hierarchy

### Sony Japan mono reissue (2021) – the current reference

**Cat. (Sony Japan)** | Format: Mono, 180g, six-eye label replica, tip-on jacket, 2021 | **Domestic cutting and domestic pressing at Sony Music Solutions' Shizuoka plant, sourced from the original master.** Part of a broader Sony Japan vinyl series revisiting Davis's Columbia catalog, cut and pressed entirely in Japan rather than licensed out. Collectors and reviewers consistently rate the Shizuoka plant's surface quality above RTI, QRP, and MoFi's SuperVinyl – "quieter than dead quiet" is a common description – and this title's tip-on packaging replicates the original faithfully, down to reproducing the "Miles" labeling error rather than silently correcting it. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/20649181-Miles-Davis-Milestones) **$50–$90.**

### Mobile Fidelity mono, MFSL 1-374 (2013)

**Cat. MFSL 1-374** | Format: Mono, 180g, numbered, 2013 | **Mastered by Krieg Wunderlich at Mobile Fidelity, 1/4", 15ips analog master tape via DSD64, on the Gain 2 Ultra Analog System.** Reception is genuinely split: some listeners find it clean and engaging, while others – including at least one prominent reviewer – consider it thin compared to the original tapes as heard on the Mosaic box set, and prefer the 2013 Record Store Day mono below for a warmer, more airy presentation. Worth comparing before assuming it's the automatic top pick. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/4716235-Miles-Davis-Milestones) **$60–$120 secondary.**

## Japanese pressings

Beyond the current Sony Japan Shizuoka pressing above, CBS/Sony issued *Milestones* in Japan on more than one occasion, including a vintage pressing (catalog SONP-50087) and an entry in CBS/Sony's "AP" audiophile half-speed series from the late 1970s/early 1980s – both well-regarded among Japanese-pressing collectors, though neither is as easy to source today as the current Sony Japan reissue. **$40–$90 with obi, when found.**

---

## Pressings to avoid

**CS 9428 / Columbia Jazz Masterpieces CJ 40837:** Not to be avoided outright – both are legitimate period Columbia releases – but buyers should understand what they're getting. CS 9428 (1967) is explicitly rechanneled fake stereo, not a real stereo mix. The 1987–88 Columbia Jazz Masterpieces reissue (CJ 40837, later CDs also drew on this same digital remaster) is digitally sourced and reviews are mixed – reasonable for casual listening, not a substitute for the mono original or the modern analog-sourced reissues above. Some individual copies from the later CD/digital lineage have also been reported to combine mono and stereo channels oddly; if buying either of these secondhand, confirm which release you're actually getting before paying originals-adjacent prices.

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $45:** The [**2013 Record Store Day mono reissue**](https://www.discogs.com/release/4493829-Miles-Davis-Milestones) – Kevin Gray mastering, RTI pressing, genuine tape provenance, and consistently the best per-dollar recommendation for this title.

**$50–$90:** The [**Sony Japan mono reissue (2021)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/20649181-Miles-Davis-Milestones) for the finest pressing quality currently available, or the [**MoFi SuperVinyl stereo (MFSL1-528)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/29189929-Miles-Davis-Milestones) if you specifically want to hear the legitimate 2000 stereo mix.

**$60–$200:** A clean original [**Columbia six-eye mono, CL 1193**](https://www.discogs.com/release/17262070-Miles-Davis-Milestones) – genuinely attainable for this title relative to other late-1950s Davis originals.
