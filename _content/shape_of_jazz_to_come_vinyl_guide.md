# The Shape of Jazz to Come on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*The Shape of Jazz to Come* was recorded on May 22, 1959 at Radio Recorders in Hollywood, California – a single session engineered by Bones Howe and produced by Nesuhi Ertegun for Atlantic Records. The quartet of Ornette Coleman (alto saxophone), Don Cherry (cornet), Charlie Haden (bass), and Billy Higgins (drums) recorded the album that would permanently alter the direction of jazz. Coleman had originally wanted to title the album *Focus on Sanity* after its fourth track; Ertegun chose the final title to signal its ambition.

The stereo presentation is musically essential for this album in a way that differs from most jazz of the era. Coleman hard-pans the horns – Ornette and Cherry on opposite extremes of the stereo field – creating the dramatic shock of "Lonely Woman," where bass and drums establish an atmospheric stillness before the two horns blaze in from hard left and right. The mono collapses this to a more unified ensemble picture. Both formats have strong advocates; unlike some Atlantic stereo albums of the period, the hard panning here was clearly intentional and is intrinsic to the music's drama.

The Atlantic tape library was largely preserved through Warner's acquisition and has remained accessible. No catastrophic tape-loss event comparable to the ABC/Impulse fire applies here, meaning reissues have drawn from genuine first-generation source material. The three best modern options are the ORG 45rpm 2LP (Bernie Grundman, Pallas), the Speakers Corner 180g (Kevin Gray, Pallas), and the VMP Classics mono 180g (Ryan K. Smith, RTI). The consensus leans toward the VMP for listeners who prefer mono and the ORG for stereo.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original stereo Atlantic SD 1317 bullseye label](https://www.discogs.com/release/488973-Ornette-Coleman-The-Shape-Of-Jazz-To-Come) – green, blue and black bullseye, 157 West 57 Street address; the original dramatic stereo presentation.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [ORG Music ORGM-1099 45rpm 2LP](https://www.discogs.com/release/4427710-Ornette-Coleman-The-Shape-Of-Jazz-To-Come) – Bernie Grundman, AAA, Pallas; the audiophile stereo benchmark.

**Best-value modern:** [VMP Classics VMP-C058 mono 180g](https://www.discogs.com/release/24779189-Ornette-Coleman-The-Shape-Of-Jazz-To-Come) – Ryan K. Smith, AAA, RTI; mono from the original tapes; widely praised as the definitive modern pressing.


## Recording history and tape provenance

*The Shape of Jazz to Come* was recorded entirely on **May 22, 1959** at **Radio Recorders** in Hollywood, California. Engineer **Bones Howe** captured the session; producer **Nesuhi Ertegun** supervised. Personnel: Ornette Coleman (alto saxophone on a white plastic Grafton alto – which contributed to the distinctive edge of his tone), Don Cherry (cornet), Charlie Haden (bass), Billy Higgins (drums).

The album was Coleman's debut on Atlantic, arranged through pianist John Lewis of the Modern Jazz Quartet, who had heard Coleman in Los Angeles and facilitated both a deal with Atlantic and Coleman's attendance at the 1959 Lenox School of Jazz. The album was released in November 1959, timed to coincide with Coleman's quartet beginning a landmark residency at the Five Spot Café in Manhattan – an engagement that immediately polarised the New York jazz establishment and generated intense controversy.

The recording has no piano or guitar – the "chordal instrument" that normally anchors the harmonic framework. Each piece uses a brief melodic theme (the "head"), followed by improvisation that abandons chord structure but retains melodic and rhythmic coherence, then a return to the theme. This was new: it was not the absence of structure but a different kind of structure, one Coleman called "harmolodics." The result was simultaneously more avant-garde than hard bop and more accessible than the fully free improvisation that would follow later in the decade.

**The cover photograph** is by Lee Friedlander – a close-up of Coleman holding his white Grafton saxophone. The original cover is distinctive; reissues have handled it with varying degrees of fidelity. [Fred Kaplan (Stereophile)](https://www.stereophile.com/content/ithe-shape-jazz-comei-45rpm) specifically criticised the ORG edition's cover reproduction: "The ORG cover turns his rich brown face into a splotchy red. ORG's back cover is a cheesy disaster."

## Original US Atlantic pressings (1959)

Released in both mono and stereo simultaneously:

- **Mono: Atlantic 1317**
- **Stereo: Atlantic SD 1317** (the "SD" prefix denoting Atlantic stereo)

### The Atlantic bullseye label

The 1317/SD 1317 catalogue number sits in the zone where Atlantic was using its distinctive **bullseye** (sometimes called "pinwheel") label design – green, blue, and black concentric rings around the spindle hole. [LJC's Atlantic first pressing guide](https://londonjazzcollector.wordpress.com/record-labels-guide/5-atlantic/atlantic-first-pressing-guide-label-transitions/) documents that the bullseye was used selectively for certain titles in this range, not as a universal replacement for the black label.

**First pressing stereo (SD 1317) label characteristics:**
- Green, blue and black bullseye design
- "ATLANTIC" in block letters
- Address footer: **"Atlantic Recording Corporation, 157 West 57 Street, New York 19, New York"** on the sleeve rear
- Matrix: **AVCO ST-A-59176** (Side A) and **AVCO ST-A-59175** (Side B) – hand-etched in the deadwax; the "AVCO" prefix indicates Atlantic's pressing arrangement
- No barcode

**Known error:** Discogs documents that the first pressing has a known label swap on some copies – the Side A label appears on Side B and vice versa. This is a first-pressing artefact, not an indication of a later pressing.

**First pressing mono (1317):** Black label, same address footer. The mono is less pressed-upon by collectors than the stereo given the album's musical case for stereo imaging, but commands collector interest in its own right.

### The stereo vs. mono question for this album

Unlike most jazz of this era where the stereo is an afterthought and mono was the primary format, *The Shape of Jazz to Come* has a genuine stereo case that derives from the music itself. The extreme left-right panning of Coleman and Cherry on "Lonely Woman" – horn blaring in from the extreme left after the bass-and-drums intro – is one of the most kinetic sonic moments in jazz. In stereo it is physical, almost shocking. In mono, it becomes a more blended ensemble statement.

[Hi-Fi AF on the stereo experience](https://www.hifiaf.com/artsi-shape-of-jazz/): "Is there a more perfect moment in music than when, after some atmospheric scene-setting by Charlie Haden on bass and Billy Higgins on drums, Ornette and Cherry come in HORNS BLARING on the extreme right and left, blasting out the immortal riff to 'Lonely Woman'?... Fond as I am of mono, especially for jazz of this era, this is an experience that you need to have in stereo."

The counterargument – well made by Discogs reviewers – is that the panning is artificially wide even for stereo, creating an imbalanced ensemble impression on some systems, and that the mono unifies the quartet into a more cohesive musical whole. The VMP Classics mono is the best current argument for the mono presentation.

### Later Atlantic label variants (1960s–70s)

**Plum/orange white fan label (from ~1962):** The bullseye was replaced by the plum/orange label with a white fan design as Atlantic's main release label. Copies of 1317/SD 1317 in this format are second pressings; still excellent sounding and sonically very close to originals according to the Hi-Fi AF review: "it's hard to imagine it [the original] could sound any better than my 1966 US reissue." These trade for $30–$80 in clean condition.

**Green/red Warner-era labels (1970s):** Later pressings on thin vinyl; [Fred Kaplan](https://www.stereophile.com/content/ithe-shape-jazz-comei-45rpm) noted these were the labels ORG chose to reproduce on their reissue despite the availability of original artwork – the dreadful 1970s Atlantic pressings. Sonically inferior; avoid.

### Market values

NM **stereo SD 1317 bullseye label** first press: **$200–$500**. NM **mono 1317 bullseye**: **$100–$250**. NM **plum/orange 1960s repress**: **$30–$80**. Condition is decisive – the hard panning means any surface noise is immediately audible against the quiet backdrop of the opening of "Lonely Woman."

## The reissue hierarchy

### Rhino 180g 33rpm (c.2008) — a sleeper

A standard-weight 180g reissue on the Rhino/Atlantic catalogue, which [Kaplan praised before the ORG existed](https://www.stereophile.com/content/ithe-shape-jazz-comei-45rpm): "sounds in every way better than the original pressing, which itself sounds quite good." He later revised upward to the ORG, but the Rhino remains a decent entry-level pressing. One Hi-Fi AF reviewer who owns both a 1966 Atlantic and the Speakers Corner considers the 60s Atlantic superior to the Kevin Gray cut; others disagree. Cheap at **$15–$30** used.

### ORG Music ORGM-1099 45rpm 2LP (2013) — the stereo audiophile benchmark

**Cat. ORGM-1099** | Format: 2×180g 45rpm, gatefold. **Mastered by Bernie Grundman, AAA, from the original master tapes.** Pressed at **Pallas** (Germany). The first serious audiophile treatment of this title.

[Fred Kaplan (Stereophile)](https://www.stereophile.com/content/ithe-shape-jazz-comei-45rpm): "Double all that, plus some, for the ORG 45. You hear more of the music's rhythmic subtlety, the slightest shift in dynamics: the finger-flexing on the neck of Charlie Haden's bass, the full polyrhythms (and more of the kick drum) on Billy Higgins' trapset, the sputtering of Don Cherry's pocket-trumpet mouthpiece, and the sharp beauty of Ornette's white alto sax."

[Vinyl Gourmet](https://www.vinylgourmet.com): "Audiophile remaster from Original Master Tapes by Bernie Grundman sounds stunning!"

Multiple Discogs owners with the ORG: "Sound is stellar, it is truly outstanding. Way beyond all expectations." One reviewer who has the ORG, the Speakers Corner, and the VMP: "I prefer the VMP slightly more... between Ryan's cut and the mono presentation that's the copy I need in my rotation over this Kevin Gray cut."

**Caveat:** Pressing quality on the ORG is variable. Multiple Discogs reports describe non-fill defects specifically on Side 3, affecting left-channel detail. Inspect carefully before purchase. Cover reproduction is poor relative to the original. **$80–$150** on the secondary market.

### Speakers Corner SD 1317 180g 33rpm (2021) — Kevin Gray stereo

**Cat. Atlantic SD 1317 / Speakers Corner** | Format: single 180g LP at 33⅓, Pallas pressing. **Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio (KPG), AAA, from the original analog master tapes.**

**Known issue:** The entire first pressing of this edition has swapped labels — the Side A label is printed on Side B and vice versa. This is documented by multiple Discogs owners and mirrors the same error in the 1959 original. Sonically it has no effect; it is a pressing/labelling error only.

Discogs reviewers are overwhelmingly positive: "Perfect record, mix, master and pressing. One of the best sounding records in my collection." "Kevin Gray, AAA and Pallas a winning combination." One owner who prefers the VMP: "Incredible mastering and pressing all around. Absolutely loved this and was my go to for a while. But I have to say I prefer the VMP slightly more."

One dissenting voice from Hi-Fi AF, comparing with a 1966 Atlantic: "My reissue definitely sounds better to my ear than the recent Kevin Gray Speaker's Corner reissue, which is terrific but a little too robust, lacking the sharp attack of a copy closer in time to the original tape." This is a minority view but consistent with some system-dependent responses to Kevin Gray's characteristic warmth. **~$35–$50 new** (widely available).

### VMP Classics VMP-C058 mono 180g (2022) — the current consensus best

**Cat. VMP-C058** | Format: single 180g LP at 33⅓, tip-on sleeve. **Mastered by Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound, AAA, from the original mono master tapes.** Pressed at **RTI**. Released as the VMP Classics Record of the Month for October 2022. Includes a listening notes booklet by Marcus J. Moore and a VMP obi strip.

Matrix: VMP-C058-A / VMP-C058-B, runout confirms "RKS STERLING."

This pressing is mono – a deliberate curatorial choice by VMP that has generated strong community consensus. Discogs reviewers: "Insanely good mono repressing." "When it comes to jazz records pre-65, mono is almost always the way to go. Stereo cuts usually have one channel with drums and one channel with all the melodic instruments and the balance is always off. This mono record sounds superb! Punchy, dynamic and airy." "What a brilliant pressing! This is how a record should sound." One owner who has both the Speakers Corner stereo and the VMP mono gave away the Speakers Corner: "it just wasn't getting any time on my deck when the VMP was sitting right next to it."

The case for the VMP is not purely sonic – it is also curatorial. The mono was the primary format in 1959 (the year Ornette recorded; the hard-left/right panning of the stereo reflects early stereo production choices that were not universal). Ryan K. Smith's mastering from the mono tapes gives the quartet a coherent ensemble presence that the stereo's hard panning sacrifices. **Out of print as a VMP subscription exclusive; $50–$100 on the secondary market.**

## Japanese pressings

Atlantic titles from the late 1950s and early 1960s received careful treatment from Japanese pressing plants throughout the 1970s and 1980s. Toshiba-EMI and Victor pressed Atlantic catalogue in Japan; these are generally praised on Discogs for their dead-quiet vinyl and controlled mastering. One Discogs owner comparing a Japanese pressing to the Speakers Corner: "This Japanese one sounds more alive, 'hot', and engaged. Dead silent vinyl." Specific Japanese pressings of SD 1317 are in the **$40–$100 NM with obi** range.

---

## Pressings to avoid

**EU grey-market editions (DOL, Waxtime):** *The Shape of Jazz to Come* is widely bootlegged in the EU budget market. DOL and Waxtime editions circulate heavily; these are not licensed from Atlantic/Rhino/Warner and are digitally sourced. The Atlantic catalogue is licensed through Rhino/Warner; any pressing without mastering credits and "Under license from Warner Music Group" language is suspect. One notable exception: some Waxtime copies have surprisingly good pressing quality, and several Discogs reviewers mention them positively. However their tape provenance remains unverifiable and their status as legitimate releases is doubtful.

**1970s Warner/Atlantic green-red label pressings:** Thin vinyl, inferior mastering from this era. Kaplan called them "dreadful."

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $50:** The **Speakers Corner 180g** at ~$40 new is the obvious recommendation – Kevin Gray, AAA, Pallas, stereo, widely available. If you prefer mono, hunt for the **VMP Classics** on the secondary market; it appears regularly in the $50–$80 range and is worth the premium.

**$80–$150:** The **ORG Music 45rpm 2LP** is the stereo audiophile statement. Buy from a reputable dealer who has inspected for non-fill defects on Side 3; the pressing quality is inconsistent enough that inspection matters.

**$100–$300:** A **clean 1960s plum/orange Atlantic repress** gives you the original tape transfer with period character – still excellent sounding and with authentic cover art. A clean NM original **bullseye stereo SD 1317** sits at the top of this range.

**The practical recommendation:** Buy the **Speakers Corner** for stereo at ~$40 or the **VMP Classics mono** at ~$50–$80 secondhand. Both are AAA cuts from the original tapes, both on high-quality pressing plants, and together they cover the album's two legitimate listening presentations. The ORG is the choice for committed stereo audiophiles who don't mind secondary-market prices and potential pressing variability.
