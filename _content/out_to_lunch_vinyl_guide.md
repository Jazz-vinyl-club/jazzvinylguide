# Out to Lunch! on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Out to Lunch!* was recorded on **February 25, 1964**, at Van Gelder Studio in Englewood Cliffs, New Jersey. Personnel: Eric Dolphy (alto saxophone, flute, bass clarinet), Freddie Hubbard (trumpet), Bobby Hutcherson (vibraphone), Richard Davis (bass), Tony Williams (drums). Produced by Alfred Lion. Released in April 1964; Dolphy died four months later in Berlin on June 29, 1964, making this his last studio album as a leader. The album stands among the most adventurous records in the Blue Note catalogue — Dolphy's three horns, Hutcherson's vibraphone, and the extraordinary interaction of Williams and Davis create a sound entirely unlike anything else from the period.

The mono presentation is the studio-intended version and the one Van Gelder optimised for. Original New York USA pressings with the Van Gelder stamp and Plastylite ear are the collector's target. The Tone Poet reissue (2022) brought a fresh Kevin Gray cut from the original mono tape to the widest audience; Music Matters (2014) had done the same work earlier in a more lavish package.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Blue Note BLP 4163 mono, New York USA label](https://www.discogs.com/release/4521399-Eric-Dolphy-Out-To-Lunch) – deep groove, Van Gelder stamp, Plastylite ear.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Blue Note Tone Poet 180g mono (2022)](https://www.discogs.com/release/23196534-Eric-Dolphy-Out-To-Lunch) – Kevin Gray, AAA, RTI, gatefold.

**Best-value modern:** [Blue Note Classic Vinyl 180g (2020)](https://www.discogs.com/release/16219694-Eric-Dolphy-Out-To-Lunch) – Kevin Gray, Optimal, 180g, around $25.


## Recording history and tape provenance

The session was a departure from Blue Note's typical hard bop vocabulary even by 1964 standards. Dolphy had been developing his multi-instrument approach throughout the Coltrane years and this session — his first for Blue Note as leader — distilled everything: free melodic invention, unusual timbres, and a rhythm section that had no precedent. Tony Williams, just 18 years old at the time, plays here with a rhythmic freedom that anticipates the mid-1960s avant-garde rather than looking back to the post-bop drumming of the early 1960s.

The Reid Miles cover design — featuring a "Will Be Back" clock sign with no hands — is one of the most celebrated in jazz, and was conceived in perfect sympathy with the album's refusal to be pinned to a groove or metre.

**Mono vs stereo:** Out to Lunch! is a title where the mono is strongly preferred by collectors. The stereo spread — Dolphy far left, Hutcherson far right — is typical of 1964 Blue Note stereo and creates an artificial separation that works against the ensemble's internal logic. Van Gelder's mono cut, with the musicians densely packed in a single soundfield, better captures the music's claustrophobic intensity. A stereo copy is not sonically bad, but the mono is the recommended presentation.

**Tape provenance:** The original Alfred Lion–era tapes are in the Blue Note/Universal archive. Both Music Matters (2014) and Tone Poet (2022) worked from the original mono master tape.

## Original US Blue Note pressings

**Catalogue: BLP 4163** (mono) / **BST 84163** (stereo)

### First mono pressing – New York USA label

The first pressing of BLP 4163 carries the **"Blue Note Records Inc. • New York USA"** address on the labels. This was the label used from late 1963 through 1967. Authentication points:

- **Plastylite ear** etched in the deadwax
- **VAN GELDER** stamped in the deadwax
- Deep groove labels (on both sides for the earliest copies; single-sided deep groove on slightly later pressings)
- Matrix: **BNL-4163-A RVG P** / **BNL-4163-B RVG P** (approximate; variations exist)
- Laminated sleeve (front only on many copies)

The first stereo (BST 84163) follows the same pattern. Collectors seeking the original stereo should verify the VAN GELDER stamp is present and that it reads "Blue Note Records Inc. • New York USA" rather than "A Division of Liberty Records."

**NM mono originals: $300–$700. VG+: $150–$350.**

### Liberty-era pressings (1966–1970)

Pressed after Blue Note's acquisition by Liberty Records. Labels read "A Division of Liberty Records Inc." Van Gelder stamps remain on many; these are sonically adequate but not the first-choice collector pressing. **$40–$100 VG+.**

## The reissue hierarchy

### Music Matters 33rpm 180g mono (2014) – the pre-Tone Poet benchmark

**Cat. MM33-84163** | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓, gatefold tip-on jacket. **Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio, AAA, from the original mono master tape. Pressed at QRP.** Widely praised as the finest available version before the Tone Poet series. Now out of print; secondary market prices have risen. **$150–$250 secondary.**

### Blue Note Tone Poet 180g mono (2022) – the current definitive reissue

**Cat. B0035204-01** | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓, mono, deluxe gatefold tip-on jacket. **Produced by Joe Harley. Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio, AAA, from the original mono master tape. Pressed at RTI.** The Tone Poet series represents the most comprehensive modern reissue programme for Blue Note, and Out to Lunch! is one of its highlights. Reviewers consistently describe the sonic improvement over earlier reissues: the bass clarinet has genuine weight and chest, the vibraphone resonates naturally, and Tony Williams's cymbal work is reproduced with uncanny detail. **$45–$65 new.**

### Blue Note Classic Vinyl 180g (2020) – the recommended budget option

**Cat. BLP 4163** | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓. **Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio. Pressed at Optimal in Germany.** The Classic Vinyl series delivers Kevin Gray's work at an accessible price point. Some QC variation has been reported across the series; the Out to Lunch! pressing is generally well-regarded. **$20–$30 new.**

### Japanese King GXF-3009 (1977) – the vintage alternative

**Cat. GXF-3009** | Format: 33⅓, single LP, Japan. The Japanese King pressing of Out to Lunch! is among the most praised vintage alternatives. Pressed from licensed metalwork (not direct US metal, but high-quality Japanese plating from tape), the King copy is known for its quiet surfaces, careful vinyl formulation, and the warmth typical of late-1970s Japanese jazz pressings. Distinct from the US original but a legitimate audiophile alternative. **$60–$150 NM.**

## Pressings to avoid

**Stereo originals for listening preference:** The stereo is not bad — it is an authentic Blue Note period pressing — but the extreme left-right separation works against the music. If you are buying for listening rather than collecting, mono is the recommendation.

**Later Blue Note reissues (1970s–1990s):** Various Blue Note/EMI reissues from this period are cut from dubs or digital transfers and should be avoided by the serious listener. The Classic Vinyl at $25 is a better choice than any of these.

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $30:** The [**Blue Note Classic Vinyl BLP 4163**](https://www.discogs.com/release/16219694) — Kevin Gray mastering, Optimal pressing, mono. The correct entry point.

**$45–$65:** The [**Blue Note Tone Poet 180g mono**](https://www.discogs.com/release/23196534) — the finest available version at a fair price. This is the recommended purchase for most collectors.

**$150–$250:** The [**Music Matters 33rpm AAA mono**](https://www.discogs.com/release/6192516) if found at a reasonable secondary market price. Excellent, but the Tone Poet is now the primary recommendation.

**$300+:** An original [**New York USA BLP 4163 mono**](https://www.discogs.com/master/32350-Eric-Dolphy-Out-To-Lunch) in honest VG+ or better. Seek the VAN GELDER stamp and Plastylite ear.
