# Saxophone Colossus on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Saxophone Colossus* was recorded in a single session on **June 22, 1956** at Rudy Van Gelder's studio in his parents' living room in Hackensack, New Jersey. This was Van Gelder's home studio before he moved to the purpose-built Englewood Cliffs facility in 1959. Personnel: Sonny Rollins (tenor saxophone), Tommy Flanagan (piano), Doug Watkins (bass), Max Roach (drums). The session was produced by Bob Weinstock for Prestige Records and released later in 1956 on the Prestige yellow label.

The recording is universally regarded as one of Van Gelder's finest from the Hackensack period. RVG hand-etched his initials in the deadwax of every pressing made from his original metalwork; this marking is the primary authentication point for collectors. The mono presentation is the only legitimate format — there is no native stereo recording. "Stereo" versions of this album are fake stereo (electronically rechanneled from the mono master), and they are universally considered inferior; the 1970s rechanneled pressing is widely described as having something "off" about its balance and presentation.

The Analogue Productions 200g mono reissues (2013, 33rpm, Kevin Gray) are by consensus the best-sounding modern versions of this album available, beating both the 45rpm series of the early 2000s and all other reissues in most direct comparisons. The original yellow-label Prestige first pressings sound excellent but are genuinely rare in true NM condition; groove wear from the heavy tonearms of the era is pervasive.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Prestige yellow label 7079, 446 W. 50th Street address](https://www.discogs.com/release/3934932-Sonny-Rollins-Saxophone-Colossus) — PRLP 7079, RVG etched, "AB" (Abbey Manufacturing), first pressing; the standard.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Analogue Productions PRLP 7079 200g mono 33rpm (2013/2022)](https://www.discogs.com/release/4403277-Sonny-Rollins-Saxophone-Colossus) — Kevin Gray, AAA, QRP; 5-star consensus.

**Best-value modern:** [OJC-291 early pressing (1987)](https://www.discogs.com/master/174519-Sonny-Rollins-Saxophone-Colossus) — analog, warm, significantly cheaper; skip the later 2011 OJC repress.


## Recording history and tape provenance

The session was one take after another in Van Gelder's Hackensack living room, using what was at the time advanced microphone technique and recording equipment for a jazz date. Van Gelder's approach to recording the saxophone in this period — close-miked, immediate, with the room's natural acoustics providing air — defined what generations of jazz fans came to understand as the sound of the horn. Max Roach's drum work on "Blue 7" (his extended solo with shimmering ride cymbal) and "Moritat" has been cited repeatedly by engineers and collectors as an exemplary capture of drums in the 1950s.

**The mono question:** This album exists only in mono. Van Gelder was recording in mono at Hackensack; the first stereo recordings at this facility came later. Any "stereo" copy is electronically rechanneled and is not recommended by any serious collector or reviewer.

**The RVG stamp:** Van Gelder hand-etched "RVG" in the deadwax of pressings made from his original metalwork. This appears on: all early US Prestige pressings from Abbey Manufacturing; European pressings made from the same metal parts (Metronome for Denmark, Esquire for the UK, Barclay for France). LJC notes that European pressings made from original metal parts often sound "just as good as the US original" — Prestige licensed by metal rather than tape in the late 1950s and early 1960s.

**Tape provenance:** The Prestige catalogue was eventually acquired by Concord Music Group (via Fantasy/OJC). The original Van Gelder mono tapes are in the Concord archive and have been the source for all credentialed modern reissues. Analogue Productions worked under license from Concord for their Prestige series.

## Original US Prestige pressings

**Catalogue: Prestige PRLP 7079** (mono only)

### First pressing — yellow label, 446 W. 50th Street

The definitive first pressing uses the **yellow Prestige label** with the **446 W. 50th Street, New York** address. Key characteristics:
- **Yellow label** with black text
- Address: **446 W. 50th St., New York** (the Prestige NYC office address)
- **"Long Playing" and "Unbreakable"** on label
- Catalogue: **PRLP 7079** on label
- **Pressed by Abbey Record Manufacturing Co., Inc.** — indicated by "AB" in the deadwax
- **RVG hand-etched** in the deadwax
- Matrix: **PRLP-7079-A RVG AB** (Side A) and **PRLP-7079-B RVG AB** (Side B)
- Laminated sleeve (front only)

Discogs note: there is a known discrepancy between the address on the back cover (447 W. 50th) and on the label (446 W. 50th). This appears on the first pressing; it is a printing inconsistency, not an indicator of different states.

**On vinyl quality:** LJC and multiple collector accounts note that finding truly NM copies of this pressing is extremely difficult. The heavy tonearms of the late 1950s and 1960s caused groove wear on even lightly-played copies. The AP 200g is often preferred for listening precisely because a NM original is almost never truly NM. **NM originals: $300–$800.** VG+: **$100–$250.**

### Second pressing — Bergenfield, NJ address

Prestige moved its office to Bergenfield, NJ, and the labels changed accordingly. The Bergenfield label copies still have "RVG AB" in the deadwax and are from the same original metalwork. Sonically they are generally considered equivalent to the first pressing. These are significantly more affordable at **$80–$200 NM**.

## European pressings with original metalwork

Prestige licensed its catalogue to European labels in the late 1950s using the original metal stampers rather than tape copies. The result is that European pressings made from "AB" metalwork carry the original Van Gelder cut.

**UK Esquire 32-045:** The [LJC Esquire post](https://londonjazzcollector.wordpress.com/2014/09/21/sonny-rollins-saxophone-colossus-1956-esquire/) is the definitive reference. These have RVG hand-etched, original Prestige matrix PRLP 7079 A/B, Abbey Manufacturing "AB," and a stamper or mother number "1." Sound: "bold and bright as you would expect from this superior recording." A legitimate alternative to the US first press at lower prices.

**Danish Metronome:** Also pressed from original US metal parts. Multiple collectors confirm RVG is hand-etched. Sounds excellent.

**French Barclay:** Also from original metal. Harder to find in good condition.

## The reissue hierarchy

### Analogue Productions PRLP 7079 200g mono 33rpm (2013/2022) — the benchmark

**Cat. PRLP 7079 / 753088707933** | Format: single 200g LP, 33⅓, gatefold tip-on jacket. **Mastered by Kevin Gray at Cohearent Audio (KPG), AAA, from the original analogue mono masters at Concord.** Pressed at **QRP.** Deep-groove label, flat edge profile, faithful to the original format. Machine-numbered in gold ink on back cover.

This is the pressing that effectively ended the collector debate about whether you needed an original to hear the album properly. VinylReviews.com: "Analogue Productions' 33RPM mastering produces an even larger soundstage with better depth. Add to that the fact that the new packaging is far superior to the 45RPM issue, and this one-disc package is Sonny Rollins nirvana." Acoustic Sounds: "The sound on Saxophone Colossus is upfront and immediate." Discogs: "Brilliant mastering, flat and silent vinyl." "One of the best sounding records I have ever heard!" "Absolute Fantastic Pressing and a Live Atmosphere."

The 2022 repress uses 180g rather than 200g vinyl; the matrix is identical and it sounds the same. **$60–$90 new; 200g 2013 original on secondary market: $80–$120.**

### Analogue Productions 45rpm 2LP (early 2000s) — the predecessor

AP released *Saxophone Colossus* as part of their early Prestige 45rpm series before the 33rpm mono series. Kevin Gray mastering, QRP pressing. VinylReviews considered the later 33rpm to surpass it: "the 33RPM AP edition produces an even larger soundstage with better depth." The 45rpm remains excellent but is harder to find and more expensive. **$100–$150 on the secondary market.**

### OJC-291 (1987) — the best budget analogue option

The 1987 OJC reissue is an analogue pressing with "good transparency," "nice crisp key strokes" and "propulsive energy" according to The Broken Record shootout. The 2011 OJC repress (a repress of the DCC version remastered by Steve Hoffman and Kevin Gray) is notably inferior — "spot on tonality" but "the sax is a bit rolled off" and "lifeless and dull." The 1987 OJC is the one to seek; it's the pressing you want in the sub-$30 range. **$15–$30.**

### DCC Compact Classics (1990s) — Steve Hoffman / Kevin Gray

DCC issued an audiophile pressing of *Saxophone Colossus* mastered by Steve Hoffman with lacquers cut by Kevin Gray. The Audiogon thread from 2008 describes the DCC as the best non-original they had heard. The Broken Record shootout found the 1987 OJC competitive but the DCC "the best version I've got by far." Available in limited quantities on the secondary market. **$40–$80 used.**

## Pressings to avoid

**1970s stereo rechanneled Prestige:** The green-label stereo repressings from the 1970s are fake stereo and universally condemned. The bass is "tubey" but the piano lacks weight and the panning creates an incoherent soundstage.

**2011 OJC repress:** Inferior to the 1987 OJC. Often confused with the 1987 version; check the pressing date and matrix.

---

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $30:** The **1987 OJC-291** is the answer — analogue, warm, and affordable.

**$60–$90:** The **AP 200g or 180g mono 33rpm** is the definitive modern choice. Buy new if available; it remains in print periodically.

**$80–$200:** A clean **Bergenfield second pressing** with RVG deadwax gives you the original Van Gelder metalwork at a fraction of the first pressing price.

**$300+:** The **yellow-label 446 W. 50th first pressing** in VG+ or better is the collector's reference. Accept that truly NM copies are almost mythological and factor groove condition into any purchase.
