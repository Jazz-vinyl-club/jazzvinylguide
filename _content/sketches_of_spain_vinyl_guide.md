# Sketches of Spain on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Sketches of Spain* was recorded across three sessions at Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York City – November 15, November 20, 1959, and March 10, 1960 – and released July 18, 1960 as Columbia CL 1480 (mono) / CS 8271 (stereo). Miles Davis (trumpet, flugelhorn) is joined by a 19 to 21-piece orchestra arranged and conducted by Gil Evans, with a rhythm section of Paul Chambers (bass), Jimmy Cobb (drums), and Elvin Jones (percussion). Teo Macero produced – his first Miles Davis session – with Irving Townsend also credited; Fred Plaut engineered.

Unlike the two earlier Davis–Evans collaborations (*Miles Ahead* is mono-only; *Porgy and Bess* is generally considered to sound better in mono), Columbia had true stereo recording fully sorted by this session, and the stereo mix is the one most collectors and mastering engineers treat as definitive. The collector conversation here centers on Bernie Grundman's 1998 Classic Records cut versus Mobile Fidelity's more recent restorations, with the increasingly hard-to-find original six-eye stereo pressing as the vintage benchmark.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original Columbia six-eye stereo, CS 8271](https://www.discogs.com/release/4239636-Miles-Davis-Sketches-Of-Spain) – deep groove, "6-eyes" label; typically $20–50 for a clean copy, up to $200+ near mint.
**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Mobile Fidelity UltraDisc One-Step 33⅓rpm (2025, 65th Anniversary Edition)](https://mofi.com/shop/music/vinyl/miles-davis-sketches-of-spain-numbered-180g-vinyl-lp/) – limited to 5,000 numbered copies, pressed at Fidelity Record Pressing.
**Best-value modern:** [Mobile Fidelity MFSL 1-375 (2013)](https://www.discogs.com/release/4923105-Miles-Davis-Sketches-Of-Spain) – standard MoFi reissue, widely available.

## Recording history and tape provenance

*Sketches of Spain* was the third and final collaboration between Miles Davis and arranger Gil Evans in this particular vein, following *Miles Ahead* (1957) and *Porgy and Bess* (1958–59). Davis brought Evans a recording of the adagio movement from Joaquín Rodrigo's *Concierto de Aranjuez* after hearing it at bassist Joe Mondragon's house; both men were captivated, and Evans built an entire program of Spanish-derived material around it, drawing on flamenco recordings and Alan Lomax's field recordings of Galician and Andalusian folk music that Columbia had previously released on its Masterworks ethnic series.

The sessions unfolded slowly and deliberately, the opposite of the famously spontaneous *Kind of Blue* dates from earlier the same year. On November 15 and 20, 1959, Davis and the orchestra worked through the *Concierto* adagio; journalist Nat Hentoff was present for the November 20 date and later documented the session in detail for *Hi-Fi Stereo Review*, capturing Evans's exacting, ballet-like conducting and Davis's insistence that the melody was strong enough to play almost inaudibly soft. The remaining four pieces – "Will o' the Wisp" (from Manuel de Falla's ballet *El amor brujo*), and the Evans originals "The Pan Piper," "Saeta," and "Solea" – were completed at a March 10, 1960 session. Personnel shifted across dates, with a rotating cast of trumpets, trombones, French horns, tuba, oboe, bassoon, flutes, harp, and bass clarinet; the throughline was Davis's flugelhorn and muted trumpet against Evans's dense, quasi-orchestral voicings.

Nat Hentoff wrote the original liner notes. The album became Davis's first to be certified Gold and later Platinum by the RIAA, reached the upper tier of the Billboard albums chart, and earned Davis and Evans the 1961 Grammy Award for Best Jazz Composition of More Than Five Minutes Duration. It was inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame in 1997. AllMusic's Thom Jurek called it "the most luxuriant and stridently romantic recording Davis ever made," and it has appeared in the 350s on multiple editions of Rolling Stone's 500 Greatest Albums of All Time list. The Penguin Guide to Jazz includes it among Davis's core recordings. In response to critics who questioned whether the record was jazz at all, Davis's reply became one of his most quoted lines: "It's music, and I like it."

The master tapes are held by Sony Music Entertainment (via the Columbia/Legacy catalog) and have been used for every legitimate audiophile reissue below, from Bernie Grundman's 1998 Classic Records cut through Mobile Fidelity's most recent UltraDisc One-Step.

## Stereo note

This is a true stereo recording, not a mono-only or fake-stereo situation. Both CL 1480 (mono) and CS 8271 (stereo) were issued simultaneously in 1960, and unlike some of Davis's earlier Columbia work, the stereo mix is not a compromise – engineer Fred Plaut, fresh off *Kind of Blue*, captured the full orchestra with real channel separation. Most collectors and all of the modern audiophile labels treat stereo as the primary format; Music On Vinyl's 2013 mono pressing (MOVLP692) is the only widely available modern mono option and appeals mainly to those who prefer mono's denser, more centered presentation of large ensembles.

## Original Columbia pressings (1960)

Released simultaneously in both formats:

- **Mono: Columbia CL 1480** – six-eye labels, deep groove; matrix format **XLP 49956** (Side 1) / **XLP 49957** (Side 2).
- **Stereo: Columbia CS 8271** – six-eye labels, deep groove, "6-eyes" logo; matrix format **XSM 49958** (Side 1) / **XSM 49959** (Side 2), with numerous alpha-suffix variants (1AC, 1D, 1G, 1H and others) reflecting repeated repressing through the early 1960s as the album sold steadily. Six-eye labels give way to two-eye "Guaranteed High Fidelity" and later red-label Columbia pressings through the 1960s and 1970s; none of these later variants carry the same collector premium as the six-eye originals, though they remain solid, affordable listening copies.

Because this album sold well and was pressed in large quantities over many years, six-eye originals in clean condition are considerably more affordable than comparably-aged Davis titles like *Kind of Blue* – typically $20–50 for a presentable copy, climbing toward $150–200+ only for pristine, early-matrix examples.

---

## Japanese pressings

Columbia issued this title in Japan early – **SL-1017 (1960)** and **YS-153 / YS 153 (1961, stereo)** – but neither has developed the kind of dedicated audiophile reputation that Japanese pressings of other Davis titles (like *Kind of Blue*'s 18AP series) have earned. They are worth seeking as clean, historically interesting alternatives to a worn US copy, but are not a substitute for the Grundman or MoFi cuts below if sound quality is the priority.

## The reissue hierarchy

### Mobile Fidelity UltraDisc One-Step 33⅓rpm (2025) – the current reference

**65th Anniversary Edition** | Format: Stereo, 180g, one-step, 2025 | **Mastered from the original 1/4", 15ips analog master tape (DSD64 intermediate step, analog console, lathe), pressed at Fidelity Record Pressing.** Limited to 5,000 numbered copies, housed in a deluxe foil-stamped slipcase. Unusually, MoFi chose standard 180g black vinyl over its proprietary SuperVinyl for this release after direct A/B comparison, judging it to sound better on this particular recording. Rated 4.5/5 for music and 4/5 for sonics by The Absolute Sound in its January 2026 issue.

### Mobile Fidelity MFSL 1-375 (2013) – the widely available standard-bearer

**Cat. MFSL 1-375** | Format: Stereo, 180g, numbered, 2013 | **Half-speed mastered by Krieg Wunderlich (assisted by Shawn Britton) from the 1/4", 15ips analog master via DSD64, on MoFi's Gain 2 Ultra Analog System.** Manufactured for MoFi by Sony Music Entertainment rather than pressed at MoFi's usual RTI. Consistently praised in Discogs reviews and widely regarded as an excellent, still-obtainable modern pressing; the only recurring complaint is an occasional non-fill pressing defect reported by a minority of buyers. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/4923105-Miles-Davis-Sketches-Of-Spain)

### Classic Records (1998) – the AAA benchmark

**Cat. CS 8271** | Format: Stereo, 200g Quiex SV-P, 1998 | **Lacquers cut by Bernie Grundman, manufactured and distributed by Classic Records under license from Sony.** Long the default recommendation on audiophile forums and lists (including Analog Planet's "In-Print LPs You Should Own"). Reviewers consistently describe Grundman's cut as more present and immediate than the MoFi versions, though some find the top end – particularly on "The Pan Piper" – edges toward harsh on revealing systems. Out of print since Classic Records closed; secondary market median runs around $100, with a wide range depending on condition. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/2426187-Miles-Davis-Sketches-Of-Spain)

### Music On Vinyl MOVLP692 (2013) – the mono option

**Cat. MOVLP692** | Format: Mono, 180g, 2013 | **Licensed from Sony Music Entertainment, pressed at Record Industry (Netherlands).** The only readily available modern mono pressing; well-reviewed for a dense, balanced presentation, though its exact tape source (analog vs. a high-resolution digital intermediate) isn't clearly documented by the label. A legitimate, affordable way to hear the album in mono rather than a red flag – reviewers consistently rate it positively even when uncertain of its full provenance. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/4798295-Miles-Sketches-Of-Spain)

## Pressings to avoid

A red-vinyl reissue (catalog 350234) circulating from a European public-domain label has drawn repeated, specific complaints on Discogs: reviewers report it was cut directly from a worn, pop- and crackle-riddled record rather than from a proper master of any kind, with no attempt made to clean up the source. This is a step below even the usual "sourced from a CD" public-domain problem and should be avoided regardless of price. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/21035491-Miles-Davis-Sketches-Of-Spain)

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $30:** A clean used [**Columbia CS 8271 six-eye stereo original**](https://www.discogs.com/release/4239636-Miles-Davis-Sketches-Of-Spain) – genuinely attainable for this title, unlike most Davis six-eye originals, and the most direct connection to the 1960 mastering.
**$30–$50:** [**Mobile Fidelity MFSL 1-375 (2013)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/4923105-Miles-Davis-Sketches-Of-Spain) – the best combination of availability and sound quality for most collectors.
**$50–$120:** [**Classic Records 200g SV-P (1998)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/2426187-Miles-Davis-Sketches-Of-Spain) – Bernie Grundman's more forward, immediate cut, now only findable used.
**$120+:** The [**Mobile Fidelity UltraDisc One-Step (2025)**](https://mofi.com/shop/music/vinyl/miles-davis-sketches-of-spain-numbered-180g-vinyl-lp/) for the current state of the art, while numbered copies remain available.
