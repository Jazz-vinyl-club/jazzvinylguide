# A Love Supreme on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*A Love Supreme* was recorded on December 9, 1964 at Van Gelder's studio in Englewood Cliffs – a single afternoon and evening that produced one of the most spiritually intense and commercially successful jazz recordings ever made. The quartet of Coltrane, McCoy Tyner, Jimmy Garrison, and Elvin Jones recorded only to a 2-track stereo master; the mono A-77 is a fold-down, not a discrete mix.

The central fact for collectors is the master tape situation. Around 1971–73, the original Van Gelder reel was discarded after ABC made a dub at Bell Sound. Every serious modern reissue instead draws on a 1965 flat copy Van Gelder struck for EMI Abbey Road – rediscovered in 2002 after sitting untouched for 37 years. This Abbey Road safety is the source for the 2020 Acoustic Sounds Series and the 2023 UHQR, both of which surpass the original stereo pressing in most respects. The original stereo AS-77 also carries a documented 60 Hz hum throughout – baked into Van Gelder's lacquer on the day of cutting – which the mono A-77 and all modern reissues are free of. The 2015 Impulse! GR-155 reissue has its own flaw: a documented right-channel ringing on all copies. Avoid it entirely.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original mono Impulse! A-77](https://www.discogs.com/master/32287-John-Coltrane-A-Love-Supreme) – hum-free, dense and forward, RVG lacquer from the 2-track master; $400–$800 NM.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Analogue Productions UHQR AUHQR 0007-45](https://www.discogs.com/release/27972597-John-Coltrane-A-Love-Supreme) – Ryan K. Smith, AAA, 200g Clarity Vinyl, QRP; the current sonic pinnacle.

**Best-value modern:** [Verve Acoustic Sounds Series B0032077-01](https://www.discogs.com/release/19942006-John-Coltrane-A-Love-Supreme) – Ryan K. Smith, AAA, from the Abbey Road safety tape; ~$40 new, the universal recommendation.


## Recording, mastering and the disappearing master tape

*A Love Supreme* was recorded in a single afternoon and evening on **December 9, 1964** at Rudy Van Gelder's studio in Englewood Cliffs, New Jersey, with Coltrane (tenor), McCoy Tyner (piano), Jimmy Garrison (bass) and Elvin Jones (drums, gong, tympani), produced by Bob Thiele. A second session on December 10 captured three overdubs onto the master: Coltrane's "a love supreme" vocal chant on *Acknowledgement*, a corrective long note on *Resolution*, and sax/bass/drum overdubs on the final thirty seconds of *Psalm*. A second-day sextet session with Archie Shepp and Art Davis produced material later released on the 2015 *Complete Masters* box.

The recording was captured **only to 2-track stereo tape**. Van Gelder had abandoned running parallel mono machines by late 1964; **the mono A-77 is therefore a fold-down of the stereo master, not a discrete mono mix**. Van Gelder cut both stereo and mono lacquers directly from that one stereo reel.

The tape provenance is the single most important technical fact for evaluating pressings. **[Michael Cuscuna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Cuscuna): "I'm ninety-nine percent sure that the master tapes of A Love Supreme were scrapped."** The accepted timeline is that around 1971–73, ABC ordered a recut at Bell Sound Studios in New York because Van Gelder's original master lacked an auto-fade at the end of side one. Bell Sound cut an EQ'd dub, labeled it "master," and the original RVG reel was subsequently discarded. From roughly 1973 onward, virtually every US Impulse! reissue is at least one tape generation removed from Van Gelder's original.

The rescue came in 2002, when Van Gelder retrieved a **flat first-generation copy tape he had struck in April 1965 and shipped to EMI Abbey Road** for the UK HMV pressing. It had sat untouched in the EMI vault for 37 years. **This 1965 Abbey Road safety is the source for every serious modern audiophile reissue: the 2010 Analogue Productions 45rpm, the 2020 Verve Acoustic Sounds Series, the 2023 UHQR, and the 2025 60th-anniversary mono.** [Fremer](https://trackingangle.com/music/a-love-supreme-gets-supreme-analogue-productions-uhqr-release)'s summary: "at some time in the 1970s, ABC made a one-off the master copy and then threw away the master (insane but it was the '70s). So this one-off the master became the master that's now being used."

One further wrinkle defines the pressing hierarchy: Van Gelder's original stereo lacquer contains a **low-level 60 Hz mains hum** running throughout – almost certainly a cable-fault on the day he cut it. The mono cut is hum-free. [Fremer (Tracking Angle)](https://trackingangle.com/music/a-love-supreme-gets-supreme-analogue-productions-uhqr-release): **"If you think an original pressing of A Love Supreme is 'the one', you probably haven't heard it. My copy begins with a 60Hz hum before the music begins and it continues throughout. The original is not the 'holy grail' of A Love Supreme pressings."** This single detail explains why modern reissues from the Abbey Road safety now surpass the original stereo for many listeners.

## Original US Impulse! pressings (1965)

Released **February 1965**. Catalogue numbers firmly established: **A-77 (mono), AS-77 (stereo)**. Note that the outer jacket and spine of stereo copies confusingly read "STEREO A-77" – the "S" appears only on the label itself, tripping up countless sellers. A quarter-track reel was released as **IT-2001**; Capitol Record Club editions carried **SMAS-90215 (stereo)** and **MAS-90215 (mono)**.

The original label is the classic **orange-and-black "!" design** with orange center and black outer ring. **No deep groove** – it had been phased out of Impulse! before A-77's catalogue range, so its presence is not a first-press marker here. The genuine 1965 footer reads "A PRODUCT OF ABC-PARAMOUNT RECORDS, INC. / PRINTED IN USA" with no address line and **no ® trademark marks** flanking the Impulse! logo. The gatefold jacket is **laminated on the outside, matte on the inside**, with a **white spine and black text**.

### Matrix identification

**Mono (A-77):** deadwax reads **A-77-A VAN GELDER LW** (side A) and **A-77-B VAN GELDER LW** (side B). "VAN GELDER" is a rubber stamp; "LW" is hand-etched, standing for Longwear Plating Company. **Stereo (AS-77):** deadwax reads **AS-77·A LW VAN GELDER STEREO** / **AS-77·B VAN GELDER LW STEREO**. The **"STEREO" stamp is the diagnostic marker**; if a purported stereo original lacks it, it is not RVG-cut. Bell Sound-cut copies from 1965 carry "BELL SOUND" in the deadwax – one generation removed and noticeably softer sounding.

The single most reliable first-press indicator is the **inner sleeve**: a genuine 1965 copy carries "ABC-Paramount Records, Inc., **1501 Broadway, New York 36, N.Y.**" listing catalogue only up to **A-50** (*Live at Birdland*). This inner was replaced with a "1330 Avenue of the Americas" version around March/April 1966.

### Market values

NM/NM **mono A-77** originals with Van Gelder deadwax, laminated gatefold and correct inner: **$400–$800**, exceptional copies $1,000–$2,000+. NM/NM **stereo AS-77** originals: **$150–$400** – cheaper because the hum discourages audiophile buyers and stereo was pressed in larger numbers.

## The mono vs. stereo debate, specific to this record

Because the mono is a fold-down of the same 2-track master, the difference is not the mix but the presentation. **Stereo (RVG cut):** Coltrane hard left, Jones's drums hard right, Tyner left-of-center, Garrison right-of-center, nothing in the middle – wide and dramatic, but with the 60 Hz hum baked in, fatiguing on headphones. **Mono:** Coltrane centered, denser and more forward, tympani and gong with more body, and – crucially – **no hum**. [Fred Kaplan (Stereophile, 2025)](https://www.stereophile.com): **"Mono's density, force, and focus fire the music forward like a pistol shot. My UHQR 45rpm stereo version offers bigger images, more detail, and a soundstage, but in stereo, this music floats as much as it punches, more ethereal than visceral. The mono release hits head and body hard."**

For most collectors the mono A-77 is now the more sought-after original – rarer, hum-free, and closer to how the band heard playbacks. For modern reissues, the **2020 Acoustic Sounds Series** and **2023 UHQR** are the reference stereo pressings, both hum-free from the Abbey Road safety. The **2025 60th-anniversary mono** gives modern listeners the mono presentation without hunting for a $600+ original.

## The reissue hierarchy: ABC and MCA eras (1966–1986)

**1966–1968: late orange-and-black represses.** Same A-77/AS-77 numbers; footer changes to "ABC RECORDS, INC. NEW YORK, N.Y. 10019." Many still carry VAN GELDER + LW stamps and sound essentially indistinguishable from firsts.

**1968–1971: black label with orange/red rim.** "abc RECORDS" boxed logo top right, "impulse!" script top left. Still typically carry VAN GELDER STEREO in deadwax; pressed on thinner vinyl but sonically close.

**1972–1974: ℗-year footer.** "℗ 1972 (or 1973) ABC RECORDS, INC." **This is where the Van Gelder stamp disappears** – machine-block matrices indicate Bell Sound recuts from the surviving dub. One Discogs reviewer: *"Considering it's not an RVG pressing, it sounds pretty great – not as bright, crisp, and punchy as RVG, but still beautiful, with plenty of definition that trumps anything post-1973."*

**1973–1975: black "neon impulse!" label; 1975–1979: ABC "green bullseye" label.** Least interesting variants – thin vinyl, non-laminated covers, no Van Gelder metalwork.

**1980: MCA-29017 "black rainbow" label.** The sleeper pick. **[Steve Hoffman](https://www.stevehoffman.tv) has repeatedly endorsed the 1980 MCA cut as "most likely equal to or better than searching for a first Impulse pressing"** – advanced cutting systems, and no pressing after ~1973 was from the true master anyway. Cheap at $25–50.

**1986: MCA-5660 split red/black label.** Digitally remastered by Greg Fulginiti at Artisan Sound Recorders (deadwax: "MCA-4762-ASR-1"). Perfectly acceptable but digital-era cut; purists skip it.

**Critical confirmation:** No Mobile Fidelity Sound Lab (MFSL) release of *A Love Supreme* has ever existed on vinyl, CD, gold CD, or SACD. MFSL never licensed the Impulse! Coltrane catalogue. Any listing claiming one is fraudulent.

## Japanese pressings

**1967 Impulse!/King SR-3006** – earliest Japanese issue; ships with a one-sided promo flexi (P-91) of a 1966 Tokyo TBS interview with Coltrane. Complete copies with obi and flexi: **$300–$800+**. Rare.

**1973 Toshiba-EMI IMP-88060** – Discogs reviewers call it "the best sound for this amazing album" and "hands-down the best I own." **$40–90 NM** with obi.

**1976 Nippon Columbia YP-8527-AI** – strongest reputation of any Japanese pressing despite its budget "ABC/Impulse! Best Collection" positioning. Owners describe it as "warmer, with instruments much better balanced" than the Acoustic Sounds Series. **$50–100 NM** with obi.

**1980 Victor (JVC) MCA/Impulse! VIM-4610** – Steve Hoffman's cited equal-to-original budget pick. Follow-up **VIM-5639** nearly as good. Both **$25–60 NM**.

**Note:** No confirmed SHM-Vinyl release of *A Love Supreme* exists. Treat any such listing with skepticism.

## Modern audiophile reissues (2001–2026)

**Speakers Corner AS-77 (2001)**
Cat. AS-77 / 050155-1. Single 180g LP, orange/black label facsimile, gatefold. Kevin Gray at AcousTech, AAA, from a US analog master tape sourced before the June 2008 UMG fire. Pressed at Pallas (Germany). Long out of print; **$100–250** used. Warm, quiet, well-balanced; Fremer's 2023 verdict finds it "sweet, soft and distant" next to the Sterling Sound cuts, but it was the audiophile reference for a decade.

**Analogue Productions 45rpm 2LP (2010)**
Cat. AIPJ 77, UPC 753088007774. 2×180–200g at 45rpm, gatefold tip-on. Kevin Gray at AcousTech, AAA, from a pre-2008-fire US-side tape. Pressed at QRP. Out of print at **$150–400** used. [Wayne Garcia (*The Absolute Sound*)](https://www.theabsolutesound.com): "As good as the original Impulse pressing sounds, Analogue Productions' release will leave lovers of this record pinned to their seats." Fremer (2023 revisit) finds the piano "muffled, the toms soft and distant, the cymbals lacking shimmer." Still excellent – warmer and less analytical than the later Ryan Smith cuts.

**Verve/Impulse! Acoustic Sounds Series 33⅓ (2020) – the mid-price reference**
Cat. **B0032077-01** (label cat# on Impulse!/Verve/UMe), UPC **602508889288**. Note: retailers including Acoustic Sounds use the SKU "AIMP 7701," which does not appear on the record itself. Single 180g LP at 33⅓, Stoughton tip-on laminated gatefold. **Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound, AAA**, from the 1965 Abbey Road safety tape. Pressed at QRP. List **$38.98–$39.95**. Early press (r16026339) had a Side II label misprint (reading "Resolution" instead of "Psalm" for Part IV) later corrected in a second pressing (r19942006). [Fremer (Analog Planet, Nov 2020)](https://www.analogplanet.com): **"Anyone who tells you the original pressing bests this new one simply has not heard either! Ryan's new cut is a 100% top to bottom success."** [Joseph Taylor (SoundStage!Ultra)](https://www.soundstageultra.com/index.php/features-menu/recording-of-the-month-menu/999-john-coltrane-a-love-supreme): "closer to hearing what occurred in the studio."

**Analogue Productions UHQR 45rpm Clarity Vinyl 2LP box (2023) – the current sonic pinnacle**
Cat. **AUHQR 0007-45**, UPC **753088074578**. 2×200g Clarity Vinyl at 45rpm, deluxe dowel-spine box with 12-page Ashley Kahn booklet and hand-signed inspection certificate. **Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound, AAA**, from the same 1965 Abbey Road safety tape as the 2020 cut. Hand-pressed at QRP. Limited to 10,000 copies. Launched $149.99; now **$200+** on resale. [Fred Kaplan (Stereophile)](https://www.stereophile.com): **"a wonder, better-sounding than any previous edition, including the original pressing."** [Fremer (Tracking Angle)](https://trackingangle.com/music/a-love-supreme-gets-supreme-analogue-productions-uhqr-release): "The difference between this version and every other one... is not subtle... 'insanely better' and the proverbial 'window into the studio.'" A dissenting minority argues the 2010 Kevin Gray 45 is warmer and more comfortable on some systems.

**A Love Supreme: The Complete Masters 3LP box (2015)**
Cat. B0023911-01, UPC **602547536044**. Three LPs in gatefold with 20-page booklet. **Digitally sourced** (Kevin Reeves remaster). LP1 carries the studio album; LPs 2–3 collect alternate takes and the December 10 sextet session with Shepp and Davis. Essential for the alternate material; sonically the main-album LP is inferior to the 2020 Acoustic Sounds cut. **$60–80** new.

**Impulse! GR-155 single-LP reissue (2015) – AVOID**
Mastered from Erick Labson's 1995 digital remaster, lacquers cut at The Mastering Lab. **All copies exhibit a low-level right-channel background ringing** throughout – well-documented on Discogs, Steve Hoffman Forums, and SoundStage!Ultra. Skip in favour of the Acoustic Sounds Series.

**A Love Supreme: Live in Seattle 2LP (2021)**
Cat. B0034291-01, UPC **602438499984**. The October 2, 1965 Penthouse Club performance with Pharoah Sanders and Carlos Ward. Mastered by Kevin Reeves from a 50-year-old amateur tape. Historically essential; sonically compromised – Coltrane buried in the mix. Not a substitute for the studio album. **$34** new.

**2025 60th-Anniversary Mono Edition**
UPC **602478371004**. Single 180g mono LP, gatefold tip-on. **Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound, AAA**, cut from the original analog mono tape – the first new mono cut in over fifty years. Pressed at RTI. List **~$40**. Reviews consistently positive; the answer for listeners who want mono presentation without spending $600+ on an original A-77.

**Supersense Archival Tape Edition No. 3 (2021)**
Limited 999-unit single-sided lacquer edition cut in real time onto Japanese lacquer blanks from a claimed original US master tape. ~€500–€1,000. Plays quietly, requires very high gain, wears rapidly. Interesting curio, not for daily use.

## Buyer's guide by budget

For **under $30**, the pragmatic choice is a clean **MCA 1980 yellow-label reissue (MCA-29017)** – Steve Hoffman's endorsed budget pick – or a used copy of the Japanese Victor VIM-4610 if you can source one from Japan. Both give you a decent all-analog presentation without spending serious money. Skip the 2015 Impulse! GR-155 entirely despite its cheap availability; the documented right-channel ringing makes it actively worse than a budget MCA reissue.

At **$30–75**, the answer is simple and near-unanimous: **the 2020 Verve Acoustic Sounds Series 33⅓ (B0032077-01, ~$40)**. It is the best-value pressing in the catalogue's history and the universal recommendation for any serious listener who doesn't want to pay audiophile premiums. If mono is your preference, add the **2025 60th-anniversary mono edition (~$40)**. Both are Ryan K. Smith AAA cuts from the Abbey Road safety tape. A clean Japanese Toshiba IMP-88060 or Nippon Columbia YP-8527-AI with obi also fits this tier if you want a warmer, more vintage character.

At **$75–200**, the out-of-print **Speakers Corner 2001 180g (050155-1)** is worth hunting for if you find it at a fair price – Kevin Gray's warmer, rounder character suits some systems better than Ryan Smith's more analytical Sterling Sound cut. A clean late orange-and-black 1966–68 US pressing with VAN GELDER stamps in the deadwax lives here as well, giving you genuine RVG metalwork without first-press pricing. A NM UK HMV stereo (CSD 1605) or mono (CLP 1869) also fits this tier – cut locally at EMI from the same Abbey Road tape now used for modern reissues.

At **$200–500**, the **Analogue Productions UHQR 45rpm 2LP box (AUHQR 0007-45)** is available on the resale market and represents the current outright sonic pinnacle. This is the target for anyone who wants the best available sound, full stop. A clean stereo AS-77 original with RVG deadwax also lands in this range, provided you accept the 60 Hz hum as either tolerable or part of the record's character. Mid-condition A-77 mono originals occasionally surface here too.

At **$500–2,000+**, the target is an **NM A-77 mono original** with all correct first-press indicators – 1501 Broadway inner sleeve topping out at A-50, laminated white-spine gatefold, VAN GELDER + LW hand-etched in the deadwax. The mono original delivers a presentation no modern reissue can fully replicate: the density, the hum-free silence between notes, the sense of RVG cutting directly from the live reel. This is collector territory – as much about provenance and the ritual of ownership as about sonic superiority over the 2020 AS Series. A pristine NM AS-77 stereo with matching provenance also fits this tier for those who accept the hum.

**The pragmatic two-copy solution** endorsed by Kaplan, Fremer, and the Steve Hoffman forum consensus: the **2020 Acoustic Sounds Series 33⅓ for stereo** and the **2025 60th-anniversary mono for mono**, total outlay ~$80, covers the album completely in both formats from the best available source tape. Everything above that is about the audiophile experience of diminishing returns – still meaningful, but the gap between the AS Series and the UHQR is smaller than the gap between the AS Series and anything that came before it.

## Confirmed phantom releases – pressings that DO NOT exist

- **No Classic Records 200g Quiex SV-P or 45rpm box** – frequently confused with Speakers Corner, which filled that audiophile role
- **No MFSL (Mobile Fidelity) vinyl, CD, gold CD, or SACD** – MFSL never licensed the Impulse! Coltrane catalogue
- **No Craft Recordings / OJC edition** – Craft holds Concord/Fantasy/Prestige/Riverside, not Impulse!
- **No Music on Vinyl (MOV) pressing**
- **No Nautilus Superdisc, DCC Compact Classics, or Cisco Records edition**
- **No Blue Note Tone Poet** – wrong label entirely
- **No SHM-Vinyl confirmed Japanese issue**
- **No Impex, Pure Pleasure, or ORG (Original Recordings Group) edition**

## Pressings to avoid: grey-market EU pirates

*A Love Supreme* is **not in EU public domain** (2011 extension raised protection to 70 years) yet is widely bootlegged. Avoid: **DOL, DolChess, Waxtime/Waxtime in Color, Jazz Wax, Jazz Images, Doxy, Vinyl Lovers, Vinyl Passion, Not Now Music, Ermitage, Pan Am, Blue Moon, 20th Century Masterworks, Jazz Time.** Red flags: price under $25 for sealed 180g, no "Under license from Universal/Verve/Impulse!" statement, no named mastering engineer, "Made in EU" with Spanish or Russian PO box, color-vinyl gimmicks. Legitimate AAA reissues always name the mastering engineer (Ryan K. Smith, Kevin Gray) and describe the tape source.

## What actually makes a pressing superior

Four factors dominate, in descending order. **Tape generation:** the Abbey Road 1965 flat copy used by Ryan K. Smith outperforms the pre-2008 US "master" used by Speakers Corner and the 2010 AP, which outperforms the Bell Sound-derived tape used for late ABC/early MCA reissues. **Mastering chain:** AAA cuts preserve the 3D imaging; digital-intermediate cuts sound flatter. **Cutting engineer philosophy:** Van Gelder emphasises upper midrange, forward horns, less warmth; Kevin Gray tends warmer and softer; Ryan Smith extracts more air and low-end while retaining transient snap. **Pressing plant and vinyl compound:** QRP and Pallas dominate the audiophile tier; Clarity Vinyl offers the lowest noise floor; weight alone means little.
