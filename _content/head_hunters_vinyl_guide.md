# Head Hunters on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Head Hunters* was recorded in **August–September 1973** at two San Francisco studios: **Wally Heider Studios** (engineered by Fred Catero and Jeremy Zatkin) and **Different Fur Trading Co.** (engineered by Dane Butcher and John Vieira). Mixed and produced by **David Rubinson and Herbie Hancock** at Catero Sound Co., San Francisco. Personnel: Herbie Hancock (Fender Rhodes, Hohner D6 clavinet, ARP Odyssey, ARP Soloist, pipe), Bennie Maupin (soprano/tenor saxophone, saxello, bass clarinet, alto flute), Paul Jackson (electric bass, marimbula), Bill Summers (congas and extensive percussion – including blowing across a beer bottle neck to open "Watermelon Man"), Harvey Mason (Yamaha drums). Released October 26, 1973 on Columbia KC 32731. Cover design by Victor Moscoso; photography by Waldo Bascom.

Hancock had departed entirely from his "Mwandishi" sextet period toward a music rooted in Sly Stone, James Brown, and Curtis Mayfield. "Chameleon" opens with its 15-minute groove; "Watermelon Man" is a radical reworking of Hancock's own 1962 Blue Note hit. The album became the first jazz LP to go platinum, and by RIAA count remains the second highest-selling jazz album of all time after *Kind of Blue*. In 2007 the Library of Congress added it to the National Recording Registry.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original 1973 US Columbia KC 32731, first-generation metalwork, Pitman or Terre Haute](https://www.discogs.com/release/31381-Herbie-Hancock-Head-Hunters) – still relatively affordable.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Analogue Productions 45rpm 2LP (2015/2021)](https://www.discogs.com/release/18271585-Herbie-Hancock-Head-Hunters) – Ryan K. Smith, Sterling Sound, AAA, QRP; the sonic summit.

**Best-value modern:** [Analogue Productions 180g 33rpm (2015/2021/2022)](https://www.discogs.com/release/18271585-Herbie-Hancock-Head-Hunters) – same mastering chain, single LP, widely available.


## Recording history and tape provenance

Hancock had been playing low-profile performances around the Bay Area with the new band through the summer of 1973, then entered the studio in August. The two studios used reflect a typical Bay Area production approach of the period: Wally Heider's large-room main tracking space captured the ensemble's core performances, while Different Fur was used for overdubs and additional recording. The Catero Sound Co. credit reflects Fred Catero's independent production company, from which the mixing was supervised. Catero's engineering placed warmth and body in the low frequencies – essential for the funk character of "Chameleon" and "Sly."

The album was co-produced by **David Rubinson**, one of the Bay Area's most respected producers, alongside Hancock. The decision to use Rubinson was deliberate – Hancock needed someone who could bridge his jazz sensibility with the commercial accessibility he was pursuing. Harvey Mason's drum tracks were arranged by Mason himself for "Watermelon Man." The album was remixed for quadraphonic sound in 1974 and released in SQ matrix format; the quad mix includes elements not heard in the stereo version, including an additional keyboard melody at the opening of "Sly."

**Tape provenance:** Columbia's archive of the original 1973 multi-track tapes has been well-maintained. Ryan K. Smith's Analogue Productions mastering credits confirm working "from the original master tapes," and the consistent quality across both the 33rpm and 45rpm editions supports that the tapes are in good condition. The AP pressing was manufactured for Analogue Productions by Sony Music Entertainment.

## Original US Columbia pressings (1973)

**Catalogue: Columbia KC 32731.** The "KC" prefix denotes Columbia's standard stereo LP designation from this period.

**Label:** The KC 32731 first pressing uses the **orange ring "Columbia"** label – the standard Columbia label from the early 1970s onward. The 360 Sound two-eye label had been phased out before 1973; do not expect it here.

**Cover art:** Victor Moscoso's distinctive mask-based cover design shows Hancock wearing a mask based on the *kple kple* mask of the Baoulé people of Ivory Coast. Positioned clockwise from lower left: Mason, Jackson, Maupin, and Summers.

### Pressing plant identification

Columbia's three US plants were all used for this title:
- **"P" stamp** in deadwax = **Pitman, New Jersey**
- **"T" stamp** = **Terre Haute, Indiana**
- **"S" (backwards)** = **Santa Maria, California** – also "SX" etching indicates metal parts originated from Santa Maria

Per the AnalogPlanet Columbia code guide, the alpha suffix (1A, 1B, 1C) distributed lacquers to different plants simultaneously; A and B to Pitman, C and D to Terre Haute, E and F to Santa Maria. However, this correspondence is not universal and the plant stamp is the reliable identifier.

**Matrix:** The coveted first-generation matrices are **AL 32731-1A** (Side A) and **BL 32731-1A** (Side B). Discogs collectors who specifically sought -1A/-1A copies: "Picked up an -1A/-1A pressing this afternoon, definitely changed my appreciation for this album for the better." Another: "No need to go to any of the 'premium' pressings as this one has it all."

The "Customatrix o" stamp (small circled lowercase "o") indicates Customatrix handled the plating – standard for first-year Columbia pressings.

**Market values:** Clean first-generation -1A/-1A original: **$20–$60 NM** – this album is still relatively affordable because it was pressed in very large quantities. One Discogs reviewer found a -1A/-1A for **$4** at a local shop. Good copies continue to surface at record fairs and shops for under $30.

### CBS/Sony Japanese pressings (1970s–80s)

Japanese Columbia releases were manufactured by CBS/Sony at the Shizuoka pressing plant (regarded by the Tracking Angle as "the absolute best in the world... even better than RTI or QRP"). The early Japanese pressing (r25977355) bears matrix `SOPL-238A1 1 A 1`. One reviewer: "Great sounding Japanese reissue... the snare lacks a bit of snap" compared to the AP, but the vinyl quality is superlative. The CBS logo with Sony branding "CBS/Sony" appears on both cover and labels. **$40–$80 with obi.**

### Quadraphonic SQ matrix pressing (1974)

Columbia released the quad mix in SQ (Stereo Quadraphonic) matrix format as **PCQ 32731**. The quad mix features elements absent from the stereo version, including an additional keyboard melody at the opening of "Sly." Decodeable with an SQ decoder for full four-channel playback; plays as enhanced stereo without one. Of interest to collectors and four-channel enthusiasts. **$15–$40.**

## The reissue hierarchy

### Analogue Productions 45rpm 2LP (2015/2021) – the sonic benchmark

**Cat. AAPJ 084-45** | Format: 2×180g (originally 200g) 45rpm, gatefold, Stoughton tip-on jacket. **Mastered at Sterling Sound by Ryan K. Smith, AAA, from the original master tapes. Pressed at QRP.** Manufactured for Analogue Productions by Sony Music Entertainment.

The 2021 repress is 180g rather than the original 200g; the mastering and matrix are identical.

Simon Guile at AnalogPlanet (10/11 Music, 10/11 Sound): "Listening to the opening track 'Chameleon' on any release will get you feeling funky, but the Analogue Productions 2LP 45rpm release will get you groovin'!... I'd previously had a run-of-the-mill reissue of this album that I thought was good and did the job, but because of how much I love this album I opted to spend some more bucks on the AP release, not expecting a huge difference. Boy was I wrong. There is so much more clarity to every instrument."

SoundStage! Hi-Fi on what the AP mastering reveals: "Harvey Mason's kick drum is too forward on the CD and crowds the music; on AP's LP, it's audible but in support. Reverb is now audible in the notes of Bennie Maupin's sax, and Paul Jackson's bass, still the funk backbone of the album, isn't as overbearing as it now sometimes sounds to me on the CD."

Discogs: "Impressive mastering and pressing, one of the best sounding LP I own." "Awesome pressing. I can't even believe it. It almost makes you want to cry when you hear it." One owner who compared the AP 33rpm against a clean 1973 Pitman original chose the AP: "just a bit more refined and smooth but still the magic of the original."

**$80–$120 secondary market.** Periodically in print from Acoustic Sounds.

### Analogue Productions 200g/180g 33rpm (2015/2021/2022) – strong value

**Cat. AAPJ 084** | Format: single 200g or 180g LP, 33⅓. **Same Ryan K. Smith / Sterling Sound / AAA mastering. QRP pressing.** Earlier pressing was 200g; recent runs are 180g with identical matrix.

One Discogs reviewer who compared it directly with the 2009 Music On Vinyl: "I was able to tell the difference between this and the 2009 Music On Vinyl press almost immediately. The bass on this is RICH and the midrange is lively. Each instrument has plenty of room to shine." Joseph Taylor (SoundStage! Hi-Fi): "Columbia's LP release had decent sound, but Analogue Productions' new vinyl mastering by Ryan Smith at Sterling Sound takes the sound up several notches from there."

**$35–$55 new** from Acoustic Sounds or online retailers.

### Music On Vinyl MOV (2009) – a step below

Music On Vinyl issued *Head Hunters* under their Columbia catalogue licence. The pressing is digitally sourced (cut from PCM, not analog tape) and is consistently rated below the AP editions. It is the comparison point most frequently cited when AP owners describe their upgrade experience. Dead-silent surfaces; perfectly competent, but not in the same league. **$15–$25 used.**

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $30:** A clean [**original 1973 Columbia KC 32731**](https://www.discogs.com/release/31381) with first-generation metalwork, Pitman or Terre Haute plant, -1A/-1A matrices. One of the better deals in jazz vinyl collecting; originals remain affordable because the album sold massively.

**$35–$55:** The [**AP 180g 33rpm**](https://www.discogs.com/release/7495983) – the modern audiophile standard, same mastering as the 45rpm, available new.

**$80–$120:** The [**AP 45rpm 2LP**](https://www.discogs.com/release/10453858) – the definitive version. The upgrade from the 33rpm is audible on any decent system; if *Head Hunters* is important to you, the 45rpm is worth the premium.
