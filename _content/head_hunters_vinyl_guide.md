# Head Hunters on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Head Hunters* was recorded in October 1973 at two San Francisco studios – **Wally Heider Studios** and **Different Fur Trading Co.** – and mixed by producer David Rubinson and engineer Fred Catero at Catero Sound Co., also in San Francisco. Personnel: Herbie Hancock (Fender Rhodes, Hohner clavinet, ARP Odyssey, ARP Soloist, pipe), Bennie Maupin (soprano and tenor saxophones, saxello, bass clarinet, alto flute), Paul Jackson (electric bass, marimbula), Bill Summers (congas and extensive percussion including a beer bottle on "Watermelon Man"), and Harvey Mason (drums). Released on Columbia KC 32731 on October 26, 1973.

The album is a departure from Hancock's acoustic and electric Miles Davis-influenced work toward a music rooted in Sly Stone, James Brown, and Curtis Mayfield. "Chameleon" opens with its 15-minute groove; Bill Summers blows across a beer bottle neck to create the distinctive flute-like melody that opens "Watermelon Man," reworked from Hancock's Blue Note debut. The album became the first jazz LP to go platinum and remains one of the best-selling jazz recordings of all time – by RIAA count, the second highest-selling jazz album behind only *Kind of Blue*.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original 1973 US Columbia KC 32731, -1A/-1A matrices, Pitman or Terre Haute](https://www.discogs.com/release/31381-Herbie-Hancock-Head-Hunters) – first year pressing, warm analog character, still relatively affordable.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Analogue Productions 45rpm 2LP (2015/2021)](https://www.discogs.com/release/18271585-Herbie-Hancock-Head-Hunters) – Ryan K. Smith, Sterling Sound, AAA, QRP; the sonic summit.

**Best-value modern:** [Analogue Productions 180g 33rpm (2021/2022)](https://www.discogs.com/release/18271585-Herbie-Hancock-Head-Hunters) – same mastering chain, single LP, widely available at ~$35–$50.


## Recording history and tape provenance

Hancock assembled a new band in 1973, departing entirely from his Mwandishi group to pursue a funk-oriented direction. The sessions at Wally Heider's in San Francisco captured the ensemble live in the studio, with Hancock programming and playing his synthesizers and electric keyboards while the rhythm section locked into extended grooves. Fred Catero's engineering placed warmth and body in the low frequencies – essential for the funk character of the album.

The album's production was supervised by **David Rubinson and Friends, Inc.**, one of the most respected production organisations in San Francisco at the time. Rubinson worked with Hancock to shape the album's commercial accessibility without compromising its musical ambitions.

**Tape provenance:** Columbia's archive of the original 1973 multi-track tapes has been well-maintained. Both the 33rpm and 45rpm Analogue Productions pressings are credited with mastering "from the original master tapes" by Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound, and the consistency of praise for both pressings supports that the tapes are in good condition. SoundStage! Hi-Fi reviewed the 33rpm AP: "Columbia's LP release had decent sound, but Analogue Productions' new vinyl mastering by Ryan Smith at Sterling Sound takes the sound up several notches from there."

## Original US Columbia pressings (1973)

**Catalogue: Columbia KC 32731** (the "KC" prefix denotes Columbia's standard stereo LP designation in this era)

### First year pressings (1973–74)

The earliest pressings used Columbia's standard pressing plants:

- **Pitman, New Jersey** – most commonly cited; indicated by "P" stamp in deadwax (Customatrix plating indicator)
- **Terre Haute, Indiana** – no plant suffix indicator, or "TH"
- **Santa Maria, California** – "S" etched in runouts; "SX" indicates metal parts from Santa Maria

**Matrix identification:** The coveted early matrices are **AL 32731-1A** (Side A) and **BL 32731-1A** (Side B). The "1A" suffix indicates first-generation metalwork. Multiple Discogs collectors specifically seek -1A/-1A copies: "Picked up an -1A/-1A pressing this afternoon, definitely changed my appreciation for this album for the better."

The "AL" prefix is Columbia's standard matrix code for this period; the presence of a "Customatrix o" stamp (a small circled lowercase o) indicates Customatrix handled the plating, which was standard for first-year Columbia pressings.

**Label:** Red Columbia "360 Sound" label was being phased out by 1973; the KC 32731 first pressing uses the **orange ring "Columbia"** label, which was the standard Columbia label through most of the 1970s. The 360 Sound two-eye label was already gone for domestic Columbia releases by this point.

**Market values:** Clean first-year -1A/-1A original: **$20–$60 NM**. This album is still relatively affordable – originals were pressed in large numbers given its commercial success, and many copies have survived in good condition. One Discogs reviewer found a -1A/-1A for **$4** at a local shop, noting that good copies still surface at reasonable prices.

### CBS/Sony Japanese pressings (1970s–80s)

Japanese Columbia releases were manufactured by CBS/Sony in Japan. Discogs documents a Japanese pressing (r25977355) pressed at CBS/Sony with matrix `SOPL-238A1 1 A 1` – the CBS Japan prefix. These are generally praised for quiet vinyl; one reviewer calls it "a great sounding Japanese reissue" with a slight note about the snare "lacking a bit of snap" compared to some comparisons. **$40–$80 with obi.**

## The reissue hierarchy

### Analogue Productions 45rpm 2LP (2015/2021) – the sonic benchmark

**Cat. AAPJ 084-45** | Format: 2×180g 45rpm, gatefold, Stoughton tip-on jacket. **Mastered at Sterling Sound by Ryan K. Smith, AAA, from the original master tapes. Pressed at QRP.** The 2021 repress is 180g rather than the original 200g; same mastering and matrix.

This pressing is the undisputed favourite among those who have heard it alongside the 33rpm and other versions. Simon Guile at AnalogPlanet: "Listening to the opening track 'Chameleon' on any release will get you feeling funky, but the Analogue Productions 2LP 45rpm release will get you groovin'! Analogue Productions does an incredible job on this release, keeping you enveloped in the music while breaking down the barrier between your stereo and musicians... Boy was I wrong. There is so much more clarity to every instrument, and instead of the funk taking a backseat, it punches you in the face." – 10/11 Music, 10/11 Sound.

SoundStage! Hi-Fi on the 33rpm (same mastering, 33rpm): "Harvey Mason's kick drum is too forward on the CD and crowds the music; on AP's LP, it's audible but in support. Reverb is now audible in the notes of Bennie Maupin's sax, and Paul Jackson's bass, still the funk backbone of the album, isn't as overbearing as it now sometimes sounds to me on the CD." The 45rpm is reported to take these improvements further. Discogs: "Impressive mastering and pressing, one of the best sounding LP I own." "Awesome pressing. I can't even believe it. It almost makes you want to cry when you hear it."

**$80–$120 on the secondary market.** Periodically in print from Acoustic Sounds.

### Analogue Productions 180g/200g 33rpm (2015/2021/2022) – strong value

**Cat. AAPJ 084** | Format: single 180g or 200g LP, 33⅓. **Same Ryan K. Smith / Sterling Sound / AAA mastering. QRP pressing.** Earlier pressing was 200g; recent runs are 180g with the same matrix.

One Discogs reviewer who has compared the 33rpm against a clean 1973 Pitman original: "I also had a original Pitman pressing that sounded great, but chose to keep the Acoustic Sounds 33 over it after comparison, just a bit more refined and smooth but still the magic of the original." Another: "Not saying anything new here by saying this sounds great, but I will say that even on a lower-end (but not bogus) system like mine, I was able to tell the difference between this and the 2009 Music On Vinyl press almost immediately. The bass on this is RICH and the midrange is lively. Each instrument has plenty of room to shine."

**$35–$55 new** from Acoustic Sounds or online retailers when in print.

### Music On Vinyl MOV (2009) – a step below

Music On Vinyl issued *Head Hunters* as part of their Columbia catalogue licensing. The MOV pressing is digitally sourced (cut from PCM rather than analog tape) and is consistently rated below the AP in direct comparisons. "The 2009 Music On Vinyl press" is the comparison that the AP 33rpm reviewers use to demonstrate the AP's superiority. However, at its typical secondary market price of **$15–$25**, it remains an accessible option for those who cannot find or afford the AP.

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $30:** A clean **original 1973 Columbia KC 32731 -1A/-1A** first-year pressing is still findable at record shops for $10–$30 and sounds excellent. Seek the Pitman or Terre Haute plant variant with -1A matrices. At this price, this is one of the better deals in jazz vinyl collecting.

**$35–$55:** The **AP 180g 33rpm** is the modern audiophile standard at a reasonable price. The mastering clearly surpasses every non-AP option.

**$80–$120:** The **AP 45rpm 2LP** is the definitive version. If the music is important to you and you have the system to appreciate it, this is worth the premium over the 33rpm.
