# Giant Steps on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*Giant Steps* was recorded across two sessions at Atlantic's 56th Street studio in New York City: the main session on **May 4–5, 1959**, which captured six of the seven tracks with Tommy Flanagan on piano, Paul Chambers on bass, and Art Taylor on drums; and a separate session on **December 2, 1959** for "Naima," which used a different rhythm section – Wynton Kelly (piano), Paul Chambers (bass), and Jimmy Cobb (drums). Engineers were Tom Dowd and Phil Iihle; the album was produced by Nesuhi Ertegun and released in January 1960. It was Coltrane's debut for Atlantic after leaving Prestige.

The recording is controversial among audiophiles in a way few classic jazz albums are. Tom Dowd used an Ampex 300-8R eight-track recorder and produced both a dedicated mono mix and a dedicated stereo mix – neither is a fold-down. The stereo, however, places the saxophone and piano hard left with the bass and drums hard right, with no center image. This is not true stereo separation in the acoustic sense; it is early-era hard panning, and it has divided collectors ever since. Coltrane's presence collapses in the stereo mix compared to the mono, where he commands the center. The mono is almost universally preferred on this album by serious collectors who have compared both formats; the Analogue Productions Atlantic 75 stereo 45rpm (2024) has received mixed reviews partly for this reason, while earlier stereo pressings are admired despite (or because of) the panning. A further complication: the Rhino "60th Anniversary" reissue (2020) includes a 33rpm stereo LP with new mastering by Ryan K. Smith that many Discogs reviewers find more satisfying than the AP 45rpm for the price.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original mono black label Atlantic 1311](https://www.discogs.com/release/2155244-John-Coltrane-Giant-Steps) – deep groove, silver lettering, 446 W. 50th Street address; the preferred presentation.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Analogue Productions Atlantic 75 45rpm 2LP (2024)](https://www.discogs.com/release/30051634-John-Coltrane-Giant-Steps) – Ryan K. Smith, AAA, QRP; the definitive stereo treatment, with caveats.

**Best-value modern:** [Rhino 60th Anniversary 33rpm 180g (2020)](https://www.discogs.com/master/32236-John-Coltrane-Giant-Steps) – Ryan K. Smith, widely praised at ~$25–$30.


## Recording history and tape provenance

The principal sessions took place **May 4–5, 1959** at Atlantic's 56th Street studio. Engineers Tom Dowd and Phil Iihle recorded to an Ampex 300-8R eight-track machine – then a state-of-the-art multi-track format that gave Dowd flexibility in mixing. The studio was Atlantic's "office studio," not a purpose-built acoustic space, which contributes to the somewhat dry, close-miked character of the recording. The harder, brighter quality of the Atlantic sound compared to Coltrane's Prestige recordings (done by Rudy Van Gelder in Hackensack) has been noted by multiple reviewers; Van Gelder knew Coltrane's sound intimately and the Prestige sessions tend to have more air and depth.

Personnel on May 4–5: Coltrane (tenor saxophone), Tommy Flanagan (piano), Paul Chambers (bass), Art Taylor (drums). The session was intense: "Giant Steps" itself runs at approximately 300 beats per minute, and the harmonic language was new enough that Flanagan, who had little preparation time, plays relatively sparingly behind Coltrane's solos – audibly navigating the changes in real time. This actually serves the recording well, letting Coltrane's lines breathe.

"**Naima**" was recorded separately on **December 2, 1959**, with Wynton Kelly, Paul Chambers, and Jimmy Cobb – essentially the Kind of Blue rhythm section from a few months earlier. It is the only track with no editing and the most conventionally recorded piece on the album.

**The tape situation:** Dowd recorded to 8-track and produced discrete mono and stereo mixes; neither is a fold-down. The stereo mix places horns and piano hard left, rhythm section hard right – a common Dowd practice of the period. This means both mixes have legitimate provenance from the original session. The tapes have remained in the Atlantic/Rhino/Warner archive and have been the source for all modern audiophile reissues. There is no documented tape degradation issue of the kind affecting some Impulse! titles.

## Original US Atlantic pressings

Released in both mono and stereo:

- **Mono: Atlantic 1311**
- **Stereo: Atlantic SD 1311**

The controversy around original pressings of *Giant Steps* is well-documented and unusual. [The "Into Somethin'" blog](https://into-somethin.blog/2017/01/15/john-coltrane-giant-steps-atlantic-sd-1311/) has thoroughly documented the label question: the mono first pressing unambiguously uses the **black label with silver lettering**, but the stereo first pressing's label is genuinely contested. A widespread belief holds that the stereo first press should have the bullseye/green-blue label – but detailed research suggests the first stereo pressing may actually have been on the **plum/orange white fan label**, meaning there may be no bullseye stereo first pressing. The bullseye appears on the Danish Metronome pressing, which was pressed from original US-supplied stampers (AVCO matrix), but this is a European pressing, not the domestic first.

**Mono first pressing (1311) – black label:**
- Black label, silver lettering, "Long Playing Unbreakable"
- **Deep groove** around the label – the key identifier for the true first pressing
- Matrix: **PRLP/ST-A-59201-A AT** (Side A), **ST-A-59202-A AT** (Side B) – hand-etched "AT" (Atlantic, from their mastering)
- 446 W. 50th Street address on the sleeve back
- Pressed by Abbey Record Manufacturing (AB in deadwax)
- NM copies: **$300–$600**

The non-deep-groove pressing with the same stampers is a second pressing, sonically very close. LJC, who owned both stereo and mono originals, sold the stereo specifically because the hard panning "got to me after a while" and preferred the mono's centered presentation.

**Stereo (SD 1311) – label controversy:**
The Discogs entry for the standard-acknowledged "first stereo" (r488973-equivalent) shows **green bullseye** labels, but the Into Somethin' blog argues this may not be chronologically first. For practical collecting purposes, the earliest domestic stereo copies on green bullseye labels are highly sought, but the mono black label is the unambiguous first pressing and the sonically preferred format.

### Matrix guidance

**Mono:** Look for `ST-A-59201-A AT` and `ST-A-59202-A AT` – the "A" suffix indicates first-generation metalwork. The "AT" is hand-etched by Atlantic's mastering department.

**Stereo:** Look for `ST-A-59201 AVCO` and `ST-A-59202 AVCO` – "AVCO" is the etching used on Atlantic pressings of this era; a single-digit numeral after the catalogue number (e.g. `ST-A-59201-B` = second stamper generation).

## Later Atlantic label variants

**Green bullseye / plum-orange labels (1960s):** The plum/orange white fan label reprints are generally well-regarded. LJC notes that an orange/red Atlantic pressing of *Giant Steps* sounds "absolutely stellar." These trade for **$20–$60** in clean condition and are the most cost-effective vintage option.

**Red/green Warner-era labels (1970s):** Columbia introduced these after the Warner acquisition of Atlantic in 1968. Pressed on thinner vinyl; generally the worst-sounding vintage option. Avoid.

## The reissue hierarchy

### Rhino 60th Anniversary 180g 33rpm (2020) – recommended entry point

**Cat. R1-1311 / 0603497843091** | Format: single 180g LP, 33⅓, gatefold. **Mastered by Ryan K. Smith, from the original master tape.** Stereo.

This pressing has accumulated an exceptionally positive Discogs reception relative to its price point (~$25–$30). One reviewer: "I spent much of my first listen to this record with my hand over my heart, my head tilted back, my eyes closed, and my jaw dropped. It's not like I haven't heard this album before, but I've certainly never heard it with such clarity and power." Another comparing it directly to the AP 45rpm: "The bass is a lot more nuanced and detailed... the Rhino has a smidge more punch." There are also minority reports praising the AP 45 as clearly superior.

The Ryan K. Smith mastering is the same engineer as the AP 75th edition – the differences between them are attributable to the 45rpm speed advantage and the QRP pressing plant quality, not a different mastering chain.

### Analogue Productions Atlantic 75 45rpm 2LP (2024) – the audiophile statement

**Cat. AAPA 010-45** | Format: 2×180g 45rpm, gatefold, Stoughton tip-on jacket. **Mastered by Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound, AAA, from the original master tape. Pressed at QRP.**

The format is stereo. Multiple Discogs reviewers with strong systems love it: "One of the absolute best sounding jazz records in my collection; it's straight up UHQR quality imo." "Coltrane's saxophone virtuosity is so much clearer." However, a notable contingent finds it underwhelming relative to its price (~$50–$80): "Abit bass shy, abit lacking in overall clarity. On these fronts the recentish Rhino Stereo 33 perhaps even is overall preferable." One reviewer who compares it to a 1961 mono copy notes the original "was full of depth and had more presence in the highs of the cymbals."

The hard-panning issue of the stereo mix cannot be fixed by any mastering engineer, as it is baked into the original mix. Any stereo pressing of *Giant Steps* will place Coltrane to the extreme left. If this is not an issue for your system or preferences, the AP 75 is the finest stereo presentation of this album available. If you prefer Coltrane centered, go mono.

**Note:** A manufacturing defect has been reported on some copies – the label on Side A is not bonded to the center hole. Cosmetic only; does not affect sound.

### AP Atlantic 75 33rpm 180g (2024) – same mastering, 33rpm

**Cat. AAPA 010** | Same Ryan K. Smith mastering, pressed at QRP, but at 33⅓. Available at approximately half the price of the 45rpm set. A legitimate option for those who find the 45rpm format inconvenient.

### Rhino "Heavyweight Champion" box set (1995) – the Steve Hoffman cut

Atlantic's complete Coltrane box set, mastered by Steve Hoffman and Bernie Grundman, includes *Giant Steps* alongside Coltrane's other Atlantic albums. Hoffman's mastering from the original tapes is highly regarded; the pressing quality on the box set vinyl is generally good. This set appears on the secondary market for around **$100–$200** for the complete box and includes detailed liner notes and rare material.

## Japanese pressings

Toshiba-EMI pressed Atlantic Coltrane titles throughout the 1970s–80s and these are consistently praised for dead-quiet vinyl and detailed sound. The Japanese stereo approach to the hard panning is identical to the US stereo – it is a mix decision, not a pressing artefact – but Japanese vinyl quality often surpasses US equivalents. These trade for **$40–$100 with obi** and represent excellent value.

---

## Pressings to avoid

**EU grey-market (DOL, Not Now Music, etc.):** The Atlantic catalogue is heavily bootlegged in the EU. Any pressing without Warner Music Group licensing language and without credentialed mastering provenance should be avoided. OJC-series Coltrane is from the Prestige catalogue; for Atlantic titles, there is no OJC equivalent.

**1970s red/green Warner Atlantic labels:** Thin vinyl, degraded sound. These are widely available and cheap for a reason.

---

## Buyer's guide by budget

**Under $35:** The **Rhino 60th Anniversary 180g 33rpm** (2020) is the clear choice – Ryan K. Smith, AAA, widely praised, excellent value.

**$50–$80:** The **Analogue Productions Atlantic 75 45rpm 2LP** (2024) for the ultimate stereo presentation. Buy with awareness of the hard panning. Or spend the same money finding a clean **1960s plum/orange Atlantic** repress that captures the period character.

**$150+:** A **clean 1960s mono or early stereo Atlantic** pressing gives you the original tape transfer in its period presentation. The mono black label is the collector's benchmark; VG+ copies at this price point are the real thing.
