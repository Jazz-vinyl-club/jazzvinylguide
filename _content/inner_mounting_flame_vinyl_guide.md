# The Inner Mounting Flame on Vinyl: The Definitive Pressing Guide

## Summary

*The Inner Mounting Flame* was recorded on **August 14, 1971** at CBS Studios, New York City. Personnel: John McLaughlin (acoustic and electric guitars); Jerry Goodman (acoustic and electric violins); Jan Hammer (Fender Rhodes, ring modulator, piano); Rick Laird (electric bass); Billy Cobham (drums). Produced by **John McLaughlin**; engineered by **Don Puluse**. Released **November 3, 1971** on Columbia Records as **KC 31067**. The album is credited to "The Mahavishnu Orchestra with John McLaughlin." All compositions by John McLaughlin.

Five musicians from five countries – McLaughlin (English), Cobham (Panamanian), Hammer (Czech), Goodman (American), Laird (Irish) – in a single week of rehearsals produced music that defined jazz-rock fusion and has not been surpassed in its idiom. The original US Columbia pressing is the reference; the Speakers Corner 180g and Friday Music clear vinyl are the two meaningful modern options, both made from the original analogue tapes and drawing genuinely contrasting reviews.

**Best early pressing for audiophiles:** [Original US Columbia KC 31067, Terre Haute pressing (1971)](https://www.discogs.com/release/557286-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – AAA from source, "IT" in deadwax, purple insert.

**Best modern pressing for audiophiles:** [Friday Music 180g clear vinyl](https://www.discogs.com/release/9485353-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – Joe Reagoso mastering, RTI pressing; brighter, more revealing character.

**Best-value modern:** [Speakers Corner 180g (2008)](https://www.discogs.com/release/1718798-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-with-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – pure analogue, Pallas pressing, HCB-EBS matrix (Heinz Gäde mastering).


## Recording history and tape provenance

John McLaughlin was born in Doncaster, Yorkshire, in 1942. He came up through British rhythm and blues with the Graham Bond Organisation in the mid-1960s before relocating to New York in 1969, where he joined Miles Davis's electric band and played on *In a Silent Way* and *Bitches Brew*. He then joined Tony Williams's Lifetime. By early 1971, encouraged by Miles Davis, he was ready to lead his own group.

The name "Mahavishnu" was given to McLaughlin by Sri Chinmoy, an Indian spiritual leader based in New York whose followers included several prominent musicians of the era. McLaughlin explained: "Mahavishnu is from Sanskrit. Maha means 'great.' Mahavishnu is 'great vishnu.' Vishnu is part of the Hindu pantheon – the sustainer of the universe." McLaughlin's spiritual practice under Sri Chinmoy shaped the music's character directly: the album opens with "Meeting of the Spirits" and the insert accompanying every original pressing contains Sri Chinmoy's poem "Aspiration."

McLaughlin assembled his quintet with uncommon care. **Billy Cobham** (born in Panama, raised in New York, trained at the High School of Music and Art) had played with McLaughlin on his 1970 solo album *My Goal's Beyond* and was already one of New York's most sought-after session drummers. **Jerry Goodman** had also appeared on *My Goal's Beyond* as a member of The Flock. **Jan Hammer**, a Czech émigré who had studied at the Berklee College of Music in Boston, brought a unique approach to the electric keyboard – he used a ring modulator to generate vocal-like pitchbending effects that had no precedent in jazz. **Rick Laird**, from Dublin, was a Berklee graduate who had worked extensively in London's jazz scene before arriving in New York.

The five met in New York in July 1971 and rehearsed for a single week. Their debut gigs followed immediately, at the **Gaslight at the Au Go Go** in Greenwich Village, opening for bluesman John Lee Hooker. McLaughlin recalled: "The first set was shaky but the second set just took off and every night it was great. They wanted to hold us over and a few days after the second week, we went into the studio." Columbia Records had already signed them.

**The session:** The album was recorded in a single day at CBS Studios, New York, on August 14, 1971. Don Puluse engineered. All compositions had been written by McLaughlin in advance – unlike much jazz-rock of the period, there was no significant improvisation on overall structure, though solos were spontaneous. The tracks range from the shattering intensity of "Vital Transformation" and "Awakening" to the unaccompanied acoustic "A Lotus on Irish Streams," in which McLaughlin and Goodman explore Irish folk melody in a chamber setting with no rhythm section.

**Track notes:** "Meeting of the Spirits" opens with McLaughlin's arpeggios alone – a technique he would later develop on his double-neck guitar (six-string and twelve-string), using the twelve-string neck for opening arpeggios and the six-string for solos in later performances. "The Noonward Race" features Cobham at his most ferocious, with Hammer's distorted electric piano and McLaughlin's harmonically dense soloing creating layers that sound closer to late King Crimson than to jazz. "A Lotus on Irish Streams" strips everything away – just acoustic guitar and violin in a duo that references Irish traditional music without pastiche. "The Dance of Maya" uses odd time signatures (7/8 and 9/8 sections interlocked) and a wah-saturated guitar tone that is the album's most overtly rock-derived moment. "You Know, You Know" is a showcase for Cobham's melodic drum work – a brooding piece in which the rhythm section carries the melody, with the album's instrumentation consisting solely of guitar, violin, keyboards, bass, and drums. "Awakening" closes the album in a state of pure controlled combustion: three and a half minutes of velocity and precision that remains one of the most intense closers in the jazz-rock catalogue.

**Sri Chinmoy and the cover:** The cover photograph was taken by Pranavananda Anthony Hixon, a follower of Sri Chinmoy. The design was by Ron Coro. Every original pressing includes a 9" × 10" insert carrying Sri Chinmoy's poem "Aspiration" – this insert is often missing from used copies and its presence is a positive marker for completeness.

**Critical reception and legacy:** AllMusic gave the album five stars; Richard Ginell wrote that it "defined the fusion of jazz and rock a year after Miles Davis's *Bitches Brew* breakthrough." The Penguin Guide to Jazz placed it in the Core Collection. *Downbeat* named it album of the year for 1972 in both the Jazz and Pop categories – the only album in the magazine's history to hold both titles simultaneously. Bill Milkowski in JazzTimes (1998): "One is struck by the grandiose reach of the quintet that dared to call itself an orchestra. Pieces like 'Meeting of the Spirits' and the fragile, acoustic 'A Lotus on Irish Streams' are like classically-inspired suites in miniature." The Melody Maker, on original release, wrote: "the effects of this remarkable album will be far reaching." The album's reputation for extreme volume was literal: it had a documented reputation in its day for inducing listeners to blow out their home speakers in the attempt to reproduce its dynamics.

**Tape provenance:** The original Columbia master tapes are held by Sony Music. Both the Speakers Corner (2008) and Friday Music pressings confirmed working from the original analogue master tapes. The Friday Music pressing was mastered by Joe Reagoso at Friday Music Studios (confirmed via multiple retailer listings for this release) – not Kevin Gray, who mastered Friday Music's later reissue of the follow-up album *Birds of Fire* alongside Reagoso, a detail worth not conflating with this title. The Speakers Corner pressing was mastered by Heinz Gäde (HCB-EBS in the deadwax) and pressed at Pallas, Germany.

**The band's dissolution:** The original Mahavishnu Orchestra lasted barely two years. Acrimony over writing credits, financial disputes, and personal tensions – particularly between McLaughlin and Hammer – resulted in dissolution after a final concert on December 30, 1973. The follow-up *Birds of Fire* (1973) reached #15 on the Billboard album charts; the unreleased third studio album sessions appeared in 1999 as *The Lost Trident Sessions*. A second-lineup Mahavishnu (1974–76) with Jean-Luc Ponty on violin replaced the original; a further reformation occurred in 1984.

## Stereo note

*The Inner Mounting Flame* was recorded and released in **stereo only**. There is no mono version. The stereo field is wide and energetic – instruments are placed decisively across the panorama, which rewards careful listening on a system with good imaging. The album's dynamic range is extreme: from Cobham's quietest rolls to McLaughlin's full-stack electric attacks, the gap is genuinely wide. Original Columbia pressings are noted for their "slightly distorted mix" character – an artefact of the recording itself rather than the pressing, and arguably intrinsic to the album's identity.

## Original US Columbia pressings

**Catalogue: KC 31067** (original 1971); also seen as **PC 31067** on later repressings.

Released November 3, 1971. Single LP with gatefold sleeve and purple 9" × 10" Sri Chinmoy insert. Original copies manufactured by CBS Inc.

**Three original pressing plants** produced this title simultaneously:

**Terre Haute, Indiana (TH):** [r557286](https://www.discogs.com/release/557286-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – "IT" etched in runout. Considered by many collectors the finest-sounding original pressing: "Super organic sounding, yet powerful. Drums and keyboards are especially impressive-sounding." Purple insert included. Matrix: PAL 31067 / PBL 31067 suffix variants.

**Pitman, New Jersey:** [r9971709](https://www.discogs.com/release/9971709-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) and [r19608823](https://www.discogs.com/release/19608823-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – The Pitman plant is generally considered on par with Terre Haute for this title. One reviewer: "As AAA as it gets if you're orthodox about it." Purple insert included.

**Santa Maria, California:** [r14551678](https://www.discogs.com/release/14551678-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – West Coast pressing; well reviewed but collector consensus gives a slight edge to Terre Haute and Pitman.

**Condition note:** Original pressings were extremely popular and were often played hard. Finding a genuinely VG+ or NM copy requires patience. The slightly distorted character noted by reviewers on any pressing is inherent to the recording, not a pressing defect. NM originals command a real premium over VG+ copies, with the exact gap varying by pressing plant and condition.

### 1970s US Columbia reissues

Later 1970s pressings carry PC 31067 catalogue numbers and Carrollton, Georgia or other plant identifiers. [r4923574](https://www.discogs.com/release/4923574-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) is a well-documented mid-seventies pressing. Sonically close to the originals if the stampers are early; more variable on later iterations.

## The reissue hierarchy

### Speakers Corner 180g (2008) – the analogue European reference

**Cat. SC 31067** | Format: 180g LP, 33⅓, stereo, gatefold, includes Sri Chinmoy "Aspiration" insert. **Pure analogue mastering by Heinz Gäde (HCB-EBS in the deadwax). Pressed at Pallas, Germany.** Marketed as a "Pure Analogue" reissue.

Discogs community is sharply divided. Positive: "Perfect pressing, nice packaging, and the tunes have never sounded better." "My copy is rich, full and expansive. The pressing is dead flat and nigh silent." "I have an original US press, and the only differences are in the signal level – a bit lower on this reissue, but when you crank it up, it's pretty much on par in clarity and richness." Critical: "I have to say, my OG pressing sounds way better than this Speakers Corner pressing and the photos on the SC cover are blurry, like they just took a photo of the OG cover." And concerning: "Freaking Awakening sounds like the tape is flaking off the reel while it's playing." One reviewer summarises the choice plainly: "Recommended unless you want a lighter and brighter cut in the form of the KPG/FM clear vinyl." The low signal level is consistently noted and requires more volume than usual – not a defect, just a characteristic. Repressings with a QR code sticker on the shrink wrap exist alongside the original 2008 edition; both are sonically identical. [Discogs r1718798](https://www.discogs.com/release/1718798-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-with-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame)

### Friday Music 180g clear vinyl – the brighter modern alternative

**Cat. KC 31067** | Format: 180g clear LP, 33⅓, stereo, gatefold. **Mastered by Joe Reagoso at Friday Music Studios, from the original analogue master tapes. Pressed at RTI.**

The character is brighter and more present than the Speakers Corner – described by one reviewer comparing both as "lighter and brighter... for those with dull systems or well worn hearing will likely want to go that way." This is not a criticism in context: the original recording has an energetic, slightly forward quality and this cut renders this honestly. One Discogs reviewer: "I was going to mention I thought Friday Music version kicked ass." Gatefold jacket. [Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/release/9485353-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame)

### Japanese CBS/Sony pressings – the vintage alternative

The Japanese first-pressing (**SOPL-10**, 1971/72, [r4183685](https://www.discogs.com/release/4183685-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame)) was manufactured by CBS/Sony Japan simultaneously with the US release. One Discogs owner: "On top of that, this Japanese pressing sounds amazing. Jazz Fusion masterpiece right here." Another reviewer notes these early SOPL numbers are arguably first pressings rather than reissues. A later 1976 CBS/Sony Japan pressing ([r2623855](https://www.discogs.com/release/2623855-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame)) is also well regarded and easier to find. Both carry CBS/Sony Japan's characteristic quiet, well-controlled pressings, typically with obi.

### UK/Netherlands CBS pressing – a period-contemporary European option

A 1972 UK and Netherlands pressing (**CBS S 64717**) followed roughly a year after the US and Japanese releases, on a plain orange CBS label. No dedicated sound-quality comparisons against the US original have surfaced, so treat it as a collectible period pressing from the same general era rather than a confirmed audiophile alternative.

## Pressings to avoid

**Generic EU budget reissues (Music on Vinyl, DOL, Waxtime, etc.):** *The Inner Mounting Flame* is a Columbia/Sony title and attracts unlicensed and poorly sourced budget pressings. Avoid anything without a credentialed mastering chain and licensing provenance.

**1980s–90s Columbia reissues:** Various Columbia reissues from this period were cut from digital transfers. Not recommended when an original or the Speakers Corner/Friday Music is available.

**$$ (Affordable):** A [**1970s US Columbia reissue (PC 31067)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/4923574-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) in honest VG+ – the budget entry point for period-analogue sound.

**$$ (Affordable):** The [**Friday Music 180g clear vinyl**](https://www.discogs.com/release/9485353-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – Joe Reagoso mastering, RTI pressing; brighter character than the Speakers Corner.

**$$ (Affordable):** The [**Speakers Corner 180g (2008)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/1718798-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-with-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – Heinz Gäde mastering, Pallas pressing; quieter and warmer character; pure analogue.

**$$$ (Mid-range):** A [**Japanese CBS/Sony SOPL pressing with obi**](https://www.discogs.com/release/4183685-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) – excellent quality, faithful reproduction; early SOPL copies may be simultaneous first pressings.

**$$$ (Mid-range):** An original [**US Columbia KC 31067 (Terre Haute or Pitman)**](https://www.discogs.com/release/9971709-The-Mahavishnu-Orchestra-With-John-McLaughlin-The-Inner-Mounting-Flame) in honest VG+ with purple insert – AAA, period-correct, the definitive version.
