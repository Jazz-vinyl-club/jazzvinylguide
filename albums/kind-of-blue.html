Columbia · 1959

Kind of Blue

Miles Davis

Recorded: March 2 & April 22, 1959   Original cat#: CL 1355 / CS 8163

Kind of Blue was recorded at Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York City across two sessions: March 2, 1959 (Side 1: "So What," "Freddie Freeloader," "Blue in Green") and April 22, 1959 (Side 2: "All Blues," "Flamenco Sketches"). Producer was Irving Townsend; recording engineer and mixer Fred Plaut; post-production editing and tape management by Teo Macero. Personnel: Miles Davis (trumpet), John Coltrane (tenor saxophone), Julian "Cannonball" Adderley (alto saxophone, absent on "Blue in Green"), Bill Evans (piano on all tracks except "Freddie Freeloader"), Wynton Kelly (piano on "Freddie Freeloader"), Paul Chambers (bass), Jimmy Cobb (drums). Davis arrived both days with only modal sketches; the performances were largely first takes.

A tape machine error on March 2 ran the prime three-track deck roughly 2.4% slow (confirmed by spectrum analysis), causing Side 1 to play sharp on every pressing made from the three-track master – that is, all original stereo pressings and any reissue derived from the three-track. The original mono pressings (CL 1355) were made from a separate, simultaneously-running full-track mono machine that was not affected by the speed error; they play at correct pitch. The defect went undiscovered for 33 years until Mark Wilder found it in 1992 – noticing a pitch discrepancy between the surviving mono and stereo tapes; the original mono masters were already lost by then, so all modern "mono" reissues are new dedicated mono remixes from the three-track safety reel – an active mixing process that handles the discrete center channel (carrying Davis's trumpet) separately, not a simple passive fold-down of two channels into one. This single fact divides the reissue landscape into two lineages: the Grundman/Classic/AP chain (cut direct from the three-track session master in 1995, one generation closer to the source) and the Wilder/Sony/MoFi chain (from Wilder's 1997 two-track fold-down). Every serious pressing decision for stereo reissues follows from understanding which lineage you are buying into.

Best early pressing for audiophiles: Columbia CS 8163 six-eye stereo – black "Stereo Fidelity" label; earliest 1E/1K matrices; speed-affected (sharp) but sonically magnificent tube cut.

Best modern pressing for audiophiles: Analogue Productions UHQR 45rpm 2LP (AUHQR 0004-45, 2022) – Grundman metal parts, 200g Clarity Vinyl, QRP; consensus best.

Best-value modern: Analogue Productions AAPJ 8163BLU 2LP (2025) – same Grundman lineage, speed-corrected, ~$50–$60 new.

    Recording history and tape provenance

    Columbia recorded both sessions on at least two machines simultaneously: a prime full-track mono deck and a prime half-inch three-track deck. The documented existence of a surviving three-track safety reel (which became the basis for all modern work) confirms that safety copies of the three-track existed; whether these were recorded in parallel during the sessions or dubbed from the prime afterward is not definitively established in available primary sources.

    On March 2 only, the prime three-track capstan ran slow. The full-track mono machine was unaffected. The original 1959 stereo mix was made from the three-track prime, so Side 1 plays sharp on all original stereo pressings and any reissue derived from the three-track. The original 1959 mono mix (CL 1355) was made from the full-track mono prime and plays at correct pitch. Side 2 of all formats is unaffected; the three-track machine had been corrected before April 22.

    Mark Wilder discovered the discrepancy in 1992 while pulling tapes for a reissue – detecting a pitch difference between the surviving mono tape and the three-track stereo master. Both original full-track mono tapes were already lost – the most likely explanation is that the tapes were degaussed and reused, though no documentation survives. For his 1997 Sony SBM remaster, Wilder created a new analog three-to-two mixdown using a tube Presto deck, GML line mixer, and Ampex ATR-102 at 15 ips with Dolby SR – speed-corrected from the safety reel. That 1997 two-track is the source for virtually every subsequent Sony/Legacy digital release and MoFi's vinyl box.

    For the 2013 Sony mono reissue, Wilder and Steve Berkowitz decided to make one final three-track transfer: a new dedicated mono remix direct from the safety reel to analog tape, replicating the original 1959 mono leveling curves using all three discrete tracks, alongside DSD and high-resolution PCM captures. The 2013 mono pressing was cut from this newly made mono remix – not from the 1997 stereo two-track.

    Bernie Grundman cut directly from the half-inch three-track session master in 1995, folding the center channel to L/R and applying fades live during cutting – the same method used to produce the original 1959 stereo mixes, and one tape generation closer to the source than Wilder's 1997 two-track. Critically, this was only possible because Grundman worked on the same custom speed-corrected playback hardware that Wilder and Sony's engineering team had spent years configuring; the tape research and deck modifications Wilder established were the prerequisite for Grundman's session. Classic Records used these metal parts for all their KoB releases; Analogue Productions acquired them when Classic closed and has used them for all UHQR and 2025 editions.

    Collectors identify speed-corrected pressings by running times: uncorrected "So What" runs ~9:07, corrected ~9:20. Spectrum analysis confirms the speed error at 2.4%.

    The cover photograph is by Jay Maisel – a tight close-up of Davis's face lit against darkness, widely considered one of the great jazz album covers. Cover misspellings: "Adderly" for Adderley appears on all pressings from 1959 into the 1970s. The Side 2 track-listing error (All Blues listed before Flamenco Sketches) appears on the earliest six-eye labels and persists on the back cover into at least 1977 (confirmed on the PC 8163 red label).

    Original US Columbia pressings (1959)

    Released in both mono and stereo:

    • Mono: Columbia CL 1355
    • Stereo: Columbia CS 8163

    Six-eye label identification

    The original pressings use Columbia's six-eye label – named for the six CBS "eye" logos arranged around the perimeter. Mono labels are red with "GUARANTEED HIGH FIDELITY" arc; stereo labels are black with "STEREO FIDELITY" banner at top.

    Mono (CL 1355): - Red six-eye label, "GUARANTEED HIGH FIDELITY" - Deep groove pressing ring around the label - Matrix format: XLP 47324–1[alpha] (Side 1) / XLP 47325–1[alpha] (Side 2) - First pressing matrices: 1A, 1B, 1C, and some 1D copies; all earliest copies carry the Side 2 label error (All Blues listed first, Flamenco Sketches second) - LJC's survey of ~200 matrix pairs finds 1D the most common mono matrix; white-label promos used lacquers D, G, H (Terre Haute plant, confirmed by "T/TA" stamps) - Plays at correct pitch – derived from the full-track mono machine, unaffected by the three-track speed error

    Stereo (CS 8163): - Black six-eye label, "STEREO FIDELITY" at top - Matrix format: XSM 47326–1[alpha] (Side 1) / XSM 47327–1[alpha] (Side 2) - True single-alpha first matrices (1A, 1B, 1C) are disputed – LJC's comprehensive research found no verified 1A/1A or 1B/1B stereo copy in a sample of ~200. The earliest reliably documented commercial stereo copies are 1E/1K and 1H; white-label stereo promos used lacquers E, K, AC, and AE - Some early stereo copies also carry the Side 2 label error; the 1AA and 1AC matrices are documented with the error - The stereo was pressed in significantly smaller quantities than mono; over two-thirds of six-eye copies at auction are mono, reflecting period purchasing habits - Side 1 plays sharp – derived from the three-track prime, affected by the 2.4% speed error

    The "Adderly" misprint appears on all copies until well into the 1970s – it is not a dating indicator and all sellers who cite it as rare are mistaken.

    The Side 2 track-listing error on the label (not just the back cover) is the primary indicator of earliest pressings. The back cover error persisted independently to at least 1977.

    Market values

    NM stereo CS 8163 six-eye, early matrix: $400–$700+. White-label stereo promos: $1,000–$2,000. NM mono CL 1355 six-eye with Side 2 label error: $250–$600. White-label mono promos: $800–$1,500. Clean six-eye copies in honest VG+ are significantly more affordable; ultrasonic cleaning is standard practice for anything in this price range.

    The mono vs. stereo question

    This is Kind of Blue's most genuine collector controversy. The stereo mix places instruments across the field (Davis and Chambers near centre; Coltrane right; Adderley and Evans left depending on track) with the echo chamber send/return affecting only the centre channel. Mono has demonstrably stronger bass below 600 Hz – up to 5dB more per spectrum analysis by magicvinyldigital.net – which drives much of the format preference. The stereo's three-dimensional depth and 30th Street Studio ambience cannot be replicated in mono.

    Fremer (AnalogPlanet): the mono reissue makes "a strong case for the superiority of the mono mix" and is "a necessity not an 'add-on' option." The practical position taken by most experienced collectors: own both.

    Later US Columbia label variants

    Early two-eye "360 Sound" stereo (1962–65, black-text label): Still tube-cut by Columbia's core engineering teams. Many experienced collectors – including Tom Port of Better Records – consider clean early two-eyes essentially equivalent to six-eyes sonically; price differences reflect collectibility more than sound. Black-text two-eye NM: $100–$250.

    Late two-eye "360 Sound" stereo (1966–70, white-text label): Columbia moved to solid-state cutting systems and revised mastering chains from the mid-1960s onward. The sonic step-down relative to the early two-eyes is real and documented – these pressings reflect a fundamentally different engineering environment than the tube-era originals. Still listenable and period-authentic; simply not in the same class as the black-text pressings. White-text two-eye NM: $50–$150.

    KCS 8163 / PC 8163 red "Trident" label (1974 onward): A sleeper. One Discogs reviewer with the full range of pressings calls the 70s red label "sweeter and more alive sounding than the Classic remix by BG" – a minority view, but consistent enough to note. Some Santa Maria plant copies with Customatrix metalwork have been specifically praised. $15–$40.

    The two tape lineages and their reissues

    Lineage A – Grundman / Classic / Analogue Productions

    Bernie Grundman's 1995 direct cut from the three-track session master is the foundation. Classic Records used these metal parts for everything they released; Analogue Productions acquired them when Classic closed and has used them for every subsequent UHQR and the 2025 2LP.

    Classic Records CS 8163 180g 33⅓rpm (1995)

    Discogs r3841623 | Format: 2×LP, 33⅓, 180g. Structure: Sides A/B at original (uncorrected) speed; Side C corrected speed; Side D = alternate take of "Flamenco Sketches" at 45rpm (previously unreleased). Same Grundman cut. The first vinyl speed-corrected release of Side 1. $150–$350 used.

    Classic Records CS 8163-45 4×45rpm single-sided box (1999)

    Discogs r5189885 | Format: 4×LP, 45rpm, single-sided, Clarity Vinyl, stereo, uncorrected speed. The same Grundman 1995 cut. Fremer (AnalogPlanet): "The Classic single sided 45rpm version of 'Freddie Freeloader' was definitely more open, spacious and transparent" than the MoFi. Fred Kaplan (Stereophile): considered it "very close in quality to Columbia's Six-Eye first pressing." Out of print; $500–$1,500 on the secondary market.

    Analogue Productions UHQR 33⅓rpm (UHQR 0004, 2021)

    Discogs r18884065 | Format: single 200g Clarity Vinyl, 33⅓, stereo, speed-corrected, numbered to 25,000, hand-pressed on Finebilt press at QRP. Grundman metal parts. Michael Fremer (AnalogPlanet): "Quite clearly and obviously this new UHQR Kind of Blue sourced from Bernie Grundman's 'live' mix from the 3-track master, directly to lacquer sounds best compared to two early '6-Eyes,' Classic's own double 180g set and that label's single-sided 45rpm set, and Mobile Fidelity's double 45 boxed edition." Fred Kaplan (Stereophile, August 2021): the UHQR 33 is "the best-sounding Kind of Blue ever, superior in every way to all previous pressings, including the original… Cobb's drumkit is spooky real." Some copies reported with Clarity Vinyl surface noise that responds to ultrasonic cleaning. ~$100 MSRP; $150–$250 secondary.

    Analogue Productions UHQR 45rpm 2LP (AUHQR 0004-45, 2022)

    Discogs r24373778 | Format: 2×200g Clarity Vinyl, 45rpm, stereo, speed-corrected, numbered to 25,000, QRP. Grundman metal parts. The current consensus best pressing of Kind of Blue in any format.

    Fred Kaplan (TrackingAngle.com): 11/11 music and 11/11 sound – "Everything is more present, more brassy, woody, or metallic, depending on the instrument. There's a more palpable sense of a human being blowing through a mouthpiece, plucking a string, coaxing a keyboard, or tapping a snare drum." Mike Hobson (Classic Records founder): "The Analogue Productions UHQR pressing from the same metal parts has taken it up a notch and gotten us even closer to the sound on those 1/2-inch analog session master tapes." ~$150 MSRP; $200–$350 secondary.

    Analogue Productions AAPJ 8163BLU 2LP (2025)

    Discogs r35666779 (black vinyl) | r35596555 (blue vinyl, 1,500 copies) | Format: 2×180g, 33⅓ (except Side 4), gatefold tip-on by Stoughton, QRP.

    LP structure: LP1 Side 1 = speed-corrected ("So What," "Freddie Freeloader," "Blue in Green"); LP1 Side 2 = "All Blues," "Flamenco Sketches" at their native correct speed (April 22 session – never affected by the March 2 tape fault); LP2 Side 3 = original uncorrected speed ("So What," "Freddie Freeloader," "Blue in Green"); LP2 Side 4 = "Flamenco Sketches (Alternate Take)" at 45rpm. Same Grundman/Classic metal parts.

    Mark Smotroff (AnalogPlanet): 11/11 music, 10/11 sound – "a fine release, and I'm quite happy to add it into my collection. It is certainly a marked improvement over the 2010 Columbia/Legacy blue-vinyl edition." The right play copy for regular enjoyment relative to the more precious UHQR. ~$50–$60 new.

    Lineage B – Wilder / Sony / Mobile Fidelity

    Mark Wilder's 1997 two-track fold-down is the source for MoFi's vinyl box and Sony/Legacy digital releases. It is one generation further from the source than the Grundman cut but represents a thoughtful, well-regarded transfer.

    Sony/Legacy 180g mono (RSD Black Friday 2013, cat. 88883761031)

    Discogs r5164493 (numbered RSD edition, 6,000 copies) | r5142904 (unnumbered standard edition) | Format: single 180g, 33⅓, mono, speed-corrected, RTI pressing. Mastered by Ryan K. Smith at Sterling Sound (matrix: "STERLING RKS") from a newly made 2013 mono fold-down of the safety three-track – not from the 1997 stereo two-track.

    Since both original full-track mono tapes are lost, this is the definitive modern mono pressing. Fremer (AnalogPlanet): the mono reissue is "a necessity not an 'add-on' option." Music on Vinyl also released an EU-pressing mono the same day as MOVLP961 (r6956871, EU, numbered; r5166551 also documented). $30–$80 used.

    Mobile Fidelity MFSL 2-45011 45rpm 2LP (2015, repressed 2020, 2021)

    Discogs r7303082 (2015 first run) | r16433790 (2020 hand-numbered repress) | r19794313 (2021 gold-stamped repress) | Format: 2×180g, 45rpm, stereo, speed-corrected, numbered box, RTI pressing. Mastered by Krieg Wunderlich ("KW @ MoFi" in deadwax). Source chain: Wilder's 1997 two-track → DSD-64 digital intermediate → analog console → lathe. The digital step was not disclosed until the 2022 "MoFiGate" revelations.

    Pre-2022 it drew strong praise. Post-2022, comparisons against the UHQR 45rpm have placed it lower. Kaplan (pre-2022, Stereophile): "the bass is too strong, the highs seem boosted." Fremer: finds the Classic/Grundman cut "more open, spacious and transparent" than the MoFi. Still a musical pressing – better than most – but no longer the reference it once seemed. $150–$300 used.

    Music on Vinyl MOVLP019 2LP stereo (2010)

    Discogs r2506823 (black vinyl) | r2317524 (blue vinyl, 3,000 numbered copies) | Format: 2×180g, 33⅓, stereo, speed-corrected, gatefold, Optimal pressing. Includes "Flamenco Sketches (alt.)" and "On Green Dolphin Street." Source: 24/96 PCM from Wilder's master – digitally sourced; mastered by George Marino at Sterling Sound. Dead-silent surfaces. $30–$60.

    Japanese pressings

    CBS/Sony 18AP 2056 (1981)

    Stereo, uncorrected speed (predates 1992 discovery); heavy dead-quiet Japanese vinyl, SJ Seal of Approval, obi strip and insert. Consistently praised on Discogs for clear, well-defined sound and exceptional pressing quality. NM with obi: $50–$150.

    CBS/Sony SOPL 155 SX68 Mark II (1972)

    Earlier Japanese audiophile pressing in the SX68 series; matrix SOPL-155A1. Heavy and quiet; generally well-regarded. NM with obi: $40–$120.

    Pressings to avoid

    EU public-domain pressings (DOL, Doxy, WaxTime, Not Now Music, Jazz Wax, Vinyl Passion, Jazz Images, Green Corner, 20th Century Masterworks): Not licensed from Sony; sourced from CD or worse. "Very silent vinyl but sounds like the master was a not very high quality MP3" is a consistent Discogs description. The coloured 180g variants at $15–$20 are tempting; they sound like what they are.

    Sony/Legacy 50th Anniversary blue LP (2008, in box set only): Lacquers by Greg Calbi and Ray Janos; pressing (Noiseland) widely criticised for noisy non-fill. Fremer: "poorly pressed on 180g with a lot of noisy non-fill." Not sold separately.

    1980s Columbia Jazz Masterpieces series: Digital remix with added reverb to an early PCM tape; notably rebalanced mix.

    Buyer's guide by budget

    Under $60: The AP AAPJ 8163BLU 2LP (2025) at ~$50–$60 new – Grundman lineage, speed-corrected, tip-on gatefold, QRP. The entry point into the authoritative source chain. Add the Sony 88883761031 mono at $30–$80 for the other half of the picture.

    $150–$250: The AP UHQR 33rpm for a step up in pressing luxury from the same source. Ultrasonically clean before first play; Clarity Vinyl surface noise often clears after cleaning.

    $200–$350: The AP UHQR 45rpm is the definitive modern pressing. Worth every penny if the music matters to you and you have the system to appreciate it.

    $250–$600: A clean Columbia CL 1355 six-eye mono – plays at correct pitch; the bass-forward, intimate presentation many experienced collectors consider the definitive listening copy regardless of pressing generation.

    $400–$700: A clean Columbia CS 8163 six-eye stereo original for the tube-cut, period character no reissue fully recovers – note Side 1 plays sharp at the original (uncorrected) speed.

    Note on Mobile Fidelity: still a musical pressing worth having if acquired cheaply (roughly $80–120 used), but not worth premium prices now that the AP 2025 undercuts it on both price and provenance.
